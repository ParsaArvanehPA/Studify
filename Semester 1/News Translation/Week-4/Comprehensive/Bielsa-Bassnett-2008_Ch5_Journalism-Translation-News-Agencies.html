üåç 2. News Agencies' Regional Centers

Organizational Structure

Efficiency through Coordination:
  • Local offices write news reports
  • Regional/global centers filter, translate, edit
  • Progressive trend toward decentralization
  • Regional centers operate with relative autonomy

Global Organizational Structure

Agency HQ Regions
Reuters London 3 regions: Europe/ME/Africa (Switzerland), Americas (NY), Asia/Pacific (Singapore)
AFP Paris 5 regions: N. America (Washington), Latin America (Montevideo), Asia/Pacific (Hong Kong), Europe/Africa (Paris), Middle East (Nicosia)
IPS Rome 5 regions: Africa (Johannesburg), Asia/Pacific (Bangkok), Europe/Med (Berlin), Latin America (Montevideo), N. America/Caribbean

Montevideo - Continental Capital of Agency Journalism

Why Montevideo?
  • High educational level of population
  • Low salaries
  • IPS moved from Costa Rica to Montevideo (1994)
  • Montevideo = IPS world center for translation
  • AFP established Latin American center (1997)

IPS History & Character

Emerged mid-1960s:
  • Exchange agency between Latin America & Europe
  • Context: questioning economic/political/communications systems, seeking new world information order
  • Spanish = main language for many years
  • Principal client base in Latin American media
  • Gradually shifted to international NGOs and civil society
  • Since 1970s (UN summit coverage), English gained importance

IPS Montevideo Desk

  • Staff: 7 (regional editor, 2 translators-editors Spanish‚ÜíEnglish, 2 translators-editors English‚ÜíSpanish, 4 Spanish editors)
  • Output: ~30 analytical features daily (~1,200 words), 40% in Spanish
  • Translations: 5-6 English features from Spanish, 4-5 Spanish from English
  • Also: Selection translated locally into 14 other languages
  • New product: Breves (shorter notes, 2,000-3,000 bytes)

AFP Montevideo Desk

  • Created: 1997 regional center (previously in Paris)
  • Responsibility: Coordinates 21 Latin American offices + processes Americas info in Spanish
  • Staff: Chief regional editor, desk chief, 15 Spanish editors, 2 French editors, 6 sports editors, photographic service
  • Material: 70-80% in Spanish, translations ~30%
  • Spanish service: AFP's 3rd language globally (after French, English) - 230,000 words daily (global output = 600,000)
  • Hours: 7 a.m. - 3 a.m. (unstaffed 4 hours, Paris takes over)
  • Daily production: ~500 texts (1-line bulletins to 600-word updates)

‚öñÔ∏è 3. AFP vs IPS - Dominant vs Alternative Model

Dominant Model (AFP, Reuters)

Characteristics:
  • Translation fully incorporated into news production
  • Translators = journalists (NO professional translators employed)
  • Entry test = news translation test
  • Job title = "news editor" (not translator)
  • All have journalistic training/background
  • All translations edited by second person
  • Fast pace: 30-40 texts daily, translations rarely take >30 min
AFP Spanish desk chief: "I don't want to use the word translator, because none of the journalists working for us are translators: they are editors. By necessity they must rework‚Ä¶ The one I prefer is to edit. There are no translators working here. We select journalists and not translators, journalists who must at least know two languages in addition to Spanish and journalists who have previous experience, if possible in various media, agencies, radio, newspapers."

Alternative Model (IPS)

Characteristics:
  • Employs both journalists AND professional translators
  • Hybrid figure: "translator-editor"
  • Translators and editors work side by side, undertake similar tasks
  • Longer, analytical features (~1,200 words)
  • More time: translation can take up to 2 hours
  • Daily quota: 5 texts (vs AFP's 30-40)
  • More autonomous: translators/editors choose texts to translate
  • Individual responsibility (except new editors, whose texts are checked)
  • Translations NOT signed but marked with initials
IPS translator-editor: "I am maybe both. I studied to be a translator and when I started to work here it was as a translator. But this job involves as much journalism as translation. I learned with time, absorbing those themes. And currently I think that indeed I am partly a journalist. I have even started collaborating with a publication, Tierram√©rica, in which I write articles. I have also been sent to cover conferences on sustainable development‚Ä¶ If you are not partly a journalist, you cannot really do this job."

Alternative model empowers translators by recognizing them as such (not just as journalists), allowing more interventionist role in editorial matters. Coordinator of translations participates in editorial meetings.

IPS Language Politics

Objective: "Balanced representation of ethnic diversity and geographical distribution"

Linguistic policy:
  • Promotes equality between languages
  • Encourages communication between minority languages (European + non-European)
  • Translation into highest number of languages possible for local media presence
  • Global services limited to 2 languages (English + Spanish), but excellent local selections in minority languages
  • First international news service in Swahili (1980s)
  • Only agency producing in Nepali, Thai
  • Aim: Reach local publics, especially Third World
IPS editor-in-chief Miren Guti√©rrez: "IPS has traditionally been an English-Spanish service. But our main aim is to have local impact. I'll give you an example of how important language is. In Sri Lanka... translators at first refused to translate material not perceived as originating from 'their' side, once this was overcome there was explicit recognition of the value of a common platform of communication. We are trying to launch a service in Arabic and Hebrew with the same approach."

üîß 4. Translation Values & Strategies

Source Text as Raw Material

Key principle: Source text = basis for elaboration of NEW text designed for specific audiences

NOT finished product - view it as material requiring transformation for maximum efficiency
Reuters foreign desk head (Europe): "Any journalist worth his or her salt would never simply reproduce a press statement. The basic facts are there. But the order often has to be rearranged. This is especially true of press statements where the real (especially if it's bad) news is often tucked away at the bottom. Relevant background needs to be added to a press statement and the same might be the case when rendering an English story more relevant or comprehensible to the local target audience."

AFP French Editors - Special Case

  • Title: "Regional journalist for Latin America"
  • Produce French syntheses of Latin American events for French public
  • Add relevant background for European public
  • Adapt style/journalistic norms to different context
  • Often use >1 source text for single report
  • Work autonomously - select texts themselves
  • Context: Low French media interest in Latin America (few correspondents)

Three Main Translation Tasks

Task Description
1. Selection & Synthesis Only relevant information rendered in target language. May combine 2-3 notes on same subject into one.
2. Prioritization Adapt original text to new audience needs. Highlight references to Latin American countries, move to top even if originally at end.
3. Changing Angles/Nuances Adjust news angle/POV when new informative context justifies it.
IPS translator-editor: "We decide which notes to translate into English and we translate them for the English-speaking market, thinking of this market, of this audience. We add context, re-edit, reorganize the note, we give it a new title. We do a lot of work with the note; it rarely is a direct translation, just as it comes‚Ä¶ We even combine notes if there are two or three about a subject when we do not need three notes in English on that subject."

Key Translation Values

  • Fluency: Translations must read as if originally written in target language (not as translations)
  • Comprehensibility: Easy to read and understand, simple vocabulary
  • Context: Note contains ALL necessary information - reader doesn't need other sources
  • Precision: Especially for quotes, numbers, dates, quantities
  • Faithfulness: Respect reporter's analysis and personal vision (despite wide scope for alteration)
  • Conciseness: No unnecessary words

Editing Process

Two-level quality assessment (AFP - all translations edited):

Level 1 (without source text):
  • Journalistic style: concise headline/lead, inverted pyramid
  • Coherent, clear, concise, logical narration
  • Not too literal, natural language for whole region
  • Check false friends, incorrect expressions

Level 2 (comparing to original):
  • Verify numbers, dates, quantities
  • Check quotes translated faithfully/accurately
  • Desk chief also checks against similar texts for homogenization

Selection Criteria for Translation

AFP (desk chief selects):
  • Editorial priorities/previsions from daily meeting
  • Urgent information
  • Geographical location (proximity to Latin America)
  • Relevance for region
  • Client needs (not too local re: Europe/North American events)
  • Certain themes always translated (e.g., Washington institutional info)

IPS (translators/editors select):
  • Editorial policy based on inclusion
  • Thematic priorities from weekly editorial meeting
  • Explicit policy: translate more about Africa (insufficient presence)
  • Quality of dispatch (own sources, appropriate analysis)
  • Temporal factor (recent events)
  • Timeliness

‚ö° 5. Speed & Accuracy - The Central Tension

Historical Importance of Speed

Famous scoops:
  • Reuters: 70-minute break announcing Gandhi shooting (1948)
  • AFP: Death of Israeli hostages (Munich Olympics 1972) - 56 minutes ahead. Reporter got info from city mayor who refuted official version

Old practices:
  • Getting to phone/telegraph first
  • Making access difficult for others
  • UPI journalist delayed AP colleague's Kennedy assassination transmission by holding phone
  • Bribing telegraph officials
  • Pretending working phones were broken

Contemporary Pressure

Today:
  • Near-instantaneous coverage possible
  • 24/7 continuous information channels
  • News agencies no longer exclusive source of breaking news
  • Newspapers' closing time ceased to be reference
  • Must satisfy varied client needs at any time of day
  • Increasing acceleration of information circulation
AFP desk chief: "People at the agency work at a very accelerated pace‚Ä¶ A journalist here processes between twenty and forty texts daily to which I must pay attention, of which four or five are translations. Speed leads you to produce errors, to a more literal translation, to changing meanings and contexts. It is very limiting. And it is also true that everything is accelerating even more. Some years ago a newspaper could wait for an analysis... Today, a newspaper in Quito wants to receive immediately an analysis of the oil problem that an English newspaper is publishing today or is going to publish tomorrow... There are complex translations, but it is difficult to have more than an hour to do them. To translate 600 words in an hour and guarantee that the translation is good is very complicated."

Speed = Part of Quality

Speed NOT seen as undermining accuracy - it's intrinsic to journalistic translation quality!

AFP editor: "To translate means to translate fast, well and accurately"

IPS senior member: "Speed is as important as quality, it is part of quality. Maybe [the text] comes out with mistakes, it might not be perfect, but it has to come out fast."

Variation by Urgency

  • Urgent information: Less time, less space for modification, tends to be word-for-word
  • Non-urgent: More time for rewriting, adaptation

IPS Difference

IPS translator-editor: "IPS has increasingly moved into the timeless note, which is our niche: a note with context, with an angle, with a different focus from mainstream media, something good, with a different focus, the necessary context and sources, sources from civil society, from the people who do not have a voice."

IPS regional editor: "There is a great difference between the fast translation of a dispatch of four or five paragraphs and literary translation or the translation of an essay or of scientific works. IPS is not dedicated to literature or to scientific work; we produce news that has to be published in newspapers and on the internet and that is going to be read today or tomorrow and no more. Then, beyond all the values that an article might have, our work needs to have essentially a journalistic rhythm."

üëÅÔ∏è 6. Translator Invisibility - The Paradox

Arguments FOR Invisibility

  • Translations unsigned (like editions)
  • Internal codification system (initials) for responsibility - public doesn't see
  • Invisibility guarantees good quality - respects original producer's work/vision
  • Translation more invisible than editing (people understand editing, not translation)
  • Notes read as if written by native speaker

Arguments AGAINST Invisibility

Translators DO intervene:
  • Transform reality from different context for public whose characteristics they know
  • Journalistic sense of translator prevails
  • Add nuances, even important ones
  • Intervene in orientation, hierarchization, prioritization
  • "Fifty-fifty" visibility - information oriented to make it attractive to regional public

The Transformative Power

Translator's visibility/transformative role directly related to power to CHANGE news angle/POV to produce text functioning more effectively as news for different public.

Pushes notion of equivalence to its limit - reveals how different versions of global news can function in diverse local contexts.

Central Paradox

Paradox in news agency production:
  • Need to harmonize criteria across different newswires (consistent editorial politics)
  • VS
  • Need to adapt to cultural, geographic, linguistic differences (regional needs)

Result: Emphasis/news angles determined by local circumstances. Texts tailored to specific context to function successfully as news.

This paradox = at the very core of global organizations maintaining local presence

Example: Ronald Reagan's Funeral Coverage

Problem observed during fieldwork:
  • English notes from Washington celebrated/praised Reagan as respected head of state
  • Had to be rendered in Spanish for Latin America
  • In region: Reagan associated with aggressive US foreign policy, dirty wars in Central America = sinister connotations
  • Adding background on Reagan's economic policies + participation in Nicaragua/El Salvador/Guatemala wars = more significant to Latin American readers than "ex-president's last look of love to his wife"

üåê 7. English Hegemony in News Agencies

AFP:
  • English not predominant but increasingly gaining importance
  • Related to expansion in North American/Asian markets (1980s onwards)
  • Anglophone editor-in-chief appointed (1999)
  • Growing importance affects organizational matters + news models
  • Harmonization of criteria favors Anglophone model: shorter, more concise, direct texts vs French/Spanish models (rhetoric, literary style more important)

IPS:
  • English key for reaching readers via internet
  • Spanish media still predominate among subscribers
  • In theory equal importance, but in practice "global service" = English; Spanish = "regional, Latin American service"
  • English gradually replacing Spanish as internal working language
  • Global meetings now in English (used to be necessary to learn Spanish)
  • Prize for best published feature: only English articles/translations can participate
