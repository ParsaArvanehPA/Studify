1. Introduction: The Changing Criteria of "Good" Translation

Core Premise

Central Question: What makes a translation "good"?

Key Insight: The concept of "good" translation changes over time

Example: Haji Baba of Isfahan translation

  • When fluency was the main criterion â†’ considered good translation
  • By modern standards emphasizing cultural identity representation â†’ may no longer be considered good
  • May be seen as tool serving power relations of its time

Quality Assessment vs. Translation Criticism

Historical Context: These two have always been discussed together

Recent Developments: Can now be distinguished from each other

Article Goal: Separate these concepts and propose framework for translation criticism based on new linguistic and cultural studies approaches

2. Quality Assessment (Ú©ÛŒÙÛŒØªâ€ŒØ³Ù†Ø¬ÛŒ)

Definition

Quality Assessment: Examining translation quality using criteria such as correct/incorrect, good/bad, beauty, and fluency

Framework: Relationship between target and source text defined through concept of "equivalence"

Examples of Quality Assessment Issues

Example 1:

  • Source: "Films and television have now become the main tools of representation"
  • Incorrect Translation A: Focuses on television as main tool
  • Correct Translation B: Film and television have become main tools of representation
  • Issue: Translation A distorts the meaning

Example 2:

  • Source: "Within the cultures of the developing countries, there is diversity, multiple schools of thought and numerous debates"
  • Incorrect Translation A: Refers to "developed countries" instead of "developing"
  • Correct Translation B: Accurately refers to developing countries
  • Issue: Translation A completely changes the reference

Two Key Concepts in Quality Assessment

1. The Concept of "Good" Translation

  • Changes over time
  • What was considered good decades ago may not meet today's standards
  • Example: Haji Baba translation - good when fluency was key, questionable now with emphasis on cultural representation

2. The Concept of "Fidelity"

Key Question: To what or whom should translation be faithful?

  • If faithful to source text/author: Becomes literal, loses fluency
  • If faithful to target reader: Becomes fluent, distances from source
  • Problem: Is there a middle ground for fidelity?

Where Quality Assessment is Used

  • Translator training programs
  • Administrative environments
  • Publishing industry

5. Facet 1: Intertextuality (Ø¨ÛŒÙ†Ø§Ù…ØªÙ†ÛŒØª)

Origin and Definition

Theorist: Julia Kristeva (French)

Original Context: Study of literary genres

Discovery: All texts belonging to one genre share common linguistic and structural components

Example: All Persian novels share narrative structure, characterization, and other elements â†’ allows us to categorize them as novels

Core Concept of Intertextuality

Kristeva's Insight: Texts relate to each other, reference each other, quote from each other (directly or indirectly)

Examples:

  • Novel based on story from classical literature
  • Poetry beginning with Hafez verse

Result: Repetition of concepts and formal, semantic, thematic, structural elements

Definition: This relationship = intertextuality

Meaning: Every text is a kind of intertext - has relationship/dialogue with texts before and after it

Kristeva's Formulation

Key Quote (Allen, 2000:3): "Every text is constructed from quotations placed side by side, and every text contains/is transformation of another text"

Implication: Each text is a link in an intertextual chain - incorporates elements from previous texts

Limitation: No text is limited to direct/indirect references and quotations

Two Components of Every Text:

  1. Referential/Repetitive Part: Elements quoted/referenced from other texts
  2. Non-referential Part: New/non-repetitive elements created by author

Application to Translation

Key Shift: Replace "equivalence" relationship with "intertextuality"

Why?

  • Target text is NOT copy of source
  • Cannot be equivalent to source
  • Written by different person for different readers in different language
  • Read and interpreted in different society

Relationship Redefined:

  • Target text quotes concepts, themes, forms, meanings from source
  • Thus repeats parts of it
  • BUT also has characteristics that are NOT repetitive â†’ cause difference from source

Sources of Non-Repetitive Parts:

  • Linguistic, discursive, cultural, social differences between two languages/societies
  • Translator's viewpoint and working method

Example: Omar Khayyam's Rubaiyat

Source Text: Persian Rubaiyat

  • Genre: Poetry
  • Discourse: Philosophical
  • Has intertextual relationship with all previous Persian poetry and philosophical discourse

FitzGerald's English Translation:

  • NOT identical to Persian original (cannot be)
  • BUT shares commonalities with it
  • Repetitive part: Repeats terms, concepts, themes, meanings, even form (quatrain) from Khayyam
  • ALSO has intertextual relationship with English poetry regarding genre and discourse
  • Non-repetitive part: FitzGerald's own creation

Secondary Translations (Early 20th century):

  • Translated into German, Russian, French, Arabic, Armenian, Spanish from FitzGerald's English
  • Share commonalities with FitzGerald's translation
  • Through it, share commonalities with Khayyam's original
  • BUT all differ from each other and from FitzGerald due to multiple translation processes

Conclusion: Relationship between Khayyam's Rubaiyat and, for example, Swahili translation is intertextual - doesn't fit into "equivalence" or "balance" framework

6. Facet 2: Critical Discourse Analysis (ØªØ­Ù„ÛŒÙ„ Ú¯ÙØªÙ…Ø§Ù† Ø§Ù†ØªÙ‚Ø§Ø¯ÛŒ / CDA)

Nature of CDA

Field: Interdisciplinary branch connecting linguistics and critical thinking

Focus: Relationship between text and society

Concerns: Issues like inequality and discrimination in society as manifested in language through text

Core Assumptions of CDA

Fundamental Premise (Fairclough, 1995): Language, power, and ideology are interconnected

  • Language is vehicle of ideology
  • Text is arena of ideology's appearance and presence
  • Everything in text (words, meanings, structures) indicates positioning
  • This positioning causes some social groups to be centered in power
  • Other groups are marginalized

Ideology in Translation Studies

Note on Term: Has multiple meanings, some more political than others

In Political Sense: Has negative connotation, associated with distortion, domination, political manipulation

In Translation Studies: Used in broader sense, NOT limited to political meanings

Prez Definition: Set of group beliefs

Calzada Perez (2003:5): Ideology relates to "ways of thinking, evaluating, and behaving that are promoted in society, become dominant, and turn into custom"

What is Analyzed

  • Translator's or publisher's notes
  • Preface (Ù…Ù‚Ø¯Ù…Ù‡)
  • Afterword (Ù…ÙˆØ®Ø±Ù‡)
  • Footnotes (Ù¾Ø§Ù†ÙˆÛŒØ³)

Why Important: Often indicate ideological positioning

Example 1: Footnotes in Colonial Era

Context: Western translations of Indian literature during colonial period

Translator Action: Added numerous footnotes to fill information gaps

Effect (per Appiah, Indian translation scholar):

  • Made Indian society seem exotic, mysterious, discoverable
  • Paved the way for colonialism

Example 2: FitzGerald's Preface to Rubaiyat

Context: Translator who didn't know Persian properly

Claims in Preface:

  • Gave himself complete freedom in translation
  • As Allen said: For one Khayyam quatrain, wrote 42 quatrains
  • Then states: "Persian poetry needs little artistic refinement"

Analysis:

  • Judgment has more ideological than literary importance
  • Shows Western translator influenced by colonial-era Orientalist perspective
  • Didn't consider richness of Persian poetry
  • Didn't recognize poetry for Iranians was NOT just literary genre but discourse - form for expressing life, history, even philosophy
  • Rooted in Orientalism (per Edward Said) always aimed to prove Western superiority

Example 3: Coleman Barks' Rumi Translations

Notable Fact: Barks doesn't know Persian, yet claims to translate Rumi

First Volume (1995) Afterword:

  • Doesn't mention he doesn't know Persian
  • Hints he did translations "to help a friend"
  • Used help from Nicholson and others
  • To better understand works: spent time with Indian Sufi
  • Careful NOT to say he was his student or received training
  • Then states: "Since Indian Sufi believed one should mature in mysticism, I now give Hindu cooking recipe"

Analysis:

  • Placing cooking recipe alongside Rumi translation by someone who doesn't know Persian
  • Indicates devaluing of these works
  • Rooted in same Orientalist Western perspective
