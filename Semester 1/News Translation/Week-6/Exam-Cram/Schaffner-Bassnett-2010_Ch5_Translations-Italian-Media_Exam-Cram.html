üìå Essential Definitions

Term Definition
Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) Analytical approach examining how language constructs/maintains social relations, ideologies, power structures; discourse never neutral
Legitimized Texts Documents with institutional authority/credibility from state institutions or expert bodies; translation can invoke or subvert authority
Selective Appropriation Strategic selection of specific source text elements while omitting others, transforming meaning while claiming fidelity
Framing Contextualization through headlines, paratexts, editorial commentary directing interpretation and constructing meanings
Ideological Translation Translation serving specific ideological purposes, reflecting/constructing political positions rather than neutral transfer
Hegemonic Discourse Dominant discourse reflecting/reproducing power relations of hegemonic (dominant) groups or nations
Partisan Journalism Journalistic practice openly advocating for political positions rather than claiming objectivity; characteristic of Italian press
Reframing Altering event/text interpretation through strategic contextualization, even when textual content remains similar

üîë Key Theories & Concepts

1. Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) - Fairclough's Three Dimensions

  • Text: Linguistic and semiotic features of discourse
  • Discursive Practice: Processes of text production, distribution, consumption
  • Social Practice: Broader social, political, ideological contexts shaping/shaped by discourse
  • Key Insight: All three levels must be analyzed to understand how discourse operates

2. CDA and Translation (Sch√§ffner 2004)

  • Translation = form of discursive practice that can reinforce, challenge, or transform ideological meanings
  • Translation choices never purely linguistic - reflect/construct ideological positions
  • Must examine: textual shifts, selective appropriation, framing, intertextuality, social function

3. Discourse and Ideology (van Dijk 1997)

  • Discourse serves to reproduce, legitimize, or challenge dominant ideologies
  • News discourse particularly important for constructing "common sense" serving elite interests
  • Language not neutral but ideologically loaded

4. Translation as Political Practice

  • Translation in news media = political practice embedded in power relations, not neutral transfer
  • Translation choices reflect and construct political positions
  • Same text can be translated to support opposed political narratives

üìä The Calipari Case - HIGH-PROBABILITY EXAM TOPIC

‚ö° CRITICAL EXAM TOPIC: Know this case study thoroughly - context, competing narratives, translation analysis, and implications!

Case Background

  • Date: March 4, 2005
  • Location: Baghdad, Iraq - U.S. military checkpoint
  • Victim: Nicola Calipari (Italian intelligence officer, SISMI)
  • Context: Just negotiated release of journalist Giuliana Sgrena from kidnappers
  • Event: U.S. soldiers fired on vehicle; Calipari killed protecting Sgrena
  • Italian Reaction: Massive public outrage; Calipari mourned as national hero
  • Political Impact: Intensified polarization about Italy's Iraq War participation

Conflicting Narratives

Source Narrative
U.S. Military Report Vehicle speeding (80-100 km/h); ignored warnings; soldiers followed rules; no wrongdoing
Sgrena (Survivor) Slow speed; no warnings; poorly marked checkpoint; unjustified shooting
Pro-Gov't Italian Press Tragic miscommunication; procedurally correct U.S. action; maintain alliance
Opposition Italian Press U.S. culpability; cover-up; evidence of American recklessness; demand withdrawal from Iraq

Italian Press Context

üìå Key Characteristics of Italian Media:
  • Political alignment: Newspapers explicitly aligned with parties/ideologies
  • Partisan journalism: Openly advocate positions rather than claiming objectivity
  • Political instrumentalization: Media owned by/connected to political actors
  • Reader awareness: Readers understand newspapers' political leanings

üî¨ Translation Analysis - Major Findings

üìå Finding 1: Selective Appropriation by Political Alignment
  • Pro-Government Papers: Translated sections emphasizing U.S. procedural correctness; quoted exoneration conclusions; minimized inconsistencies
  • Opposition Papers: Translated sections revealing contradictions/gaps; highlighted redactions suggesting cover-up; emphasized U.S. responsibility
  • Result: Same legitimized text (U.S. report) selectively translated to support opposed narratives
üìå Finding 2: Ideological Lexical Choices
English Term Pro-Gov't Translation Opposition Translation
"Engagement" Ingaggio (neutral) Scontro a fuoco (firefight - violent)
"Incident" Incidente (accident) Uccisione (killing - deliberate)
"Rules of engagement" Regole d'ingaggio (technical) Procedure apertura fuoco (opening fire)
üìå Finding 3: Reframing Through Headlines
  • Same translated passages given opposite meanings through headlines/paratexts
  • Example Pro-Gov't: "U.S. Report Confirms Procedures Followed; Miscommunication Tragedy"
  • Example Opposition: "U.S. Report Fails to Explain; Contradictions Suggest Cover-Up"
  • Framing directed readers toward opposite interpretations of identical content
üìå Finding 4: Competing Legitimization Strategies
  • Legitimizing U.S. (Pro-Gov't): Invoked institutional authority to legitimate American version; marginalized Sgrena's testimony; supported continued alliance
  • Delegitimizing U.S. (Opposition): Highlighted inconsistencies to expose report's limitations/biases; supported withdrawal from Iraq
  • Key Insight: Legitimized texts don't automatically impose authority when translated - can be appropriated for opposing agendas

‚ö° Theoretical Implications

1. Translation as Political Practice
  • Translation in news media cannot be understood as merely linguistic transfer
  • Translation = political practice embedded in power relations, ideological struggles, institutional contexts
  • Translation choices reflect and construct political positions
2. Politics of Legitimized Texts
  • Legitimized texts can be selectively appropriated for opposing agendas
  • Translation can either reinforce or subvert institutional authority
  • Same text translated to support contradictory political narratives
  • Authority must be actively constructed through framing/contextualization
3. CDA Value for Translation Studies
  • Multi-level analysis: textual features + discursive practices + social contexts
  • Makes visible ideological work that translation performs
  • Translation always operates ideologically (explicitly or implicitly)
4. Translation and Manipulation
  • Translation as manipulation when: selection misrepresents source; framing contradicts content; authority invoked while content distorted
  • Raises ethical questions about responsibility in political discourse
