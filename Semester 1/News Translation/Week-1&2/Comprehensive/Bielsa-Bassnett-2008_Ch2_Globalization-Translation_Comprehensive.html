Exam-Oriented Summary | Bielsa & Bassnett (2008)

üìå 1. Core Argument

Main Thesis: Language and translation have been systematically neglected in current literature on globalization, despite translation being a KEY MEDIATOR of global communication.
Despite English predominance as global lingua franca, there's exponential growth in significance of translation. This paradox is central to understanding globalization!

Two Fundamental Features of Globalization

  1. Substantial overcoming of spatial barriers
  2. Centrality of knowledge and information
Result:
  • Increased mobility of people and objects
  • Heightened contact between different linguistic communities
  • Global communications system distributing images/texts globally

‚è±Ô∏è 2. Time-Space Compression (David Harvey)

Time-Space Compression: A speeding up of the pace of life while overcoming spatial barriers (Harvey, 1989: 240)

Historical Development

Period Development Impact
Renaissance Revolution in space/time conception Scientific, objective notions emerged
Enlightenment Improved maps, chronometer Uniform, measurable space/time
19th century (post-1848) Telegraph, steam shipping, railways Spatial integration accelerated
Late 20th century Information technology revolution Instantaneous global communication
Harvey's Key Insight: Enlightenment thinkers believed translation from one language to another was always possible without destroying integrity of either language - similar to how maps allowed diversity to be appreciated within spatial order (Harvey, 1989: 250)
Time-space compression implies interconnection and interdependence - ending isolated feudal worlds linked to territorial boundaries

üîì 3. Disembedding Mechanisms (Anthony Giddens)

Disembedding: The "lifting out" of social relations from local contexts of interaction and their restructuring across indefinite spans of time-space (Giddens, 1991a: 21)

Types of Disembedding Mechanisms

1. Symbolic Tokens: Money form
  • Mode of deferral
  • Connects credit and liability where immediate exchange impossible

2. Expert Systems:
  • Deploy technical knowledge with validity independent of practitioners/clients
  • Bracket time and space

3. Writing:
  • Important disembedding mechanism
  • Makes communication possible across time and space
Giddens' Definition of Globalization: "The intensification of worldwide social relations which link distant localities in such a way that local happenings are shaped by events occurring many miles away and vice versa" (1991a: 64)
For Giddens, modernity is inherently globalizing! Presupposes uniformity of time measurement and time-space distantiation

üåä 4. The Space of Flows

Present Phase of Globalization (Robertson's "Uncertainty Phase")

Starting: Late 1960s

Characteristics:
  • Intensification of global interconnectedness
  • Heightening of global consciousness

Three Key Developments

# Development Details
1 Extreme mobility of capital Deregulation of financial markets + new information technologies (Castells, 2000: 96)
2 Fall in cost/time for movement Commodities AND people - holiday travel widespread after jet airplane price fall (Lash & Urry, 1994)
3 Global communications acceleration Satellite + global TV ownership (1980s-1990s) = 24/7 instantaneous transmission

Lash & Urry: Paradigmatic Mobility Media

Organized Capitalism: Railroads, telephone via wire, postal services, roads ‚Üí time-space convergence on NATIONAL scale

Disorganized Capitalism: Fibre-optic cable, satellite communications, air transport ‚Üí time-space convergence on GLOBAL scale (1994: 25)

Instantaneous Time

Instantaneous Time (Lash & Urry): Takes place at speed beyond realms of human consciousness, brings decline in significance of clock time, final stage in abstraction of time
Acceleration empties out both subjects and objects. Objects become disposable, social relationships emptied of meaning. Leads to flattening of objects AND subjects (Lash & Urry, 1994: 31)

üíª 5. Manuel Castells: Network Society

Core Concepts

Informationalism: Main source of productivity is action of knowledge upon knowledge itself (Castells, 2000: 17)

Information Technology Revolution: Spread globally in less than two decades (mid-1970s to mid-1990s)

Global Economy: "An economy with the capacity to work as a unit in real time, or chosen time, on a planetary scale" (2000: 101)

The Space of Flows

Characteristics:
  • Fluid mobility between connected places
  • Spatial fragmentation and discontinuity
  • "Switched-off areas" culturally & spatially discontinuous

Examples of fragmentation: American inner cities, French banlieues, African shanty towns, deprived rural China/India

Castells' "Digital Language"

"New workers and managers, connected to each other around tasks and performance, able to speak the same language, the digital language."
(Castells, 2000: 212)
Castells = NEO-BABELIAN position (Cronin, 2003: 59): believes in "universal, digital language" for global networks. Translation completely absent from his account despite being Catalan bilingual speaker!
Castells distinguishes: Space of flows (instant time, computerized networks, implicitly MONOLINGUAL) vs Space of places (clock time, everyday life, linguistic diversity)

üèôÔ∏è 6. Saskia Sassen: Global Cities - KEY ALTERNATIVE

Sassen provides EXCEPTION to theories focused on hypermobility. She emphasizes MATERIAL INFRASTRUCTURES and PRODUCTION processes!

Global Cities Concept

Definition: Strategic places that are nodes in space of flows

Key Innovation: Recovers "localized processes through which globalization exists" (1998: xix, xx)

Focus: Work behind command functions, actual production process in finance/services, global market-places
Sassen's Critique: Theories emphasizing hypermobility of capital and instantaneous transmission obscure infrastructure it presupposes (1998: 202)

Her Solution: "Introducing cities into analysis of economic globalization allows us to reconceptualize processes as concrete economic complexes situated in specific places"
Analytic Borderland: Global city as frontier zone requiring its own theorization - key place for articulation of global and local, organization of material infrastructures making globalization possible (Sassen, 2000: 220)
Global city contains dynamics of BOTH mobility AND fixity - breaks with Castells/Bauman view of distinctive time-spaces for capital vs labor

üîë 7. Translation's Analytical Place in Globalization

Why Translation is Invisible

Reason #1: Emphasis on instant communication makes translation processes invisible
  • Assumption of transparency
  • Similar to literary criticism (Venuti, 1995) or travel writing (Cronin, 2000)

Reason #2: Focus on circulation sphere precludes analysis of translation as material precondition
  • Global flows become "phantasmagoric and opaque" (Appadurai, 1996: 33-35)
  • Cannot analyze productive infrastructure

Commodity Fetishism in Globalization

"The whole world's cuisine is now assembled in one place... The interweaving of simulacra in daily life brings together different worlds (of commodities) in the same space and time. But it does so in such a way as to conceal almost perfectly any trace of origin, of the labour processes that produced them, or of the social relations implicated in their production."
(Harvey, 1989: 300)
Key Insight: Translation is a KEY SOCIAL RELATION that is obscured in this experience of simultaneity. It necessarily mediates between different linguistic communities.

Translation as Material Production

Venuti (1998: 106): "Translation exposes a fundamental idealism in philosophy by calling attention to the material conditions of concepts, their linguistic and discursive forms"

This Analysis: Translation = material precondition for circulation of meaning on global scale
Translation similar to globalization: Both about "making connections, linking one culture and language to another" (Cronin, 2003: 41)

üåê 8. Globalization as Translation

Asymmetries in Translation

English Dominance (1981 data):
  • 42% of translations worldwide from English
  • 13.5% from Russian
  • 11.4% from French

Low Translation INTO English:
  • 2.4% of books published in Britain (1990)
  • 2.96% in United States (1990)
  • Compare: 9.9% France (1985), 25.4% Italy (1989)

Venuti's Analysis Extended

Anglo-American dominance expressed in:
  1. Low number of books translated into English
  2. Form of translation: domesticating strategy based on fluidity and transparency

Effect: "Invisibly inscribe foreign texts with English language values and provide readers with narcissistic experience of recognizing their own culture in cultural other" (Venuti, 1995: 15)

Translation Invisibility in Globalization

Context How Invisibility Operates
Instant Communication Assumption of transparency - unproblematic rendering
Information Economy Pressure for transparent medium + instantaneous transport (Cronin, 2000: 111-12)
Real-time News Need for simultaneous translation - human factor eliminated

Cronin's Paradox

"The network underpinned by information technology brings Anglophone messages and images from all over the globe in minutes and seconds... This cosmopolitanism is partly generated by translators themselves who work to make information available in the dominant language of the market. However, what is devalued or ignored in the cyberhype of global communities is the effort, the difficulty and, above all else, the time required to establish and maintain linguistic (and by definition, cultural) connections."
(Cronin, 2003: 49)

üó£Ô∏è 9. International English & "McLanguage"

Characteristics

"McLanguage" (Snell-Hornby, 2000: 17): The "McLanguage" of globalised "McWorld" or "Eurospeak" of multilingual continent

Nature: Supraterritorial language that has lost essential connection to specific cultural context - expresses fundamental abstractions from disembedding

Bourdieu & Wacquant: "New Planetary Vulgate"

Argument: Newspeak = new form of imperialism
  • Universalises US particularisms under dehistoricized guises (2001: 2)
  • Term "globalization" itself dresses up American imperialism
  • Makes transnational relation of economic power appear natural

Consequence: "Mental colonization" - categories from North American uses/interests universally imposed (2001: 4)
BUT: Categories/concepts cannot be imposed directly - must be translated/adapted! Translation = KEY PROCESS for mediation between cultures. This qualifies one-sided view!

üìä 10. Localization Industry

Growth Area: Significant growth in recent decades

Definition: Global products tailored to meet needs of specific local markets (Cronin, 2003; Pym, 2004)

Objective: Simultaneous availability in all languages of product's target markets

Translation Strategies in Localization

Not uniform! Combine:
  • Domestication: Appeal to target buyers
  • Foreignization: Retain exoticizing connections to language of technological innovation
