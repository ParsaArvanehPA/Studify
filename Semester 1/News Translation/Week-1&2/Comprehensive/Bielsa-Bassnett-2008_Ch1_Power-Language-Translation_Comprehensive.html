üåç 3. Language & Power Relations

Key Principle: Translation does NOT occur on horizontal axis, but on VERTICAL axis

Factors creating inequality:
  • Political factors
  • Economic factors
  • Geographical factors
'Minority' languages: Term already implies inequality
Historical Example: Latin = high-status language of Europe until late Renaissance, then vernacular languages rose in prestige (18th century onwards)

üìñ 4. Key Theoretical Concepts

Sapir's Linguistic Difference (1956)

"No two languages are ever sufficiently similar to be considered as representing the same social reality. The worlds in which different societies live are distinct worlds, not merely the same world with different labels attached"
This quote is foundational - memorize it! Explains why translation faces constraints beyond just vocabulary

Translation Constraints

  • Linguistic: No two languages have identical structures/vocabulary
  • Cultural: Politeness conventions (e.g., Japanese vs Northern European)
  • Rhetorical: Different discourse norms (French vs English public speaking)

‚öôÔ∏è 5. Translation Theories - KEY FOR EXAM

Eugene Nida: Types of Equivalence

Formal Equivalence Dynamic Equivalence
Word-for-word Sense-for-sense
Adheres closely to source structure Abandons formal equivalence for broader view
Binary distinction (Romans) Rejects equivalence as sameness

Skopos Theory (Reiss & Vermeer, 1980s)

Core Principle: Objective of target text determines how it's translated

Key Concept: Equivalent effect rather than binary equivalence

Application: Translation can deviate enormously from source yet fulfill original purpose

Particularly helpful for: Technical translation and news translation
Skopos = purpose in Greek. Remember: purpose-driven translation allows major deviations if effect is equivalent

üîÑ 6. The Cultural Turn (Early 1990s)

Translation viewed NOT as isolated activity, but directly linked to world in which translators work

Broadened focus: From purely textual to including source AND target contexts

Even-Zohar's Cultural Model (1990)

Finding: Translation varies at different cultural moments
  • Culture seeking renewal: Translates MORE texts
  • Culturally self-sufficient culture: Translates LESS

Example: 19th century Europe (independence movements) = huge translation surge
Counter-example: Britain/US (global power) = translation marginal
Current example: China translation boom (rapid change/expansion)

üåê 7. Foreignization vs Domestication - CRITICAL CONCEPT

Origins: Schleiermacher (1813)

Two choices for translators:
  1. Take the reader back to the text
  2. Bring the text across to the readers

FOREIGNIZATION

  • Reproduces source text features
  • Final product seems strange/unfamiliar
  • Foregrounds cultural other
  • Text can never seem to originate in target language

DOMESTICATION

  • Adapts to target culture norms
  • Erases signs of foreignness
  • Venuti calls it "acculturation"
  • Prioritizes target needs over source

Lawrence Venuti's Position

Book: The Translator's Invisibility

Argument:
  • Domestication = ethnocentric appropriation
  • Proposes "dissident" translation practice
  • Foreignization as protest against hegemonic practices
  • "Abusive fidelity": Faithfulness NOT dependent on fluency
Venuti wants translators MORE visible, wants innovative practices that remind readers text didn't originate in their language

üì∞ 8. News Translation - SPECIAL CATEGORY

How News Translation Differs

Literary Translation News Translation
Foreignization debated ABSOLUTE domestication
Formal/stylistic equivalence matters Transmission of information primary
Size/shape preserved Heavy editing, synthesis, cutting
Clear source-target binary NO clear source - multiple versions
Translator visible (Venuti's goal) Translator invisibility essential
NEWS TRANSLATION: Material shaped for target audience, tailored to suit needs/expectations. Ideological shifts remain fundamentally important!

News Translation = More Like Interpreting

Similarities:
  • Works in real time
  • Must synthesize rapidly
  • May not have clearly definable single source
  • May operate within/across several languages
  • Domesticates foreign for target audience

üîß 9. News Translation Strategies & Processes

Tymoczko & Gentzler Definition (2002)

"Translation thus is not simply an act of faithful reproduction but, rather, a deliberate and conscious act of selection, assemblage, structuration, and fabrication ‚Äì and even, in some cases, of falsification, refusal of information, counterfeiting"

Key Strategies

  • Synthesis: Combining multiple sources
  • Omission: Deleting unfamiliar/inaccessible parts (VERY common)
  • Explication: Adding explanatory details
  • Restructuring: Adapting to target conventions
  • Editing: Crucial part - cutting, reshaping

    • Real Example: North Korea Train Explosion

    Source: Eric Wishart (Agence France-Presse)

    Translation chain:
    1. Chinese News Agency (Chinese + English) ‚Üí Death toll: 3,000
    2. South Korean agencies (Korean + English)
    3. Local translators ‚Üí English
    4. Central editing desk (Hong Kong)
    5. French writer translates English ‚Üí French
    6. Story distributed globally ‚Üí Re-translated locally

    Key insight: "Real time, big time. Reuters are on the case, AP are on the case. You've got to get the news out fast."
    Story may go through SEVERAL translations before reaching destination, reshaped at every stage according to linguistic/stylistic constraints

    Scale of News Translation

    Reuters example:
    • Works with ~18 languages
    • Daily output = Bible equivalent in volume
    • ~60% in English, Japanese 2nd with 7%
    • Millions of words translated daily

    Statement of thesis + substantiation Citing opponent's thesis + rebuttal + substantiation
