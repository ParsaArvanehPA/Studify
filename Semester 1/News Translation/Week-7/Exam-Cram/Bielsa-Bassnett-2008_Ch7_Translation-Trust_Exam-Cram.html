2. Habermas' Validity Claims (KEY THEORY)

Three Criteria for Valid Speech:

  1. TRUTH - Propositional accuracy; validated through negotiation in discourse
  2. APPROPRIATENESS - Contextual suitability; validated through negotiation in discourse
  3. SINCERITY - Must be taken on trust; validated by speaker's BEHAVIOR

Montgomery's argument: Speech in public sphere projects itself as valid by being perceived as true, sincere, or appropriate (or one or other)

‚ö° EXAM TIP: Speech acts like promising require sincerity for satisfactory performance. Speech acts like commanding or passing sentence do NOT require speaker's emotional commitment.

3. Montgomery's Diana Case Study (1997)

Three BBC Broadcasts Analyzed:

Speaker Delivery Perceived Sincerity
Tony Blair 6 hours after crash; open air; no notes; voice trembling with emotion High (coined "people's princess")
Queen 5 Sept; Buckingham Palace; teleprompter; composed, clear, fluent Lower (emotional understatement; formal)
Earl Spencer Funeral; appeared near tears; "blood family" pledge Highest (marked personal emotion)

Key finding: Framing and context matter MORE than language. Queen said "I say from my heart" but perceived less sincere due to delay and formality.

‚ö° MONTGOMERY (2001: 404): "It is neither authority of speaker nor any sense of source being 'authorized' that determines whether broadcast will be perceived as truthful, but rather the nature and the manner of the talk itself that makes for compelling testimony"

4. Theatricalization of News

Definition:

Theatricalization: Real-life exchanges written up as though they were pieces of theatre (dramatic text format with speaker names)

Paradox:

  • Broadcast news: Deemed more authentic (spontaneous)
  • Theatre: Perceived as less authentic (contrived)
  • Yet: Newspapers use dramatic text format for "heightened veracity"

Tools for Analysis:

Both broadcast news and performance analyzed via similar tools - both are complex semiotic systems requiring audience decoding

‚ö° When transcribed/published: Issues of veracity/authenticity become FUZZY because physical dimension removed (body language, tone, pace, original context)

5. Case Study: Saddam Hussein (December 2003 - October 2005)

Phase 1: Capture (December 2003)

The Dental Examination Photo

Multiple connotations:

  1. Emphasized fall from power to powerlessness
  2. Suggested reduction to level of animal
  3. Conversely: implied captors humane (concerned about welfare)

Effect: Image became narrative in itself, setting tone for subsequent reporting

Phase 2: Visual Images Drive Commonality

‚ö° KEY INSIGHT: Common threads in story presentation driven by accessibility to shared VISUAL IMAGES, NOT common verbal elements

The Clothing Theme (Cross-Cultural Trope)

  • Cannot be traced to single source - not directly translated
  • Surfaced repeatedly across British, French, Italian press
  • Used to signal gap between appearances and reality
  • Instrument of ridicule
  • First stage in theatricalization of trial

Examples:

  • Daily Telegraph: detailed clothing descriptions
  • Corriere della Sera: "Vestito scuro e il Corano tra le mani"
  • Le Figaro: "soigneusement teints" (expertly dyed - implies vanity)
  • The Times: Story about Saddam's tailor Recep Cesur

Phase 3: The Sun's Theatrical Presentation

Front page layout:

  • Block capitals: "SADDAM CAPTURED"
  • Headline: "WE GOT HIM"
  • Prefaced with: "Ladies and gentlemen..."

Effect: Rhetorical device creating comic tone; presenting Saddam like old music-hall act

6. Transcript Variations - The Core Problem

July 2004: First Court Appearance

"The following is an edited transcript of the translators' words... Some parts of the conversation were not included in the original transcript at all"

Implication: Journalists producing own versions during hearing; amalgamated with official transcript which didn't contain all exchanges

8. Domestication vs Foreignization

Domestication:

  • Brings text wholly into target system
  • Traces of foreignness minimized
  • Colloquial target language
  • Example: Telegraph version

Foreignization:

  • Retains foreign elements
  • Reminds readers text from elsewhere
  • Some awkward phrasing
  • Example: Independent version

9. Multiple Translation Processes

Whole Series of Translation Processes:

  1. Spoken ‚Üí Written
  2. Arabic ‚Üí English
  3. Full length ‚Üí Abbreviated
  4. Initial copy ‚Üí In-house style
  5. Neutral ‚Üí Ideologically positioned

At every stage: Manipulations took place

Yet: We're invited to accept final product as true and accurate

10. Cultural Determination of Truth/Sincerity/Appropriateness

‚ö° KEY ARGUMENT: Truth, appropriateness, and sincerity are culturally determined, therefore variable

Examples:

  • British irony/understatement inappropriate where hyperbole/assertiveness are norms
  • Saddam's court speeches acceptable in own context but read differently if unedited
  • Only through translation do such disparities emerge

Editing Therefore Multi-Layered:

  1. Recognizing cultural expectation differences
  2. Accommodating house style
  3. Accommodating ideological position
  4. Presentation as global theatre

11. Conclusion: What Is "Translation" in News?

üîë MAIN ARGUMENT: Although we use word "translation" for news translation, what happens is NOT translation as generally understood

Why Journalists Object to "Translator" Label:

  • Prefer: "international journalist"
  • Process brings information from one cultural context to another
  • May involve element of interlingual transfer
  • BUT focus NOT on linguistic transferral
  • Focus IS on: Transposition of information in format meeting target readership demands

The Source Text Problem

‚ö° CRITICAL ISSUE: There may not even BE an original - calls very idea of translation into question

Traditional Definition:

Translating = transfer of text produced in one language into another

News Reality:

Frequently cannot be defined as translation this way

Yet:

There IS translation dimension when story beginning in one context told/retold for readers in another language elsewhere

Even With Source Text:

Variations so great (from textual manipulations) that we have only loose grasp of what original might have been

What Actually Happens:

Networks of foreign correspondents working within/linked to news agencies write and shape stories for designated audiences

Result: Creation of completely new texts resisting definition of translation

Power inequalities: Domination of certain languages globally means power inequalities manifest in textual strategies

12. What Study of Global News Translation Does

Makes Us Aware Of:

  1. Manipulative processes underlying what we read
  2. Serious questions about extent to which we can ever know what was/wasn't said in another cultural context
