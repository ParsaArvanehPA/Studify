Journalism and Translation Ethics

Author: Georgios Floros
Source: Bielsa (2022), Chapter 16
Focus: Comparative Analysis of Ethics in Journalism and Translation Studies

1. Introduction: The Asymmetrical Relationship

Central Argument

Despite the long historical relationship between journalism and translation (dating to 17th century), their connection remains asymmetrical. Translation studies has developed extensive terminology and frameworks for news translation, but journalism studies largely ignores translation as a distinct practice.

Key Terms for News Translation

Term Coined By Definition
Transediting Stetting (1989) Combination of translation and editing in news production
News Translation Vuorinen (1995), Holland (2006) Translation processes specific to news industry
JTR Valdeón (2015) Journalistic Translation Research as subfield
Journalator van Doorslaer (2012) Journalist-translator hybrid professional

Why Journalists Underestimate Translation

  • Power dynamics: News translation done by journalists, not translators (Bielsa 2007)
  • Professional hierarchy: Journalism as "fourth estate" vs. translation's lower professional status
  • Outdated conception: Journalists view translation as mere "word-for-wordism" and correspondence-finding
  • Lack of consensus: No stable understanding of translation in TS to disseminate to other fields

2. Overview of Translation Ethics in News Production

2.1 Explicit Approaches to Translation Ethics

Floros (2012) - Ethical Relativity

Concept: Fluctuation of ethical thresholds based on subjective values and norms

Finding: "News translation presents a case where ethical thresholds are pushed to relatively low limits because of the subjectivity of the norms governing the dominant part of the profession (journalistic norms)" (2012: 937)

Talebinejad & Shahi (2017) - Ideology and Teleology

Framework: Ideological distortions in news translation

Ethical model: Teleological/consequentialist - choices motivated by political narratives rather than "right thing to do"

Matsushita (2019) - Risk Management

Focus: Pure instances of translation (direct quotations with identifiable source texts)

Framework: Pym's risk management theory applied to news translation

Key concept: Risk mitigation - journalators incur one risk (distortion) to reduce another (job security, circulation)

Scammell (2018) - Foreignizing Alternative

Innovation: Challenges domestication norm in news translation

Proposal: Foreignization as ethical alternative that:

  • Increases reader contact with foreign source language/culture
  • Helps journalists counter accuracy issues in translating reported speech

Application: Challenges Reuters Handbook's domesticating approach

Jaber (2016) - Media Studies Perspective

Unique contribution: From communication/media studies background examining translation ethics

Case study: Syrian humanitarian disaster in The Guardian and The New York Times

Finding: Darwish (2009) surveyed 370+ global media ethics codes - NONE mention translation as factor in ensuring accuracy/objectivity

Call: Systematic inclusion of translation ethics in journalistic codes of practice

2.2 Implicit Approaches (Broader Theoretical Frameworks)

Theoretical Frameworks Used in News Translation Ethics:

  • Narrative theory (Baker 2006)
  • Political and sociological theory
  • Reception theory
  • Ideology and conflict theory
  • Social and institutional norms
  • New cosmopolitanism (Bielsa 2016a,b) - ethical openness to world and others

3. Journalism Ethics vs Translation Ethics: A Comparison

3.1 Similarities

Remarkable Convergences

Despite disciplinary separation, journalism ethics and translation ethics share striking parallels in both theoretical conceptualization and practical concerns.

Journalism Ethics Principles Translation Ethics Parallels Shared Concept
Truth, Objectivity, Accuracy Fidelity, Faithfulness, Accuracy Invariance to source material
Impartiality, Neutrality Neutrality, Invisibility Absence of personal bias
Fairness Justice toward all agents Equitable treatment
Public Accountability Accountability, Responsibility Answerable to stakeholders
Social Responsibility Social Responsibility (recent in TS) Broader societal role

3.2 Theoretical Development Trajectories

Both Disciplines Show Similar Evolution:

  1. Traditional phase: Rigid adherence to ideal concepts (objectivity, fidelity)
  2. Questioning phase: Re-examination due to:
    • Journalism: Digital media, citizen journalism, media convergence
    • Translation: Conflict contexts, sociological turn, activism
  3. Contemporary phase: Turn to more operational concepts:
    • Self-reflexivity
    • Accountability
    • Responsibility

3.3 Kohlberg's Theory of Moral Development Applied

Three Stages of Ethical Reasoning (Kohlberg 1969)

Applied by Lee, Coleman & Molyneux (2016) to journalism ethics

Stage 1: Preconventional

  • Driven by personal interests
  • Extrinsic motivations (avoid punishment, authority)
  • Corresponds to: Consequentialist ethics, risk management

Stage 2: Conventional

  • Motivated by desire to "be good"
  • Aim to maintain social norms
  • Corresponds to: Professional codes of practice

Stage 3: Postconventional

  • Highest moral conscience
  • Critical, independent thinking
  • Committed to universal values (Kantian universality, justice)
  • Corresponds to: Virtue ethics, social responsibility

3.4 Impact of Social Norms (Lee, Coleman & Molyneux 2016)

Key Empirical Findings

Two Types of Social Norms:

  1. Injunctive norms: Perception of what others think we SHOULD do
  2. Descriptive norms: Perception of what others ARE ACTUALLY doing

Research Hypotheses & Results:

  • H1: Injunctive norms → predict UNETHICAL behavior ✓ CONFIRMED
  • H2: Descriptive norms → predict ETHICAL behavior ✓ CONFIRMED

Implication for Translation: Since translators work in isolation (unlike journalists in newsrooms), professional organizations and agencies must actively promote beneficial descriptive norms to foster ethical behavior.

3.5 Ethics of Quotations

Killenberg & Anderson (1993) - Rhetorical Approach

Context: Study of quotations in MONOLINGUAL news production

Key insight: "Each quote, no matter how literally accurate in a self-contained sense, is necessarily inaccurate in a contextual sense" (1993: 50)

Test of Publicity (from Sissela Bok 1979):

"Is my behavior such that I would be comfortable having all my audiences—interview subject, editor, public, and self—simultaneously monitor my choice(s), knowing that I might later be called on to justify my actions?"

Role implication: Journalists as MEDIATORS (not mere conduits) - exactly like translators

Parallel: Translators don't use quotation marks for translations because mediated communication inevitably involves adjustment

3.6 Cross-Professional Ethical Tensions

Borden & Bowers (2009): Journalism Shares Ethical Tensions with Other Professions

Tension In Journalism Parallel in Translation
Authority vs Fallibility Risk appearing naïve if not acknowledging mistakes or non-professional knowledge (like academics) Tension with non-professional/volunteer translators; acknowledging limitations
Autonomy vs Accountability Public interest vs corporate interest (like engineers) Consequentialist dilemmas in translation/interpreting
Procedure vs Substance What procedure allows vs what is good to do (like law) Adherence to official/unstated translation policies vs ethical choice

4. The Crucial Difference: Opinion and Commentary

The Ontological Distinction

Despite remarkable similarities, there is a fundamental difference:

"Journalism entails commentary, translation does not."

Why This Matters:

  • Translators: Produce texts to function in different context while PRESERVING original communicative aim (Schäffner 2012)
  • Journalists: Pursue their OWN communicative aims when producing texts
  • Editing in translation: Inevitable necessity to keep balances in mediation
  • Editing in journalism: Almost ontological necessity that defines the profession

Historical Context:

"For most of the 400 years of modern journalism, journalists were expected to be partisan, not impartial" (Ward 2009: 71)

Occupational roles of journalists "fundamentally involve judgement, reporting, language, narration and analysis" (Zelizer & Allan 2010: 63, emphasis added)

5. Towards Synergy: Proposed Framework

5.1 Why Journalists May Not Recognize Translation

Floros's Explanation

The striking similarities in ethical approaches may lead journalists to believe that translation ethics is already subsumed in journalism ethics. Combined with the crucial difference regarding opinion/commentary, this explains why:

  1. Journalists feel less attached to source text than translators
  2. Translation is regarded as merely one technique among many in news production

5.2 Space for Synergy: At the Theoretical Level

Key Argument: Synergy vs Hybridity

Synergy (preferred term) = convergence of DIFFERING activities for mutual benefit

Hybridity = combination of similar undertakings

The two disciplines should converge at theoretical/conceptual level precisely because of their differing ethical aspects, not despite them.

5.3 Concessions Needed from Translation Studies

1. Embrace TOPC Framework

TOPC = Translation in Other Professional Contexts (Floros 2021)

  • Modeled on TOLC (Translation in Other Learning Contexts - González Davies 2014)
  • Acknowledge translation carried out by non-professional translators working in other professions
  • Tailor TS concepts to specific needs of other professions
  • Place "translation (also) at the exporting end" (Dam, Nisbeth Brøgger & Zethsen 2018)

2. Offer Theoretical Apparatus for Mediation

Emphasize that TS has extensive frameworks to inform mediating professionals on balancing opposing forces while reproducing messages in framework of social responsibility

Relevant TS Theories for Journalism:

  • Skopos Theory - functionalist approach
  • Translational Action Model - purposeful mediation
  • Polysystems Theory - role of social strata in mediation

3. Expand Translation Ethics to Include Translation Policies

Study how translation policies emerge and operate from ethical perspective → contributes to examining gatekeeping and newsworthiness in journalism

5.4 Concessions Needed from Journalism Studies

1. Recognize Translation as Sophisticated Activity

  • Move beyond "correspondence retrieving" conception
  • Acknowledge advanced apparatus of functional theories
  • See translation as governed by sophisticated ethical standards

2. View Translation as Resembling (Not Differing from) Journalism

In professional-sociological framework, translation is similar activity to journalism in terms of mediation, agency, and social responsibility

3. Evidence of Interest

Valdeón (2018) shows communication scholars ARE interested in translation as indispensable part of news production → common ground worth exploring through interdisciplinary projects

5.5 Research Needs

In Translation Studies In Journalism Studies
Specify ways to accommodate translation in other professional realms Abandon understanding of translation as mere lingual transfer
Develop TOPC framework systematically Recognize translation as powerful tool for resolving journalistic dilemmas
Investigate which norms promote ethical behavior Include translation in ethics codes and training
Figure out ways to promote beneficial norms among isolated translators Study empirically how translation affects news production (like Lee et al. 2016 study)

6. Conclusion

Summary of Argument

  1. Journalism and translation are related disciplines concerned with mediation
  2. Their ethics overlap substantially but display significant, ontological differences
  3. These differences are not sufficient to keep them apart
  4. In practice (news translation), they already work together in explicit and subtle ways
  5. This encourages synergy at the conceptual level
  6. Synergy requires both disciplines to:
    • Realize similarities and convergence possibilities
    • Actively provide space for convergence

Future Directions

For Translation Studies:

  • Cultivate TOPC as general framework
  • Understand translation as ubiquitous social practice across professions
  • Respond to needs of transcreation, (trans)adaptation, and other synergetic forms

For Journalism Studies:

  • Abandon obsolete understanding of translation
  • Recognize translation as powerful tool for resolving ethical dilemmas
  • Include translation systematically in codes of practice

8. Essential Concepts Glossary

Ethical Relativity

Fluctuation of ethical thresholds in translation as result of subjective values and norms (Floros 2012)

Consequentialist Ethics

Ethical approach that judges actions based on outcomes/consequences; corresponds to Kohlberg's Stage 1 (preconventional)

Virtue Ethics

Ethical approach based on what is inherently right/wrong; corresponds to Kohlberg's Stage 3 (postconventional)

Risk Mitigation

Incurring one kind of risk to reduce another (Pym & Matsushita 2018); e.g., accepting distortion to maintain job security

Test of Publicity (Bok 1979)

Ethical test asking whether one would be comfortable having all audiences monitor one's choices and later justify actions

TOPC (Translation in Other Professional Contexts)

Framework proposed by Floros (2021) for understanding translation as ubiquitous activity carried out across professions, each with different purposes

Pragmatic Objectivity (Ward 2009)

Re-establishment of objectivity in methods of news production/presentation (not absence of opinion), maintaining truth as "fidelity with real world"

New Cosmopolitanism (Bielsa 2016)

Ethical approach to openness to the world and to others in journalism and translation
