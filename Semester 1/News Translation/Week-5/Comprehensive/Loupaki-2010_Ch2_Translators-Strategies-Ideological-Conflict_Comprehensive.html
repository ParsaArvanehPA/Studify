Investigating Translators' Strategies in Rendering Ideological Conflict: The Case of News Translation

Author: Elpida Loupaki
Source: Schäffner & Bassnett (2010), Chapter 2
Focus: Translation Strategies, Ideology, News Translation, Greek Press

1. Introduction & Research Focus

Central Research Question

How do translators handle ideological conflict in news translation, and what factors influence their choices, particularly regarding the newspaper's political orientation?

This chapter examines the strategies used by translators when dealing with ideological conflict embedded in source texts, specifically in the context of news translation. The study analyzes articles from British and US press (The Guardian, The Economist, The New York Times) translated into Greek newspapers (Kathimerini and To Vima).

Corpus Overview

  • Source: The Guardian, The Economist, The New York Times
  • Target: Kathimerini (centre-right), To Vima (centre-left)
  • Period: April-June 2007
  • Word Count: ~4,500 words

2. Theoretical Framework

2.1 Language and Ideology

Norman Fairclough (1989)

"Ideology is pervasively present in language"

Robert Hodge & Gunther Kress (1993)

"Ideology involves a systematically organized presentation of reality [...] and presenting anything in or through language involves selection"

Definitions of Ideology

  • Neutral definitions: A set of ideas or beliefs
  • Marxist tradition: Expression of antagonism between social classes (Eagleton 1991)
  • Power perspective: Ideology as ground of conflict and struggle between contending powers

Conflict in Language

As Lukes (1974) notes: "The most effective and insidious use of power is to prevent conflict from arising in the first place."

Manifestations in language:

  • Emotive words (positively or negatively charged)
  • Euphemisms
  • Syntactic choices controlling causality or modality

2.2 Ideology and Translation

Christina Schäffner (2003)

"Any translation is ideological since the choice of a source text and the use to which the subsequent target text is put is determined by the interests, aims, and objectives of the social agents"

Key Insight on Translation Norms

Translators, as members of a socio-political structure, tend to reproduce internalized behavioral constraints (norms) shared by their community. These norms can compete with each other, reflecting constant battles for domination of different sub-groups (Toury 1995, 1999).

Important Scholarly Works:

  • Translation, Power, Subversion - Álvarez & Vidal (1996)
  • Contributions by María Calzada Pérez (2003)
  • Translation and Conflict: A Narrative Account - Mona Baker (2006)

2.3 News Translation

"The important role played by translation in the production and circulation of global information flows has been made invisible and transparent, and this has led to the assumption that information can circulate unaltered across different linguistic communities and cultures." (Bielsa 2005)

The Myth of "Sameness"

The concept of accuracy and objectivity in news translation is largely advanced by journalists themselves. However, as Baker (2006) demonstrates, translation often changes:

  • Sequence of events
  • Positioning of participants
  • Framing

These changes create new perspectives and may result in a new discursively constructed reality.

2.4 Ideological Conflict

Definition

Ideological Conflict (as used in this chapter): A result of competing socio-economic systems in politics, involving standardized and biased visions of the world promoted by antagonistic political forces (Right vs. Left).

Van Dijk's "Ideological Square" (1997)

An argumentation strategy in political discourse consisting of:

  1. Emphasizing OUR good actions
  2. De-emphasizing OUR bad actions
  3. Emphasizing THEIR bad actions
  4. De-emphasizing THEIR good actions

4. Translation Strategies Identified

Three Main Strategies

Strategy Pattern Description Techniques
1. Reproducing Ideological Conflict [conflict → conflict] Ideological conflict in ST is maintained in TT Literal translation
2. Erasing Ideological Conflict [conflict → Ø] Ideological conflict in ST is removed in TT Neutralization
Omission
3. Introducing New Conflict [Ø → conflict] New ideological conflict appears in TT Addition/Explicitation
Omission

5. Detailed Analysis of Strategies

5.1 Strategy 1: Reproducing Ideological Conflict

Example 1: Polarization Language

ST (The Guardian): "American accusations that European countries ganged up against the Bush administration"

TT (Kathimerini): Greek equivalent of "formed a gang against" - almost literal reproduction

Analysis: The emotionally charged expression promoting "us vs. them" schema is preserved.

Example 2: Marginalizing Opponents

ST (NYT): "refusing to entertain alternative views, marginalizing dissenters"

TT (To Vima): Literal Greek translation maintained

Analysis: Emotive language showing polarization is reproduced.

Example 3-4: Framing Devices

Framing in Al-Qaeda articles:

  • NYT: More neutral - "group," "organization," "network," "operatives," "leaders"
  • The Economist: More charged - "Islamist militants," "jihadists," "terrorists," "terrorist group"

Analysis: Greek translations reproduce these framing choices, maintaining the ideological positioning.

5.2 Strategy 2: Erasing Ideological Conflict

Example 5: Neutralization

ST: "in the Wolfowitz row" (informal, conflict-laden)

TT: "in the Wolfowitz case" (neutral, formal)

Analysis: The tense, conflict-indicating term is neutralized.

Example 6: Metaphor Neutralization

ST: "The Bush malaise could provide a pick-me-up for Europe"

TT: "Bush's weakening can turn out to be positive for Europe"

Analysis: Medical metaphors (GOVERNMENTS ARE HUMAN BODIES) are neutralized to simple descriptive language.

Example 7-8: Strategic Omissions

Omitted paragraph 1: Wolfowitz's advocacy for Iraq war after 9/11

Omitted paragraph 2: Details about Uzbekistan aid suspension

Analysis: Information considered already known or less important to Greek interests is omitted.

Scholarly Perspectives on Omission

Bassnett (2005): "Debates about the freedom of the translator are of no relevance in the context of news reporting." Omissions are very frequent in news translation.

Sidiropoulou (2004): "English source articles have almost always been much longer as only parts of them are often transferred in the Greek TTs."

5.3 Strategy 3: Introducing New Conflict

Example 9: Addition/Explicitation

ST: No mention in opening

TT: Added clause - "who was finally forced to resign because of the general outcry caused by the administration of the World Bank"

Analysis: Presents information as "given" (Halliday's distinction), showing clear one-sided position.

Example 10: Perspective Shift

ST (Dañino's quote): "He [Wolfowitz] presumes that anyone who opposes him is incompetent or corrupt"

TT: "One of his partners says he called Mr. Dañino 'an incompetent'"

Analysis: Personal judgment becomes presented as fact, intensifying negative portrayal.

Example 11: Agent Explicitation

ST: "killed by a missile strike in Pakistan"

TT: "killed during an attack by the Americans in Pakistan"

Analysis: Euphemistic passive becomes active with explicit agent, showing disapproval.

Example 12-13: Framing Intensification

ST: "some of Al-Qaeda's methods"

TT: "Algerian extremists followed operational standards"


ST: Neutral description of tactics "in Algeria"

TT: "fanatical Islamists in Algeria"

Analysis: Negative framing intensified; Kathimerini used more charged language after Algiers bombing.

Example 14-15: Strategic Omissions Creating Bias

Pattern in NYT article: Zig-zag pattern (pro, con, pro, con arguments)

Pattern in Greek TT: Systematic omission of "pro-Wolfowitz" arguments

Result: One-sided argumentation, altered perspective

6. Key Concepts for Analysis

6.1 Framing (Tannen & Wallat 1993)

Definition

Concepts like "frame," "script," or "schema" reflect structures of expectation, determining how events are perceived and verbalized.

Framing Devices:

  • Interpretative naming: Words chosen to describe persons, objects, situations
  • Evaluative language: Adjectives/adverbs indicating speaker's opinion
  • Modals: Reflecting judgment according to speaker's standards

Framing Changes Over Time

Research finding: Kathimerini's framing of Al-Qaeda changed after Algiers bombing:

  • Before bombing: Neutral - "organization," "members," "fighters," "groups"
  • After bombing: Charged - "murderous strikes," "horrifying Islamist organization," "extremists," "fundamentalists"

6.2 Emotive Words & Power-Function Ideology

Hodge & Kress (1993)

Emotive words serve "power-function ideology" - they reproduce polarized representation of the world by:

  • Emphasizing differences
  • Creating idea of superiority of one group over others
  • Promoting hostility

7. Factors Influencing Translator Choices

7.1 Newspaper's Political Orientation

Key Finding

The differentiation factor is the subject matter in relation to the newspaper's political position. It matters WHEN (against which political background) a translator adopts a particular strategy and FOR WHICH kind of argument.

Greek Anti-American Tradition

Left-wing/centre-left ideology in Greece has a long-standing anti-American tradition rooted in:

  • US government choices during Junta of Colonels
  • Cyprus issue

Example: Mikis Theodorakis (famous composer) stated after 9/11: "The US is the contemporary demon" (Kathimerini, 4 Nov 2001)

7.2 Target Audience Expectations

Van Dijk (1993)

People tend to accept knowledge/opinions they see as authoritative, trustworthy, and credible, unless inconsistent with their own beliefs.

Sidiropoulou (2004)

Making explicit what is implicit indicates the translator is aware that readers may be "more willing to take the role of a denier" and "are used to perceiving the world in terms of contrasts."

7.3 Relevance to Greek Reality

Coverage Patterns

Wolfowitz affair coverage:

  • Kathimerini: 2 articles (both translations), no Greek originals
  • To Vima: 1 reference only

Analysis: Lower relevance to Greek reality affected reporting options (number of texts, sources, publication timing).

7.4 Invisibility of Translation

Translation Practice in Greek Newspapers

  • Translators are NOT professional translators (often journalists, editors, columnists)
  • Translator names are NOT mentioned in published articles
  • Only foreign newspaper logo and columnist name appear
  • Articles not explicitly presented as translations

Hypothesis: This choice relates to:

  • International standing of foreign publications
  • Intention to create impression of exactitude
  • Readers believing translated articles are "more impartial"

8. Research Findings & Conclusions

8.1 Main Findings

  1. News translation is ideological: News production and its translation can rarely be ideologically free.
  2. Micro-level = Macro-level impact: Although strategies are applied at textual micro-level, they may result in shifts at macro-level.
  3. Equal strategy use, different application: Both newspapers use all three strategies at approximately the same rate, BUT apply them differently based on political orientation.
  4. Compliance with publication ideology: Translators try to comply with the ideological profile promoted by their publication.

8.2 Translation is NOT Innocent

Conclusion

Translation in the news environment is:

  • NOT as innocent as believed by some readers
  • NOT absolutely identical to original text
  • SHAPED by ideological factors
  • INFLUENCED by political orientation of publication

8.3 Open Questions

Terminological Issue

Question: Should we still use "translator" in news (re)production context?

  • People involved are not professional translators
  • They don't consider themselves translators
  • Multiple people may participate (journalists, revisers, editors)
  • Need for more general term?

Author's position: From product-based perspective, we can call people involved in text production "translators" when studying from Translation Studies viewpoint.

8.4 Future Research Directions

  • Investigation of norms governing translation of ideology in Greek news translation context
  • Larger corpus studies
  • Process-based research (not just product-based)
