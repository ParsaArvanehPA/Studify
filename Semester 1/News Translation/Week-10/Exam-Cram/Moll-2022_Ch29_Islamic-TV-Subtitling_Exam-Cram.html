1. Context: Iqraa Channel

‚úì Basic Facts

  • Founded: 1998 by Saudi media mogul
  • Type: Transnational Islamic satellite channel
  • Content: Arabic preaching programmes
  • Translation team: Cairo, Egypt
  • Target: English subtitles for Anglophone viewers

‚ö° Translators' Dual Self-Perception

  1. 'Preachers by proxy': Transmitting correct religious knowledge
  2. 'Cultural mediators': For radically different audience from Arabic original

Goal: Subtitle audience as edified/entertained as Arabic audience

2. The "Manipulation" Debate

"Muslim and Proud" Crisis (2011)

Adam (Muslim-American Editor) Randa (Translation Manager)
Sent scathing indictment instead of editing Felt moral imperative to extol Islam's virtues
Programme made Hindu worship sound 'silly' "This isn't travel show - we are calling people to Islam"
"Is it advisable to announce this on international TV?" Frustrated by Adam's refusal to 'manipulate' source text
Manipulation = lying Manipulation = skilled cross-cultural competence

Not Translating Language but Culture

  • Translators: NOT translating language (lugha) but culture (thaqafa)
  • Required traversing/adopting multiplicity of cultural viewpoints
  • Including another culture's view of one's own culture

3. Foreignization ‚Üí Domestication Shift

‚ö° 9/11 as Catalyst

  • Translators understood 'war on terror' as 'war on Islam'
  • US media (esp. Fox News) represented Islam as civilizational other
  • Saw own foreignization strategies as contributing to problematic othering
  • Major rethinking of translation strategies

Strategy Evolution

Before 9/11: Foreignization After 9/11: Domestication
Transliterated Arabic terms (e.g., 'Allah') Translate to English equivalents (e.g., 'God')
Asked audience to decipher Arabic words Make linguistically/culturally sensible to audience
Contributed to othering Reclaim intelligibility of Islam
- Counter-critiques of Western critiques

4. Venuti's Theory vs Iqraa Practice

Lawrence Venuti (1995): The Translator's Invisibility

  • Argument: Fluency/domestication = "ethnocentric reduction of foreign text to target-language cultural values"
  • Call for: Foreignizing translation registering socio-linguistic differences
  • Postcolonial view: Foreignization = resistance to cultural imperialism
  • 'Good' translation: Sounds like translation (non-idiomatic, non-fluent)

‚ö° Iqraa Translators' Counter-Argument

Problem with foreignization:

  • Made Arabic content seem incoherent, child-like, extra-terrestrial
  • Reception context militating against sympathetic reading
  • Perpetuated source-culture misperceptions
  • Example - jihad: Non-translation reinforced 'religion of war' stereotype, glossed over 'personal struggle' meaning

Randa: "I didn't want us to sound like aliens. I didn't want to reconfirm stereotypes. I wanted to show we are normal people, that we have much in common."

Strategy: Domestication of language = domestication of otherness

Appropriated strategies usually associated with ethnocentrism/domination to speak back to negative representations

5. Geopolitical Context

Power-Laden Mediated Regimes

  • Hollywood films (Shaheen 2008)
  • Western news (Said 1981)
  • Memoirs by (ex)-Muslim women (Abu-Lughod 2013)

MEMRI Threat

  • Zionist organization sending subtitled clips from Middle Eastern TV to US news/Congress
  • Mona Baker: MEMRI "circulating translations... to elaborate narrative of Arab societies as extremist, anti-Semitic, threat to Western democracies"
  • Result: Iqraa translators very careful with subtitles

6. Qur'anic Justifications

Verse 14:4 - Speak in People's Tongue

"And never have We sent forth any apostle otherwise than [with a message] in his own people's tongue, so that he might make [the truth] clear unto them"

  • Message of Islam could - indeed must - be conveyed within idioms of people addressed to

Verse 30:22 - Linguistic Diversity

"And among His wonders is the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the diversity of your tongues and colors..."

  • Human diversity (linguistic and otherwise) indicative of divinity
  • What mattered: NOT particular form of language but what it communicated

Verse 49:13 - Ta'arruf (Mutual Knowing)

"[We] have made you into nations and tribes so that you might come to know one another... Verily, the noblest of you in the sight of God is the one of who is most deeply conscious of Him"

  • Intercultural communication = practice valued by God
  • Up to humans to learn each other's languages
  • Comprehensibility and mutual knowing = divinely obligated goods
  • Also ends to establishing right conduct fostered by God-consciousness

7. Internal Debate: 'Isa vs Jesus

‚ö†Ô∏è Dissenting Translator's Argument

"Translating sayyidna (our master) 'Isa as Jesus or the Messiah - these names have specific connotations contradicting fundamental Islamic conception... 'Isa is not Jesus - big difference. For them Jesus is son of God, and this is certainly not what 'Isa was."

Danger: Distinctive theological claims (divine truth) grossly misrepresented through being rendered familiar

Domestication Champions' Response

  • Should be accomplished using terms/names recognizable to non-Muslims
  • Even as given new definitions/content in use
  • 'Jesus' could come to pragmatically signify Qur'anic 'Isa

8. Translation as Dual Critique

‚ö° External Critique

  • Challenge non-Muslims' criticisms of Islam
  • Show religion's true nature to imagined audience of religious neophytes

‚ö° Internal Critique

  • Directed at other Muslims' (preachers) interpretations of Islam
  • Translators held many preachers in low regard (mistakes in Qur'anic recitation, dubious commentaries, faulty citations)

In Both Cases

Critique conducted from within normative theological terrain of Islamic tradition

9. Religious vs Technical Texts

Technical Text Religious Text
Total 'faithfulness' to source Understand meaning behind speaker's words
No consideration of accuracy Rephrase appropriately for target audience
Meaning fixed by author Meaning fixed by God/Prophet (NOT preacher)
No moral responsibility Morally responsible before God

‚ö° KEY CONCEPT: Original Text

  • Speech NOT window onto individual subjectivity
  • Original text NOT original Arabic broadcast
  • Rather 'Message of Islam' as interpreted by each translator
  • Fidelity to God at times necessitated betrayal of preacher
  • Textual interventions bypassing preacher's Arabic speech
  • Tacitly create new, more correctly Islamic, programme through English subtitles

10. Tafsir and Tarjama

Brett Wilson (2014): Historical Relationship

13th-century Lisan al-Arab:

  • Translator (mutarjim) = 'interpreter of language' (mufassir al-lisan)
  • In Muslim contexts: interpretation (tafsir) and translation (tarjama) defined one another
  • Often difficult to distinguish

Both carried similar risk:

  • Both must make discretionary choice about apt meaning
  • "Maybe chosen meaning is what God intended and maybe it is not"

In Islamic TV subtitling:

  • Dynamic unfolds beyond Qur'an words to words ABOUT Qur'an
  • Relevant question: NOT degree of correspondence to preaching programme
  • More its conformity to God's will as interpreted by translator through Qur'anic revelation
  • Practice = space of argument/debate not only ABOUT text but WITH text (Talal Asad 1995)

11. Other-Worldly Consequences

‚ö° Yara's Distinction

Can translate (with markers):

  • "Hijab is just cultural habit, not religious obligation"
  • Prefix with "I believe" so viewers understand as one person's opinion

CANNOT translate:

  • "Fianc√© has right to see betrothed's hair" (presented as ruling)
  • Why: NOT just (falsely) descriptive but also potentially prescriptive
  • Translating would make Yara morally liable for sin incurred by viewer who then demands to see fianc√©e's hair

12. Editors vs Translators: Epistemological Clash

Foreign Editors (e.g., Adam) Egyptian Translators
Argued ABOUT programmes Argued WITH programmes
Programmes = original source text Programmes = interpretation of real original (Qur'an/Prophet)
Faithfully publicly convey (even if privately lampoon) Create 'parallel programs' when necessary
Translation replaces original Subtitles have authorial autonomy
Stability of meaning across codes Meaning NOT fixed by preacher but by divine intention
English must mean same as Arabic Limited by autonomous authority of divine revelation
Individual authorship/autonomy Responsible before God

13. Example: Nawal's Woman Status Subtitle

Illustrative Case

Preacher said: Marriage reasons, love least important; women weak/dependent, husbands accrue hasinat (divine recompense) by caring for them

Nawal's reaction:

  • Worried: "Westerners think Muslims mutakhlifin (backwards)"
  • Emphatically disagreed: characterization of women as always weak/dependent Islamically incorrect
  • Worried: dovetailed with Western stereotypes

Nawal's subtitle:

"Women enjoy a special status in Islam.
Those who support them receive God's mercy."

Analysis:

  • Nothing doctrinally incorrect
  • Women DO hold 'special status' (preacher's point)
  • But deliberately ambiguous about what makes women 'special'
  • All gender referents dropped (choice of 'those')

üéØ Central Argument

Translation at Iqraa = site for enacting religious reformist ethic attuned to both:

  1. Compelling this-worldly representation of Islam
  2. Individual otherworldly consequences of translating incorrect interpretations

Key Finding: Even while all translators argued from within Islamic frame, they did NOT espouse single 'Islamic' translational ideology but multiplicity of ethico-religious idioms, all claiming Islamic mantle

Stakes: NOT just getting it right linguistically/pragmatically, but getting it right by God, for which translators individually accountable in Hereafter

Subtitling became site of struggle between:

  • Competing epistemologies of translation/mediation
  • Competing epistemologies of critique
