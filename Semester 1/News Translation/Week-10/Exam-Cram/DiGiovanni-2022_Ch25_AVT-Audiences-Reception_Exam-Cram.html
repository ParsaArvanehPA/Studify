2. Defining Audiences - Key Classifications

‚ö†Ô∏è EXAM CRITICAL: Defining 'Audience' is Complex

Further specification needed for concept to get substance. All classifications presuppose interdisciplinarity.

Ian Christie (2012) - Audience Types

Type Definition
Contemporary vs Aggregated Aggregated = cumulative viewers over years/decades
Imagined vs Economic/Statistical Imagined = sociological; Economic = marketing impact
Individual vs Mass Focus on individual needs OR mass behavior
"We" vs "They" Researcher external OR part of audience

3. Abercrombie & Longhurst (1998): IS vs DOES

What audience IS vs what it DOES

Applied to three contemporary types:

1. Simple Audience

  • Traditional, least contemporary
  • Clear producer/consumer distinction
  • Public space performance
  • Ceremonial/sacred quality
  • Involvement: HIGH

2. Mass Audience

  • Mass communication/technology driven
  • Local ‚Üí global; public ‚Üí private
  • Less attention to performance
  • Involvement: MEDIUM
  • No spatial localization, less ceremony

3. Diffused Audience ‚ö° MOST IMPORTANT

  • Consumers AND producers come together
  • Part of performance, influences it participatory ways
  • Passive observation ‚Üí active participation
  • "Everyone becomes an audience all the time... constitutive of everyday life"

4. Athique (2016): Transnational Audiences

Globalization = 3 Forms of Movement

  1. Money
  2. People
  3. Media Content

Diasporic Audiences

Connected to minority cultural/linguistic groups and dominating role of mass broadcasters/international media industries

‚ö° Networked Audiences

  • Virtual, non-synchronous consumption
  • Passive ‚Üí active role
  • Shape creation/distribution of media content
  • Device operators interacting with malleable content and each other
  • No physical co-presence or simultaneous activity
  • Inherently "active" proposition

Meaningful today: Connected virtually, strength tangible, shapes media services worldwide

5. AVT Audience-Centred Approaches

‚ö° Gambier's THREE Rs (2018)

  1. Response: Investigated through psychological methodologies
  2. Reaction: Shared knowledge, inferential processing
  3. Repercussion: Effects on receiver(s) and sociocultural context

Variables: Sociological, Technical (AVT constraints)

Features: Textual and paratextual parameters

Kruger & Doherty (2018b)

Psychometric instruments measure:

  • Audience immersion
  • Enjoyment
  • Cognitive load

Includes: Psychological immersion (presence, transportation), perceived cognitive effort, comprehension, recall, learning

Essential: High number of participants to identify regularities

6. Historical Approaches - Two Avenues

Avenue 1: Evolution of studies in multi/interdisciplinary terms (metadiscourse) - more theoretical
Avenue 2: Historical reception of texts over time or in past - focuses on people's reactions

Evolution Timeline (Biltereyst & Meers)

Period Development
Late 1800s Early cinema - "dangers of films"
Pre/Post WWI Social scientists, psychologists (e.g., Emilie Altenloth - gender focus)
1960s Film studies institutionalized
1970s Psychoanalytical approaches - gaze/identification
1980s Feminist theory, sociology, anthropology, pragmatics
Today Varied/rich landscape - multiple approaches needed

Historical AVT Scholars

  • O'Sullivan & Cornu: Cinema history through translation lens
  • Silent era: "Films silent, but not speechless" (title cards, commentators)
  • Talkies: "Ushered multilingualism into cinema worldwide"
  • O'Sullivan (2018): Film paratext - peritext vs epitext (subtitles burned on reel vs multi-faceted options)

‚ö° Retranslation

  • Intrinsic feature of AVT but under-investigated
  • Common: same film subtitled/dubbed multiple times into single language
  • For theatrical exhibition, DVD, TV, restored films
  • Contributes to exploring aggregated audiences
  • Common denominator: archival research

7. Reception Research Tools

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Offline vs Online Measures

Offline Measures

  • Questionnaires and interviews
  • Gauge: comprehension, recall, learning, cognitive load, immersion

Online Measures

  • Eye tracking, electroencephalography (EEG)
  • "Typically more objective and physiological"
  • Respondents cannot control

Perception vs Reception (Di Giovanni 2018b)

  • Perception: Gauged by online measures
  • Reception: Requires both types for complete evaluation

Key Research Examples

Perego et al. (2015): Subtitles vs dubbing - multi-section questionnaire combining cognitive measures (comprehension, dialogue recognition, face-name association, visual scene recognition) + evaluative measures (film appreciation, self-reported effort, judgments of memory)
Szarkowska & Gerber Mor√≥n (2018): Reading speed - 70 participants, 3 languages, questionnaire + eye tracker. Result: No difficulties with fast subtitles, no increased cognitive load. Unexpected: Higher cognitive load for English speakers (limited subtitling exposure)
Louise Fryer: Psychology-based AD research - experimental psychologist. Key contribution: immersion and presence parameters in AVT/media accessibility

Triangulation

Kruger & Doherty (2018b) recommend triangulation of:

  1. Physiological measures
  2. Psychometric measures
  3. Performance measures

8. Future Trends

‚ö° Audiences More Proactive Today

  • Influence production/distribution
  • Criticize via blogs/social media
  • Direct content producers
  • Great dynamism ‚Üí need to reconsider observation tools

Three Innovative Trends

1. Accessible Filmmaking (Romero Fresco 2019)

  • Accessibility = all that allows ANY person to enjoy film
  • Design accessible from FIRST conceptual steps
  • Translation, captions, AD as part of PRODUCTION (not post-production)
  • Redesigns translator space - part of production
  • Films for all - accessible for different abilities
  • Can apply to series, theatre, live shows

2. Accessibility ‚Üí Inclusion ‚ö° KEY SHIFT

  • 'Disability' ‚Üí 'Alternative abilities'
  • Different abilities = essence of TRUE inclusion
  • Leave behind dis-abilities needing access
  • Origins: fansubbing/fandubbing - consumers turned produsers
  • Embraces diffused audiences concept

3. Inclusive Design & Design-for-All

  • People (not services/texts) at core
  • "Provide all people with dignity, comfort, convenience"
  • Allows independence and equal participation

üéØ Central Argument

Media audience complexity requires systematic, interdisciplinary investigation. From traditional simple audiences to contemporary diffused/networked audiences actively shaping content, understanding reception demands:

  • Clear audience definitions
  • Appropriate methodologies (triangulation of offline/online measures)
  • Historical awareness
  • Shift toward inclusive paradigms

Future: Media accessibility has boosted reception studies and is evolving into inclusive design where people with different abilities co-create accessible content from the start, transforming consumers into produsers.
