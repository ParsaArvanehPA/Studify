CHAPTER 4: FROM PARAGRAPH TO ESSAY
Quick Exam Summary

1. PARTS OF AN ESSAY

ESSAY: A piece of writing several paragraphs long about ONE topic. Like a paragraph, but longer and divided into multiple paragraphs.
ESSAY STRUCTURE:

I. INTRODUCTION (1 paragraph)
   - General statements
   - Thesis statement

II. BODY (2+ paragraphs)
   A. Topic sentence + support
   B. Topic sentence + support
   C. Topic sentence + support

III. CONCLUSION (1 paragraph)
   - Summary/restatement
   - Final comment

Paragraph vs. Essay Comparison:

Paragraph Part ‚Üí Essay Part
Topic sentence ‚Üí Thesis statement (in introduction)
Supporting sentences ‚Üí Body paragraphs
Concluding sentence ‚Üí Concluding paragraph

2. ATTENTION-GETTING INTRODUCTION

  • Dramatic/funny story: Tell interesting anecdote
  • Surprising fact/statistic: Share shocking data
  • Historical background: Provide context

3. THE THESIS STATEMENT

THESIS STATEMENT: The most important sentence in the essay. States the specific topic and often the writer's point of view or organization pattern.

Types of Thesis Statements:

Type Example
Basic Young people in my culture have less freedom than young people in the United States.
With Subtopics Listed Young people in my culture have less freedom in their choice of where they live, whom they marry, and what their job is.
With Number of Subtopics There are three main reasons why college tuition is rising.

4. BODY PARAGRAPHS

BODY PARAGRAPHS: Like supporting sentences in a paragraph - they develop your topic and prove your points using facts, examples, quotations, and other details.

Key Points:

  • Each body paragraph develops ONE subtopic
  • Number of body paragraphs = number of subtopics
  • Each paragraph needs a TOPIC SENTENCE
  • Support with examples, facts, statistics, quotations
  • Concluding sentence optional for body paragraphs (but required for conclusion)

5. THE CONCLUSION

Three Purposes:

  1. Signal the end (use transition signal)
  2. Remind reader of main points (summarize OR paraphrase thesis)
  3. Leave final thoughts (memorable message)

Four Techniques for Memorable Conclusions:

Technique Example
Make a Prediction "If this trend continues, fewer families will afford college..."
Suggest Consequences "As a result, the nation will waste the intelligence of a generation..."
Call to Action "We must demand that government increase funding..."
Quote Authority "As Horace Mann argued, 'education is essential for prosperity...'"

6. PATTERNS OF ORGANIZATION

Three Main Patterns:

Pattern Use For Thesis Example
Logical Division of Ideas Dividing topic into categories, reasons, types "There are three types of students..."
Chronological Order Time sequence, processes, narratives "My summer job taught me..." (implies story)
Comparison/Contrast Similarities and/or differences "There are several differences between X and Y."

7. LOGICAL DIVISION OF IDEAS (Most Common Pattern)

Three Key Rules:

  1. Divide topic into subtopics in thesis statement
  2. Discuss each subtopic in separate paragraph
  3. Use transitions between paragraphs

Ways to List Subtopics in Thesis:

Method 1: List them all

"Native Americans contributed to U.S. culture in language, art, food, and government."

Method 2: Give the number

"Native Americans made valuable contributions in four main areas."

Method 3: Use paired conjunctions

"Young people have little freedom not only in lifestyle but also in career choice."

Method 4: Use colon (:)

"The mind has three parts: the id, the ego, and the superego."

IMPORTANT: Subtopics must be in PARALLEL FORM (same grammatical structure)

Transition Signals for Logical Division:

Type Examples
Order words first, second, third, next, last, finally
Addition words also, in addition, moreover, furthermore
Coordinators and, both...and, not only...but also
Others the first reason, another advantage, one problem
