PLAGIARISM: Using someone else's words or ideas as if they were your own, without citing the source. This is a SERIOUS OFFENSE that can result in failing a class or expulsion.

2. CITATIONS

CITATION: Telling your reader where you got information. Citations serve two purposes: avoid plagiarism AND allow readers to find your sources.

Two-Stage Citation Process:

Stage 1: In-Text Citation

Short reference in parentheses at the end of borrowed information

Examples:

‚Ä¢ Without author: "... language preservation is critical ("Disappearing Languages")."

3. QUOTATIONS

Two Types:

Type Definition Example
Direct Quotation Copy exact words + use quotation marks Smith said, "Language is essential to culture" (45).
Indirect Quotation Report what was said without exact words (+ paraphrase) Smith said that language is essential to culture (45).

Reporting Verbs:

Common verbs: assert, claim, comment, declare, insist, maintain, mention, note, report, say, state, suggest, write
Phrase: according to

Punctuating Direct Quotations:

Rule Example
Use quotation marks "Language evolves constantly," the professor noted (15).
Period goes after citation "Language is complex" (Jones 12).
Capitalize first word of quote The author writes, "Many languages are dying."
Use ellipsis (...) for omitted words "Language ... is constantly changing" (Smith 20).
Use [brackets] for added words "It [language] shapes our thoughts."

4. PARAPHRASING

PARAPHRASING: Rewriting information from a source in YOUR OWN WORDS without changing the meaning. Almost as long as the original but uses different vocabulary and sentence structure.

How to Paraphrase (5 Steps):

  1. Read the original several times until you fully understand it
  2. Take notes - write only key words, not complete sentences
  3. Write from your notes WITHOUT looking at the original
  4. Check your paraphrase against original:
    • Different words? ‚úì
    • Different sentence structure? ‚úì
    • Same meaning? ‚úì
  5. Add in-text citation

Paraphrase vs. Plagiarism:

‚úó TWO TYPES OF PLAGIARISM:
1. No citation (even if words are changed)
2. Too similar to original (even with citation)
Original Language is the main means of communication between peoples.
‚úó Plagiarism Language is the principle means of communication between peoples. Only 1 word changed!
‚úì Good Paraphrase Humans communicate through language (Kispert). Different words + structure

5. SUMMARIZING

SUMMARY: Condensed version of a source that includes ONLY the main points. Much SHORTER than the original (unlike paraphrase).

Summary vs. Paraphrase:

Paraphrase Summary
Length Almost as long as original Much shorter than original
Details Includes most details Only main points
Words Your own words Your own words
Citation Required Required

How to Summarize (6 Steps):

  1. Read original several times
  2. Underline most important points only
  3. Take notes - few words for each main idea
  4. Write from notes without looking at original
  5. Check that you haven't changed the meaning
  6. Add in-text citation
Original (85 words): "Language is the main means of communication between peoples. But so many different languages have developed that language has often been a barrier rather than an aid..."

Summary (30 words): People communicate through language; however, having different languages creates communication barriers. A universal language could bring countries together culturally and economically (Kispert).

6. CHANGING DIRECT TO INDIRECT QUOTATIONS

Sequence of Tenses Rules:

Direct Quote ‚Üí Indirect Quote
Simple present ‚Üí Simple past
"The exam is at 8." ‚Üí She said the exam was at 8.
Present perfect / Simple past ‚Üí Past perfect
"We have eaten." ‚Üí They said they had eaten.
Will / Can / May / Must ‚Üí Would / Could / Might / Had to
"I will go." ‚Üí He said he would go.

Steps to Convert:

1. Remove quotation marks
2. Add subordinator "that" (optional)
3. Change verb tense (if needed)
4. Change pronouns/time words
Direct: Maria said, "I am studying now."
Indirect: Maria said (that) she was studying then.
