1. INTRODUCTION: WHAT IS A PARAGRAPH?

PARAGRAPH: A group of related sentences that discusses one (and usually only one) main idea.

Key Characteristics

  • Length: Can be as short as one sentence or as long as ten sentences or more
  • Number of sentences is unimportant
  • Must be long enough to develop the main idea clearly
  • All sentences must be related to the main idea

3. TOPIC SENTENCES

TOPIC SENTENCE: The most general statement in a paragraph that names the topic and the controlling idea.

Two Essential Parts

1. TOPIC

  • Names the subject of the paragraph
  • Answers: "What is this paragraph about?"

2. CONTROLLING IDEA

  • Limits or controls the topic to a specific area
  • Narrows the topic to something discussable in one paragraph
  • Provides focus and direction

Formula

TOPIC + CONTROLLING IDEA = TOPIC SENTENCE

Example:

Convenience foods + are easy to prepare

TOPIC + CONTROLLING IDEA

Examples of Topic Sentences

Topic Sentence Topic Controlling Idea
Driving on freeways requires skill and alertness. Driving on freeways requires skill and alertness
Immigrants have contributed many delicious foods to U.S. cuisine. Immigrants have contributed many delicious foods to U.S. cuisine
The Caribbean island of Trinidad attracts tourists because of its calypso music. The Caribbean island of Trinidad attracts tourists because of its calypso music

Position of Topic Sentences

‚úì Most Common: Beginning of Paragraph

  • Best spot for topic sentences
  • Readers expect to know what they'll read about immediately
  • Follows English writing conventions

Less Common: End of Paragraph

  • Paragraph begins with examples or facts
  • Topic sentence at end = conclusion drawn from examples
Example Patterns:

Beginning Position:
‚Üí TOPIC SENTENCE (main idea stated)
‚Üí Supporting detail 1
‚Üí Supporting detail 2
‚Üí Concluding sentence

End Position:
‚Üí Example/Fact 1
‚Üí Example/Fact 2
‚Üí Example/Fact 3
‚Üí TOPIC SENTENCE (conclusion)

Common Problems with Topic Sentences

‚úó Problem 1: TOO GENERAL

Example: "English has been influenced by other languages."

Issue: Doesn't provide enough guidance about paragraph content

Solution: Add specific controlling idea

‚úó Problem 2: TOO SPECIFIC

Example: "The slang expression so long (meaning 'good-bye') is probably a corruption of the Arabic salaam."

Issue: This is a supporting detail, not a main idea

Solution: Make it broader to cover multiple examples

‚úó Problem 3: INCOMPLETE

Example: "The Arabic origin of many English words."

Issue: Not a complete sentence

Solution: Make it a complete statement

‚úó Problem 4: UNRELATED IDEAS

Example: "Advanced communication technologies have improved dramatically, and they have also enhanced business relationships and nurtured social interactions."

Issue: Three unrelated controlling ideas (requires 3 separate paragraphs)

Solution: Focus on ONE controlling idea per paragraph

‚úì Well-Written Topic Sentence

Example: "People hold many superstitious beliefs about the moon."

  • Complete sentence ‚úì
  • Clear topic (superstitious beliefs about the moon) ‚úì
  • One controlling idea ‚úì
  • Not too general, not too specific ‚úì

4. SUPPORTING SENTENCES

SUPPORTING SENTENCES: Explain or prove the topic sentence with specific details.

Critical Rule

‚ö†Ô∏è Biggest problem in student writing: Failure to support ideas adequately

‚úì Solution: Use specific details to be thorough and convincing

Types of Supporting Details

1. EXAMPLES

  • Easiest to use (from personal knowledge/experience)
  • Make writing lively and interesting
  • Help readers remember your points

Signal Words for Examples: for example, for instance, such as

Example: "For example, a business executive might tell her coworker that his ideas are terrible. Then she might interrupt him when he tries to explain his ideas."

2. FACTS AND STATISTICS

  • Objective statements of truth
  • Provide concrete evidence
  • Increase credibility
Example: "In 2009, almost 1,000 automobile deaths occurred in the United States alone because drivers were distracted by cellular phones."

3. QUOTATIONS

  • Expert opinions
  • Research findings
  • Authoritative statements
Example: "If you put 20-year-old drivers behind the wheel with a cell phone, their reaction times are the same as 70-year-old drivers who are not using a cell phone," said University of Utah psychology professor David Strayer.

Opinions vs. Facts

OPINIONS (Not Acceptable as Support)

Definition: Subjective statements based on personal beliefs or attitudes

Examples:
  • ‚úó "Women are better communicators than men."
  • ‚úó "Smoking is a bad habit."
  • ‚úó "English is an easy language to learn."

Rule: You CAN express opinions, but you MUST support them with facts.

FACTS (Acceptable as Support)

Definition: Objective statements of truth

Examples:
  • ‚úì "At sea level, water boils at 100 degrees Celsius."
  • ‚úì "Women live longer than men." (needs proof)
  • ‚úì "Cigarettes are addictive." (needs proof)

Opinion vs. Fact Comparison

Category Example Status
OPINION Photographs of ultrathin fashion models send the wrong message to girls and young women. Needs factual support
FACT (but needs proof) Fashion models are extremely thin. Needs specific evidence
SPECIFIC SUPPORTING DETAIL The average model weighs 23 percent less than the average woman of the same height. Can be used as support ‚úì

Labeling System for Support

  • O = Opinion (needs factual support)
  • F-NP = Fact that Needs Proof (must provide evidence)
  • SSD = Specific Supporting Detail (ready to use)

5. CONCLUDING SENTENCES

CONCLUDING SENTENCE: Serves two purposes - signals the end of the paragraph and reminds the reader of important ideas.

Two Methods

Method 1: SUMMARIZE MAIN POINTS

  • Review key supporting details
  • Helpful when multiple points were discussed
Example: "These examples clearly show that these days there is a greeting card for every possible life event and for a few nonevents as well."

Method 2: PARAPHRASE TOPIC SENTENCE

  • Repeat the main idea in different words
  • Helpful for simpler paragraphs
Topic Sentence: "Native people create legends to explain unusual phenomena in their environment."
Concluding Sentence: "This story is a good example of a legend invented by native people to interpret the world around them."

When to Use Concluding Sentences

‚úì Use for:

  • Single paragraphs
  • Long paragraphs
  • Paragraphs with multiple supporting points

Not always needed for:

  • Paragraphs within multi-paragraph essays
  • Very short paragraphs

6. VOCABULARY: WORD FAMILIES

WORD FAMILIES: Groups of related words with similar meanings but different parts of speech.

Benefits

  • Quickly improve vocabulary
  • Understand word relationships
  • Use correct word forms in writing

Example Word Family: SUCCESS

Part of Speech Word Example Sentence
Noun success Her success in business was remarkable.
Verb succeed She will succeed in her goals.
Adjective successful He is a successful entrepreneur.
Adverb successfully They completed the project successfully.

Common Word Endings by Part of Speech

Part of Speech Common Endings Examples
Verbs -ate communicate, dominate, indicate
Adjectives -ive, -ful, -al aggressive, passive, successful
Adverbs -ly aggressively, passively, successfully

7. THE WRITING PROCESS (6 STEPS)

Overview: Writing is a process consisting of a series of steps. Follow these six steps for successful paragraph writing.

STEP 1: PREWRITE TO GET IDEAS

Purpose: Choose a topic and generate ideas

Technique: LISTING (Brainstorming)

  • Write topic at top of paper
  • List everything that comes to mind
  • Don't worry about spelling/grammar
  • Don't worry if some ideas are off-topic
  • Work quickly and freely
Example Listing:

COMMUNICATION
- many different ways
- letters‚Äîwrite grandma
- different purposes?
- problems communicating?
- texting‚Äîtoo much! addiction?
- smartphones
- talking‚Äîfun, face to face, friends

STEP 2: ORGANIZE YOUR IDEAS

Tasks:

  1. Look for related points in your list
  2. Cross out anything off-topic or repeated
  3. Group similar ideas together
  4. Narrow topic to fit one paragraph
  5. Choose ONE main idea to focus on
  6. Select related supporting points

Result: A focused plan for your paragraph

STEP 3: WRITE THE FIRST DRAFT

Focus: Making ideas clear

Include:

  • Topic sentence
  • Supporting sentences (examples/facts)
  • Concluding sentence

Don't Worry About:

  • Grammar
  • Punctuation
  • Spelling
  • Perfection

Important: You can add/delete ideas while writing‚Äîjust ensure they support your topic

STEP 4: REVISE THE DRAFT

Focus: Content and organization (NOT grammar/mechanics yet)

Process:

  1. Read paragraph for general overview
  2. Check that paragraph has topic sentence
  3. Verify topic is developed with enough support
  4. Confirm opinions are supported with facts
  5. Ensure concluding sentence is on topic
  6. Make notes in margin about improvements

Peer Review:

  • Have a classmate read your draft
  • Get feedback using peer review form
  • Discuss suggestions
  • Decide which changes to make

STEP 5: EDIT AND PROOFREAD

Focus: Format, mechanics, grammar, sentence structure

Check For:

  • Format: Proper paragraph structure
  • Mechanics: Capitalization, punctuation, spelling
  • Grammar: Verb tenses, subject-verb agreement, etc.
  • Sentence Structure: Complete sentences, variety

Method:

  • Use a checklist (Writer's Self-Check)
  • Mark errors with correction symbols
  • Note all needed corrections

STEP 6: WRITE A NEW DRAFT

Tasks:

  1. Include ALL changes from Steps 4 and 5
  2. Proofread again carefully
  3. Ensure draft is neat and error-free
  4. Submit to instructor
Remember: Writing involves continuous writing, revising, and rewriting until satisfied with final product.

Memory Aid: The Hamburger Analogy

Think of a paragraph like a hamburger:

üçî TOP BUN = Topic Sentence
   (Holds everything together, tells you what you're getting)

ü•¨ LETTUCE, TOMATO, CHEESE = Supporting Sentences
   (The substance, the details, the proof)

üçî BOTTOM BUN = Concluding Sentence
   (Closes everything up, leaves you satisfied)
Example:
  • Top Bun: "Cell phones pose serious safety risks while driving."
  • Fillings: Statistics about accidents + Expert quote + Example of distracted driver
  • Bottom Bun: "These facts clearly demonstrate the dangers of using cell phones on the road."

The "SPECS" Method for Topic Sentences

Remember a good topic sentence has SPECS:

  • Specific enough (not too broad)
  • Provides direction (controlling idea)
  • Expressible in one sentence
  • Clear and complete
  • Single focus (one main idea)

The 3-2-1 Support Rule

For adequate support in an exam paragraph, aim for:

  • 3 types of support (example + fact/statistic + quotation/additional example)
  • 2 sentences minimum per supporting point
  • 1 clear connection back to the topic sentence
Example Application:

Topic: "Social media has changed how young people communicate."

Support Type 1 (Example):
‚Ä¢ For instance, teenagers now prefer sending Snapchat messages over making phone calls.
‚Ä¢ This shift shows a preference for visual, brief communication.

Support Type 2 (Statistic):
‚Ä¢ According to a 2020 study, 73% of teens use social media daily for interpersonal communication.
‚Ä¢ This represents a dramatic increase from just 45% in 2010.

Support Type 3 (Expert Quote):
‚Ä¢ Communication expert Dr. Jane Smith notes that "digital platforms have fundamentally altered youth interaction patterns."
‚Ä¢ This expert opinion confirms the transformative impact of social media.

Common Student Errors (and How to Avoid Them)

‚úó ERROR #1: The "List" Paragraph

Problem: Presenting only a list of points without development

Wrong:
"There are many types of communication. First, there is verbal communication. Second, there is written communication. Third, there is nonverbal communication. Finally, there is digital communication."
Right:
"There are many types of communication, each serving different purposes. Verbal communication, for example, allows for immediate feedback and emotional nuance through tone of voice. During a job interview, a candidate's confident tone can be as important as the words themselves..."

Lesson: DEVELOP each point with specific details, don't just list them!

‚úó ERROR #2: The "New Idea" Conclusion

Problem: Introducing fresh information in the concluding sentence

Wrong:
Topic: Benefits of exercise
Conclusion: "Therefore, exercise offers many health benefits, and it's also quite affordable compared to medical treatments."
(Affordability is a NEW idea!)
Right:
"Therefore, these health benefits demonstrate why regular exercise is essential for well-being."

Lesson: Concluding sentence = summary or paraphrase, NOT new information!

‚úó ERROR #3: The "Drifting" Paragraph

Problem: Supporting sentences that drift away from the controlling idea

Example:
Topic Sentence: "Texting while driving is dangerous."
Drift: "...Additionally, texting plans can be expensive, with some carriers charging high rates for unlimited messaging. Many people spend too much money on their phone bills..."
(This drifted to costs, not danger!)

Lesson: EVERY supporting sentence must directly relate to your controlling idea!

Exam Strategy: The 5-Minute Topic Sentence Test

Before writing your full paragraph in an exam, spend 5 minutes perfecting your topic sentence:

Step Time Action
1 1 minute Write a draft topic sentence
2 1 minute Circle the topic, underline the controlling idea
3 1 minute Ask: Too general? Too specific? Complete? One focus?
4 1 minute Revise based on your answers
5 1 minute Final check with SPECS method

Why this works: A strong topic sentence guides your entire paragraph. Investing time here saves time later!

The "TREES" Method for Supporting Sentences

Build strong support with TREES:

  • Topic connection (Does it relate to your topic sentence?)
  • Relevant details (Is the information pertinent and useful?)
  • Examples or evidence (Do you have concrete proof?)
  • Explanation (Have you explained WHY/HOW this supports your point?)
  • Specific (Is it detailed enough, not vague?)

Final Exam Tips

  1. Start Strong: Your topic sentence is worth spending extra time on‚Äîit guides everything else.
  2. Support Generously: When in doubt, add MORE specific details. Examiners want thorough development.
  3. Use Signal Words: They make your paragraph easier to follow and show you understand paragraph structure.
  4. Don't Introduce New Ideas at the End: Probably the #1 mistake students make in conclusions.
  5. Save Time for Proofreading: Even 3 minutes can catch major errors that hurt your score.
  6. Practice the 30-Minute Format: Time yourself regularly so you develop a natural rhythm.
  7. Read Good Examples: The more well-written paragraphs you read, the better your own writing becomes.
