Chapter 1: Background & Theory

Definition & Core Concepts

Question 1

Define Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) and explain its main objectives.

Answer

Definition: Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) is the systematic study of how language is used to persuade, manipulate, and control individuals and social groups. It focuses on language as a controlling force in society and its role in maintaining or changing social structures.

Main Objectives:

  • Practical: Analyze discourse practices that reflect/construct social problems; investigate how ideologies become "frozen" in language; increase awareness to address injustice and power misuse
  • Theoretical: Demonstrate language's significance in power relations; investigate how meaning is created in context; examine the role of speaker/writer purpose and authorial stance

Key Goals: Develop awareness of language and ideology; understand how discourse controls society; question conventional discourses; work towards social justice.

Question 2

What is the difference between "critical" in CDA and "critical" in everyday usage? Why is this distinction important?

Answer

Everyday usage: "Critical" often means negative evaluation or fault-finding.

In CDA: "Critical" means critique — systematic analysis that can be positive OR negative. For example, CDA can analyze successful resistance movements (positive) just as well as oppressive propaganda (negative).

Why important: This distinction is crucial because CDA is not about negativity — it's about examining underlying structures, assumptions, and power relations in ANY discourse. The goal is understanding and awareness, not judgment.

Question 3

List and explain the six different meanings of the term "discourse."

Answer

# Meaning Explanation
1 Broad All symbolic interaction including spoken, written, visual communication (films, music, gestures)
2 Spoken Simply spoken interaction, as opposed to written
3 vs. Text The whole act of communication including both production and comprehension
4 Institutional Communication in specific contexts (legal discourse, medical discourse, scientific discourse)
5 A discourse A particular long text like a lecture or sermon (NOT commonly used in CDA)
6 Multi-modal Uses more than one mode (words + pictures + music together)

Question 4

What are the Seven Standards of Textuality proposed by Beaugrande & Dressler? Explain each briefly.

Answer

  1. Cohesion: How words and sentences connect in sequence through grammatical links (pronouns, conjunctions, etc.)
  2. Coherence: Cognitive relations, logical connections, and mutual knowledge that make a text "make sense"
  3. Acceptability: Appropriateness to the cultural setting and audience expectations
  4. Intentionality: The producer's purpose and goals in creating the text
  5. Informativity: Suitable balance of new information vs. given information
  6. Situationality: Relevance to the specific situation in which text is used
  7. Intertextuality: Reliance on and reference to previous texts

Question 5

Compare and contrast "Speech Community" and "Discourse Community."

Answer

Speech Community Discourse Community
Share language features (dialect, accent) May never meet face-to-face
Frequently interact in person Conscious, chosen participation
Usually accident of birth/location Deliberate membership based on interest
Example: Geordie dialect speakers in Newcastle Example: Medical researchers worldwide, online gaming communities

Key difference: Speech communities are formed by geography/birth; discourse communities are formed by shared purpose/interest.

Question 6

Define "ideology" and "frames" in the context of CDA. How are they related?

Answer

Ideology: A set of beliefs and attitudes shared by a social group, often unconscious and taken for granted. Ideologies can be hidden in single words (e.g., "democracy," "freedom," "collateral damage").

Frames: Mental structures that shape how we see the world (concept from George Lakoff). Frames are triggered by words and are culture-specific cognitive models. Example: "cricket" triggers a frame including pitch, bat, ball, umpire, wicket.

Relationship: Frames are the building blocks of ideology. When certain frames are repeatedly activated (e.g., "immigrants as flood/invasion"), they shape ideological worldviews. Ideologies are maintained through the consistent use of certain frames.

Question 7

What does "making strange" mean in CDA? Why is it an important analytical skill?

Answer

Definition: "Making strange" is the practice of observing familiar customs as an outsider would — stepping back from normalized practices to see them with fresh eyes.

Importance:

  • Helps identify practices that have become so familiar they seem "natural"
  • Reveals hidden power relations in everyday discourse
  • Enables denormalization of problematic practices

Example: Traditional marriage vows required women to "obey and serve" — this was so normalized it seemed unremarkable. "Making strange" revealed the gender inequality, leading to change.

Chapter 2: Discourse & Social Context

Context & Meaning

Question 8

Explain the relationship between sign, context, and meaning. Provide examples.

Answer

Core principle: Meaning is created when a sign occurs in a specific context. The same sign can have completely different meanings in different contexts.

Examples:

  • Red flag on beach: Danger/no swimming
  • Red flag at demonstration: Political symbol (socialism)
  • Cross (X) on student work: Error/incorrect
  • Cross (X) on ballot: Selection/vote

Key insight: Signs don't carry fixed meanings — context determines meaning.

Question 9

What are performatives? What conditions must be met for them to work?

Answer

Definition: Performatives are utterances that IS an act of doing something — the words themselves perform the action simply by being spoken.

Examples: "I promise...", "I bet...", "I pronounce you husband and wife", "I sentence you to..."

Conditions (Felicity Conditions):

  • The speaker must have the authority to perform the act
  • The circumstances must be appropriate
  • The proper procedures must be followed

Example of failure: "I pronounce you husband and wife" said by an actor in a play does NOT perform a marriage because the conditions aren't met.

Question 10

Differentiate between meaning, sense, and reference. Why is this distinction important for CDA?

Answer

Term Definition Example with "dog"
Meaning Dictionary definition (context-free) Four-legged domesticated animal
Sense Full discoursal meaning in context "What a dog!" (insult about a person)
Reference Who/what is actually referred to "The dog" = that specific animal there

Importance for CDA: CDA focuses on sense because the same words with the same dictionary meaning can carry very different ideological messages in context. Example: "terrorist" vs. "freedom fighter" might refer to the same group but carry opposite senses.

Question 11

List and explain the seven types of Socially Shared Knowledge (Mutual Knowledge).

Answer

  1. Subject matter facts: Knowledge about the topic being discussed
  2. Cultural knowledge: Festivals, customs, legal system, historical events
  3. Social roles & hierarchies: How people are expected to behave in different roles
  4. Institutional practices: Workplace norms, school procedures, genre conventions
  5. Moral values: What the group considers right/wrong, appropriate/inappropriate
  6. Co-text & context: What has already been said in this specific event
  7. Individual participants: Knowledge about the specific people involved

CDA insight: All these categories incorporate issues of identity and power. When shared knowledge includes biased assumptions, those biases become invisible "common sense."

Grice's Co-operative Principle

Question 12

State Grice's Co-operative Principle and list the four maxims with explanations.

Answer

Co-operative Principle: "Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."

The Four Maxims:

Maxim Rule Details
Quantity Say enough, not too much Be as informative as required; don't be more informative than required
Quality Tell the truth Don't say what you believe false; don't say what lacks evidence
Relation Be relevant Make contributions relevant to current exchange
Manner Be clear Avoid obscurity; avoid ambiguity; be brief; be orderly

Question 13

What is the difference between violating and flouting a maxim? Give examples of each.

Answer

Violating Flouting
Deliberately breaking the maxim Blatantly and obviously failing to fulfill it
Intent: To deceive or mislead Intent: To trigger implicature
The hearer doesn't notice the breach The hearer notices and draws conclusions
Example: Lying about your whereabouts Example: Sarcasm, irony, exaggeration

Flouting example: A book review saying a footballer's autobiography is "equal to Shakespeare's first folio" — obviously untrue, so readers understand it as mockery (flouting Quality for sarcasm).

Question 14

What is implicature? How does it rely on the Co-operative Principle?

Answer

Implicature: Meaning that is evident although not explicitly stated — "reading between the lines."

How it works: Implicature relies on the assumption that speakers are following the Co-operative Principle. When something seems to violate a maxim, we look for an implied meaning that makes the utterance cooperative.

Example:

  • A: "Is John a good student?"
  • B: "He attends class regularly."
  • Implicature: B didn't directly answer, only mentioning attendance. We infer that's all B can positively say — implying John is NOT particularly good academically.

Question 15

Explain the difference between "Context of Culture" and "Context of Situation."

Answer

Context of Culture Context of Situation
Traditions of the community Setting (physical/social environment)
Institutions Identity of participants
Discourse communities Specific instance of communication
Historical context Power relationships present
Under continuous long-term change Changes with each contribution

Key insight: Context is dynamic, not static. Language use is PART OF context — language and context shape each other.

Chapter 3: Positioning & Point of View

Stance & Authorial Position

Question 16

What is "stance" in CDA? Describe the three types of stance with examples.

Answer

Stance: The authorial position taken in a text, reflecting attitude toward subject matter and audience. Can be conscious or unconscious.

Type Description Examples
Conscious-Explicit Deliberate positioning, openly revealed Film reviews, opinion pieces, critical essays
Conscious-Hidden Deliberate positioning, concealed Advertising, promotional letters, propaganda
Unconscious Unaware positioning, reveals ideology Racist/sexist discourse, biased reporting

Question 17

What is "synthetic personalization" (Fairclough)? How is it used in promotional texts?

Answer

Synthetic personalization: The practice of mass communication disguising itself as personal communication — a letter sent to millions is written as if it's just for you.

Techniques used:

  • Simulated friendship: First names, informal tone
  • Pseudo-intimacy: "We know you...", "You deserve..."
  • False scarcity: "Limited time", "Only for you"
  • Flattery: "As a valued customer..."
  • Positive presupposition: "When you order..." (not "if")

Purpose: Create trust and intimacy to manipulate the reader while hiding commercial intent.

Question 18

Explain Van Dijk's "Ideological Square." How does it appear in discriminatory discourse?

Answer

The Ideological Square: A pattern identified by Van Dijk in ideologically biased discourse:

  1. Emphasize OUR good qualities
  2. Emphasize THEIR bad qualities
  3. Mitigate (downplay) OUR bad qualities
  4. Mitigate (downplay) THEIR good qualities

Example in racist discourse:

  • "We are hardworking and law-abiding" (emphasize our good)
  • "They are criminals and freeloaders" (emphasize their bad)
  • "Our mistakes are understandable" (mitigate our bad)
  • "Even when they succeed, it's through unfair advantage" (mitigate their good)

Question 19

List five markers of sexist discourse and explain how each functions.

Answer

  1. Generic Masculine: Using "he/him" as universal ("Each student should bring his book") — excludes women linguistically
  2. Asymmetric Address: "Mr. Smith and Janet" — unequal naming conventions
  3. Semantic Derogation: Female terms acquire negative connotations (master/mistress, bachelor/spinster)
  4. Role Stereotyping: "Female doctor", "male nurse" — marking non-stereotypical roles as unusual
  5. Appearance Focus: Describing women's looks when irrelevant ("The attractive scientist...")

Question 20

How do passive voice and nominalization hide agency in texts? Provide examples.

Answer

Passive Voice: Removes the agent from the sentence.

  • Active: "Police shot protesters" — clear who did the shooting
  • Passive: "Protesters were shot" — who shot them? Hidden.

Nominalization: Turns actions into abstract nouns, obscuring actors.

  • Verb form: "Colonial forces killed indigenous people"
  • Nominalized: "The colonization led to population decline" — who colonized? Who died? How?

Effect: Both techniques obscure responsibility. Events appear to happen without anyone causing them. This is common in political and historical discourse to avoid assigning blame.

Analysis Practice

Question 21 (Analysis Practice)

Analyze the following text for stance and ideological positioning:

"We must protect our borders from those who would destroy our way of life."

Answer

Pronoun Analysis:

  • "We" — inclusive of citizens, creates in-group
  • "Our borders/our way of life" — possessive, claims ownership
  • "Those who" — vague othering, enemy unspecified

Modality: "Must" — strong obligation, no alternative presented

Presuppositions:

  • There IS a threat (presupposed, not proven)
  • There IS a unified "way of life" (presupposes homogeneous culture)
  • "They" want to "destroy" (attributes intent)

Stance: Conscious-explicit nationalist positioning with us/them construction. Creates fear and solidarity simultaneously.

Question 22 (Analysis Practice)

Compare these two ways of describing the same event:

A: "Australia was discovered by Captain Cook in 1770."

B: "British forces invaded Aboriginal lands in 1770."

What does each framing reveal about ideological stance?

Answer

Version A Analysis:

  • "Discovered" — implies land was empty, unknown
  • Ignores 60,000+ years of indigenous presence
  • Stance: Eurocentric, colonial perspective
  • Effect: Legitimizes colonization, erases indigenous history

Version B Analysis:

  • "Invaded" — implies prior occupation, violence
  • "Aboriginal lands" — acknowledges indigenous ownership
  • Stance: Indigenous perspective, anti-colonial
  • Effect: Challenges colonial narrative, restores agency

Key insight: Same event, completely different framing. Lexical choice reveals ideological stance. Neither is "neutral" — both carry ideology.

Summary Questions

Question 23

What analytical framework would you use to identify authorial stance in a text? List at least seven elements to examine.

Answer

Nine-Point Stance Analysis Framework:

  1. Pronoun Use: Who is "we"? Who is "they"? Inclusive or exclusive?
  2. Modality: Certainty markers (must, will, should, might, could)
  3. Evaluative Language: Adjectives and adverbs expressing judgment
  4. Presuppositions: What is assumed as shared knowledge?
  5. Voice: Active (shows agency) vs. Passive (hides agency)
  6. Lexical Choices: Word selection reveals ideology (terrorist vs. freedom fighter)
  7. Metaphors: Conceptual frameworks used (flood, invasion, disease)
  8. What's Absent: Whose voices are silenced? What's not said?
  9. Genre Expectations: How does text conform to or deviate from norms?

Exam Tips

How to Answer CDA Exam Questions

  1. Define terms precisely: Use the technical vocabulary from the course
  2. Always give examples: Abstract concepts need concrete illustrations
  3. Connect to power: CDA is fundamentally about power relations
  4. Quote from texts: When analyzing, cite specific words/phrases
  5. Consider what's absent: What's NOT said is often as important as what IS said
  6. Reference theorists: Van Dijk, Fairclough, Grice, Lakoff — mention them when relevant
  7. Avoid being judgmental: CDA is about analysis, not personal opinion
