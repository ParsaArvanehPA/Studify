Session 3: Positioning and Point of View

Critical Discourse Analysis | Chapter 3 - Stance and Authorial Position
Conscious and Unconscious Positioning in Texts
Key Focus of This Chapter: Understanding how stance (authorial position) can be conscious (explicit or hidden) or unconscious. Learning to identify authorial positioning in different text types: reviews, promotional texts, racist/sexist discourse, and historical writing.

1. Position and Attitude (Stance)

What is Stance?

Stance (also called authorial position or point of view) is the position taken by a writer or speaker in a text. It reflects their attitude toward the subject matter and the audience.

Stance can be:

  • Conscious — the author deliberately positions themselves
  • Unconscious — the author is unaware of the position they're expressing

1.1 Three Types of Stance

Type of Stance Description Text Examples
Conscious - Explicit Deliberate positioning, openly revealed to reader Film/book reviews, opinion pieces, critical essays
Conscious - Hidden Deliberate positioning, concealed from reader Promotional letters, advertisements, propaganda
Unconscious Unaware positioning, reveals ideology/worldview Racist/sexist discourse, biased reporting, textbooks
Why Does This Matter?

Understanding stance is crucial for CDA because:

  1. Explicit stance is transparent: We know the reviewer is giving their opinion. We can agree or disagree.
  2. Hidden stance is manipulative: The advertiser pretends to be helpful while trying to sell us something.
  3. Unconscious stance is ideological: The writer doesn't even realize they're expressing a biased worldview.

CDA's task: Expose hidden and unconscious stances, making visible what would otherwise remain invisible.

2. Film and Book Reviews (Conscious - Explicit)

Key Feature

In reviews, writers openly express subjective opinions using evaluative language and first-person pronouns. The reader knows they are reading someone's opinion.

2.1 Linguistic Features of Reviews

Feature Purpose Examples
First-person pronouns Show personal involvement "I found...", "We believe...", "In my opinion..."
Evaluative adjectives Express judgment Brilliant, tedious, compelling, disappointing, masterful
Modality Hedge or strengthen claims "You should see this", "You might enjoy", "You must watch"
Direct address Engage reader "You will love...", "Don't miss...", "If you enjoy..."
Presupposition Assume shared values "As we all know...", "Obviously...", "Naturally..."
Example Analysis:

Text: "This film is undoubtedly one of the worst I've seen this year."

Analysis:

  • "undoubtedly" = intensifier (strengthens claim)
  • "one of the worst" = evaluative phrase (negative judgment)
  • "I've seen" = first-person (personal experience)
  • Result: Explicit negative stance — reader knows this is the reviewer's opinion

3. Promotional Letters (Conscious - Hidden)

Key Feature

In promotional texts, writers deliberately hide their persuasive intent while positioning products/services favorably. They manufacture intimacy and trust to manipulate the reader.

3.1 Deceptive Strategies in Promotional Texts

Six Key Manipulation Techniques

  1. Simulated Friendship: Use of first names, informal tone
  2. Synthetic Personalization: "Dear [Name]" though mass-produced
  3. Pseudo-Intimacy: "We know you..." / "You deserve..."
  4. False Scarcity: "Limited time offer" / "Only for you"
  5. Authority Claims: "Experts recommend..." / "Studies show..."
  6. Flattery: "As a valued customer..." / "Sophisticated buyers like you..."
Technique Linguistic Marker Hidden Purpose
Inclusive "we" "We're offering you..." Create false solidarity between company and customer
Second-person focus "You'll love...", "Your needs..." Simulate personal attention (it's mass-produced)
Positive presupposition "When you order..." (not "if") Assume compliance before it happens
Imperative softening "Why not try...", "Just send..." Disguise commands as friendly suggestions
Synthetic Personalization (Fairclough): The practice of mass communication disguising itself as personal communication. A letter sent to millions is written as if it's just for you.

Case Study: Promotional Letter Analysis

Text: "Dear Sarah, As one of our most valued customers, we wanted you to be the first to know about this exclusive opportunity..."

Analysis:

  • "Dear Sarah" — synthetic personalization (auto-generated)
  • "most valued customers" — flattery (everyone gets this letter)
  • "we wanted you" — simulated care (marketing department)
  • "first to know" — false exclusivity (mass mailing)
  • "exclusive opportunity" — false scarcity (standard offer)

Hidden Stance: The writer positions themselves as a caring friend while actually trying to sell something. The commercial intent is concealed behind pseudo-intimacy.

Spotting Hidden Stance

How to identify conscious-hidden positioning:

Ask these questions:

  1. What is the writer trying to GET from the reader? (money, vote, action)
  2. How is this goal disguised? (as advice, information, friendship)
  3. What relationship is being manufactured? (friend, expert, ally)
  4. What would the text look like if the writer was honest about their purpose?

Red flags for hidden commercial intent:

  • Excessive use of "you" and "your"
  • Claims of exclusivity or urgency
  • Flattery without evidence
  • Assumed intimacy with a stranger
  • Questions that presuppose positive answers

4. Racist and Sexist Discourse (Unconscious Stance)

What is Unconscious Stance?

Unconscious stance occurs when writers are unaware they're expressing a discriminatory worldview. It reveals naturalized ideology through language choices that the writer considers "normal" or "neutral."

4.1 Racist Discourse Markers

Feature Function Example
Othering pronouns Create us/them divide "They are taking our jobs"
Nominalization Obscure agency "The immigration problem" (who says it's a problem?)
Generalizations Stereotype groups "Those people always..."
Metaphors Dehumanize "Flood of refugees", "swarm of migrants", "invasion"
Presupposition Naturalize bias "Even the good ones..." (presumes most are bad)

Van Dijk's Ideological Square

Teun van Dijk identified a pattern in ideologically biased discourse:

  1. Emphasize OUR good qualities
  2. Emphasize THEIR bad qualities
  3. Mitigate OUR bad qualities
  4. Mitigate THEIR good qualities

This "ideological square" operates in racist, nationalist, and other discriminatory discourse.

4.2 Sexist Discourse Markers

Key Features of Sexist Language

  • Generic Masculine: Using "he/him" as universal (excludes women)
  • Asymmetric Address: "Mr. Smith and Janet" (unequal naming)
  • Semantic Derogation: Negative connotations for female terms (master vs. mistress)
  • Role Stereotyping: "Female doctor", "male nurse" (marked forms)
  • Trivialization: Diminutives (lady doctor, career girl)
  • Appearance Focus: Describing women's looks when irrelevant
Example of Sexist Discourse:

Text: "The politician and his attractive wife arrived at the event."

Analysis:

  • "The politician" — man defined by his role/profession
  • "his attractive wife" — woman defined by appearance AND possession ("his")
  • Implication: Man has public identity; woman's identity is through relationship to man and physical appearance

Compare: "The politician and her supportive husband arrived" — this would feel "marked" to many readers, revealing the hidden expectation that politicians are male.

Case Study: Newspaper Headlines

Text A: "Mother of three wins Nobel Prize"

Text B: "Scientist wins Nobel Prize"

Analysis:

  • Text A defines the woman by her family role, not her professional achievement
  • Would we see "Father of three wins Nobel Prize" for a male scientist?
  • The "mother" frame reduces her identity to domestic sphere
  • This is unconscious sexism — the writer probably thought they were being "human interest"
Identifying Unconscious Bias

The key test for unconscious bias:

Would this be written differently for a different group?

  • If describing a male professional, would appearance be mentioned?
  • If describing a native citizen, would "loyalty" be questioned?
  • If describing the dominant group, would group membership be stated?

Example Analysis:

"The female CEO announced record profits" vs. "The CEO announced record profits"

  • Why is "female" mentioned? Because male is the unmarked default
  • This marking makes women in leadership seem exceptional/unusual
  • The writer probably didn't intend sexism — but the language perpetuates it

CDA insight: Unconscious bias is often more powerful than conscious bias because it appears "natural" and goes unchallenged.

5. History Textbooks (Mixed Conscious/Unconscious)

Key Issue

History textbooks often claim objectivity but contain subtle positioning through lexical choices, voice, and narrative structure. The stance may be partly conscious (editorial decisions) and partly unconscious (cultural assumptions).

5.1 How Stance Appears in History Texts

Device Effect Example
Passive Voice Hide agency, obscure responsibility "Mistakes were made" (by whom?)
Nominalization Turn actions into things, obscure actors "The colonization of..." (who colonized?)
Lexical Choice Frame events ideologically "Settlers" vs. "invaders", "discovery" vs. "invasion"
Modality Present opinion as fact "It is clear that...", "Obviously...", "Undoubtedly..."
Omission Silence alternative perspectives Ignoring indigenous viewpoints in colonial history
Example: Comparing Historical Framings

Version A (biased): "Australia was discovered by Britain in 1788"

Version B (less biased): "British forces arrived in Australia in 1788"

Version C (indigenous perspective): "British forces invaded the lands of the Aboriginal peoples in 1788"

Analysis:

  • Version A: "discovered" implies the land was empty (ignores 60,000+ years of indigenous presence)
  • Version B: "arrived" is more neutral but still doesn't acknowledge existing ownership
  • Version C: "invaded" acknowledges prior occupation and the violence of colonization

Each version encodes a different ideological position.

Case Study: Passive Voice and Responsibility

Text: "During the colonial period, native populations were significantly reduced."

Analysis:

  • Passive voice: "were reduced" — BY WHOM?
  • Euphemism: "reduced" — meaning killed through violence, disease, starvation
  • Nominalization: "colonial period" — obscures who was doing the colonizing
  • Effect: Makes genocide sound like a natural process rather than human action

Rewritten with agency: "Colonial forces killed, displaced, and starved indigenous populations."

6. Analyzing Authorial Stance - Complete Checklist

Nine-Point Analysis Framework

  1. Pronoun Use: Who is "we"? Who is "they"? Inclusive or exclusive?
  2. Modality: Certainty markers (must, will, should, might, could)
  3. Evaluative Language: Adjectives and adverbs expressing judgment
  4. Presuppositions: What is assumed as shared knowledge?
  5. Voice: Active (shows agency) vs. Passive (hides agency)
  6. Lexical Choices: Word selection reveals ideology (terrorist vs. freedom fighter)
  7. Metaphors: Conceptual frameworks used (war, journey, disease, flood)
  8. What's Absent: Whose voices are silenced? What's not said?
  9. Genre Expectations: How does text conform to or deviate from norms?

7. Key Theorists and Concepts

Teun van Dijk

Key Concept: Ideological Square

Analysis of racist discourse showing how positive self-presentation combines with negative other-presentation.

Norman Fairclough

Key Concept: Synthetic Personalization

Mass communication disguised as personal — the "manufactured intimacy" of promotional discourse.

George Lakoff

Key Concept: Conceptual Metaphor Theory

How metaphors reveal worldview — the frames we use shape how we think about issues.

8. Applying Stance Analysis: Worked Examples

Example 1: News Report Analysis

Text: "Police were forced to use tear gas when the protest turned violent."

Stance Analysis:

  • "were forced" — passive, removes police agency, implies no choice
  • "turned violent" — protest as agent of violence (were police also violent?)
  • Presupposition: Police action was reactive, justified
  • Absent: Protester perspective, what preceded the tear gas

Alternative framing: "Police deployed tear gas against protesters" — assigns agency to police

Stance: Original text has pro-police positioning, probably unconscious (standard news framing)

Example 2: Political Speech Analysis

Text: "We must protect our way of life from those who would destroy it."

Stance Analysis:

  • "We" — who is included? (citizens, "real" citizens, the nation?)
  • "must" — strong modality, no alternative presented
  • "our way of life" — presupposes shared values, homogeneous culture
  • "those who" — vague othering, enemy unspecified
  • "would destroy" — intent attributed to "them"

Stance: Conscious-explicit nationalist positioning with hidden us/them construction

Chapter Summary: The Three Stances

Key Takeaways from Chapter 3:

1. Conscious-Explicit Stance (Reviews)

  • Writer openly shares opinion
  • Reader knows it's subjective
  • Uses "I," evaluative language, modal verbs
  • CDA task: Examine what values underlie the judgments

2. Conscious-Hidden Stance (Advertising)

  • Writer hides persuasive intent
  • Manufactured intimacy and trust
  • Synthetic personalization, flattery, false scarcity
  • CDA task: Expose the hidden commercial/political agenda

3. Unconscious Stance (Bias)

  • Writer unaware of their positioning
  • Reveals naturalized ideology
  • Othering pronouns, metaphors, passive voice, omission
  • CDA task: Make visible what the writer takes for granted

Analysis Framework:

  1. Identify text type and expected stance
  2. Examine pronouns (who is included/excluded?)
  3. Find evaluative language (judgment words)
  4. Check voice (active reveals, passive conceals)
  5. Note lexical choices (loaded words)
  6. Identify presuppositions (what's assumed?)
  7. Consider omissions (what's silenced?)

Remember: No text is neutral. Every text has a stance — the question is whether it's visible or hidden, conscious or unconscious.

9. Summary: Key Points to Remember

Essential Takeaways from Chapter 3:
  • Stance = authorial position toward subject and audience
  • Three types: Conscious-Explicit, Conscious-Hidden, Unconscious
  • Reviews use first-person, evaluative adjectives, modality
  • Promotional texts use synthetic personalization and manufactured intimacy
  • Racist discourse uses othering pronouns, dehumanizing metaphors, presuppositions
  • Sexist discourse uses generic masculine, asymmetric address, appearance focus
  • Van Dijk's Ideological Square: emphasize our good/their bad, mitigate our bad/their good
  • History textbooks use passive voice and nominalization to obscure agency
  • Lexical choice (settlers vs. invaders) reveals ideological framing
  • Always ask: What's NOT said? Whose voice is absent?
