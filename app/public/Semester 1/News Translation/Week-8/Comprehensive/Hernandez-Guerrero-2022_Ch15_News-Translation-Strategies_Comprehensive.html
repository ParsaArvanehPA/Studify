News Translation Strategies

Author: María José Hernández Guerrero

Source: Bielsa (2022), Chapter 15

Type: Comprehensive Study Guide

Introduction: Translation in News Production

Core Principle

Translation is present at ALL stages of news production, from information generation until transmission to the destined audience. News media have a dual function: selecting and broadcasting – they filter news, give it specific style and language for target audience, and culturally/ideologically mark news items.

Translation in Mass Media

Key Quote (Bielsa & Bassnett 2009:11):

"Translation is one element in a complex set of processes whereby information is transposed from one language into another and then edited, rewritten, shaped and repackaged in a new context, to such a degree that any clear distinction between source and target ceases to be meaningful."

Sociological Implications: A significant portion of news circulating globally has been translated. These translated texts actively influence how readers perceive the world around them, forming their opinions.

Key Transformation Concepts

News translation is heavily influenced by news production processes in media organizations. Different concepts account for transformation processes:

1. Gatekeeping

Definition

Gatekeeping (Communication Studies): "The process of selecting, writing, editing, positioning, scheduling, repeating and otherwise massaging information to become news" (Shoemaker, Vos & Reese 2009:73)

Fujii's Four Gatekeeping Functions (1988)

  1. Controlling quantity: Cutting the original
  2. Message transforming: Altering expressions of the original
  3. Message supplementing: Adding expressions/information
  4. Message reorganization: Changing structure of the original

Fujii's View: "Such large-scale message manipulation certainly goes beyond the work of mere translation"

Vuorinen's Counterargument (1995)

Proposes that gatekeeping operations (deletion, addition, substitution, reorganization) be considered part and parcel of normal textual operations performed in any translation, particularly news translation, to produce functionally adequate target texts.

Valdeón's Two-Level Model (2016)

Gatekeeping in journalistic translation occurs at two levels:

  • Institutional Level: Media select material that can/should be translated (occurs BEFORE translation)
  • Individual Level: Journalist-translator selects information to disseminate, making personal decisions regarding omissions, additions, adaptations

2. Transediting

Transediting (Stetting 1989)

Definition: "A pragmatic translation strategy which involves radical editing"

Editing has always been part of translating. Transediting involves translating and editing simultaneously.

Type Description
Cleaning-up Transediting Adaptation to a standard of efficiency in expression
Situational Transediting Adaptation to intended function of translated text in new social contexts
Cultural Transediting Adaptation to needs and conventions of target culture

Relationship to Gatekeeping: Chen (2009) argues gatekeeping encompasses transediting. Valdeón (2016) argues gatekeeping is on a higher level than translation.

3. Recontextualization

Recontextualization Process

Verschueren (2007:79-80): The process of recontextualization "affects meaning, function and reception: a translated text involves not only linguistic displacement but also contextual/cultural dislocation"

Schäffner & Bassnett (2010:8): "Recontextualisation always involves transformation, determined by goals, values and interests of the new content into which information is being recontextualized"

Media professionals not only filter, translate and edit; they also recontextualize for a local media outlet and its audience. Messages are transformed and information presented from a different perspective.

4. Localization

Pym's View (2004:4)

News is considered a localized product: "The foreign news we read in the local press can legitimately be seen as a localisation of foreign-language texts, at some point transformed by the international agencies, and transformed in ways that go beyond endemic notions of translation."

5. Rewriting

Rewriting in Journalism

Hernando (1990:57): Rewriting is a collaborative text production process where one or more editors rewrite information based on data from other journalists.

Bielsa & Bassnett (2009:57): "Journalistic rewritings are the form in which news is made available to readers worldwide"

Applications of Rewriting:

  • Agency news undergoes rewriting in national language before redistribution
  • Foreign correspondents' news results from intercultural mediation
  • International press news translated by national newspapers is rewritten for new readers

Principal Strategies in News Translation

Terminology and Conceptual Framework

Confusion in Translation Studies Terminology

Terms like procedures, techniques, norms, tactics, methods occasionally function as synonyms for strategy while at other times they refer to different concepts. The term "translation strategies" has become the most widely used term in news translation research.

Schjoldager's Framework (2008)

Distinguishes between two types of strategies:

  • Macrostrategies (Macro-textual strategies): Relate to overall plan of translator
  • Microstrategies: Focus on solving specific translation problems related to words, phrases, sentences – individual decisions at micro-textual level

Similar to Chesterman's (1997) distinction between global and local strategies

Macrostrategies in News Translation

Dominant Macrostrategy: Domestication

In journalism, the underlying objective is always that the audience understands the message efficiently with maximum clarity. The dominant macrostrategy in news translation is DOMESTICATION, as illustrated in studies by Bassnett (2005), Bielsa & Bassnett (2009), Hernández Guerrero (2009), Holland (2013).

Domestication in Communication Studies

Gurevitch et al. (1991) - European Study

"In order to be judged newsworthy, an event must be anchored in a narrative framework that is already familiar to and recognizable by newsmen as well as by audiences"

Clausen's Analysis (2004) - Japan Study

Uses "domestication" to refer to processes of making information comprehensible to audiences in a given culture.

Two main factors supporting domestication across levels:

  1. International effort to make information understandable to national audiences (incorporated into professional mental scripts and models)
  2. Contextual differences at national, organizational, and professional levels causing news content to differ

Domestication in Translation Studies

Venuti's Dichotomy (1995)

Strategy Characteristics
Domestication Translating using transparent, fluid, invisible style to reduce foreign traces; conforming to target audience expectations and reading habits
Foreignization Giving importance to linguistic and cultural differences in original text and reflecting them in translation

Bassnett's Application to News (2005:127)

Acculturation (domestication) prevails in news reporting because foreignization is detrimental to understanding.

Example: Analysis of Saddam Hussein speeches and al-Qaida statements in British media showed that deliberately highlighting foreign elements serves NOT to increase awareness but rather to emphasize strangeness and reinforce distance. Preserving foreignness reinforces negative perceptions.

Bielsa & Bassnett's Conclusion (2009:10)

"The dominant strategy is absolute domestication, as material is shaped in order to be consumed by the target audience, so has to be tailored to suit their needs and expectations."

Microstrategies in News Translation

Communicative purposes of news texts determine the use of domesticating translation strategies at textual micro-level. Journalistic material is restructured, rewritten and remodelled by media to function in new linguistic and cultural contexts.

Van Dijk's Source Text Processing Strategies (1988)

Reporters utilize these strategies in news production:

  • Selection
  • Reproduction
  • Summarizing
  • Local transformation: Addition, deletion, permutation, substitution
  • Stylistic and rhetorical (re-)formulation

Permutation: Reorganization of information by relevance criteria – important information moved forward (up), unimportant moved backward (down)

Core Four Microstrategies

The Four Principal Microstrategies

Supported by Vuorinen (1995) and Hursti (2001), these four "gatekeeping operations" are used to produce functionally adequate target texts:

  1. DELETION/OMISSION
  2. ADDITION
  3. SUBSTITUTION
  4. REORGANIZATION

Product-Based Studies: Evidence Across Languages

Scholar(s) Language Pair/Corpus Key Strategies Identified
Valdeón (2005) BBC World → BBC Mundo (Spanish) Omissions, additions (most used), permutation
Valdeón (2007a) BBC Mundo & CNN en Español Reorganization, additions, omissions, substitutions
Bani (2006) Internazionale (Italian magazine) Cutting/summarizing, inclusion of explanations, generalization, substitution
Hernández Guerrero (2006) Libération → Spanish Amplification (addition), compression, omission (most used)
Kang (2007) Newsweek → Newsweek Hankuk Pan (Korean) Omission, addition, generalization, particularization, re-perspectivization
Loupaki (2010) English → Greek news Literal translation, neutralization, addition, explicitation, omission
Chen (2011) English-Chinese (Taiwan) Selection, deletion, addition, combination, synthesis, abridgement, recomposition
Putri (2019) Indonesian → English (VICE Indonesia) Recomposition/change of paragraph order, deletion, summarizing, addition

Ethnographic Study: News Agencies

Bielsa & Bassnett (2009) - Reuters, AFP, IPS

Observed internal operations, interviewed journalists, analyzed translations at major agencies.

Key Finding: Translation involves all kinds of textual manipulation. Omission is a key strategy in news translation.

Most Frequent Modifications:

  • Change of title and lead (informative subtitles)
  • Elimination of unnecessary information
  • Addition of important background information
  • Change in the order of paragraphs
  • Summarizing information

Foreignization Strategies in Specific Elements

When Foreignization Appears

Some scholars describe foreignization strategies in specific elements like proper names and cultural references:

Kwieciñski (1998) - English-Polish: Marked dominance of highly foreignizing strategies in culture-specific items (transference with no glosses). Originally opaque Anglo-American items rapidly absorbed by Polish audience and "deforeignized".

Scammell (2018) - Reuters: Tendency toward domestication, but small percentage of foreignizing strategies: retention (loanwords), specification (keeping untranslated), direct translation (calque).

Chaal (2019) - Arabic translations: Common domesticating strategies, but identifies "preservation" (retaining proper names of local institutions, journals, applications) to keep local color.

Risk Management Framework

Matsushita & Pym: Risk Management Theory (2015-2019)

New perspective: Translation strategies are ways of managing risk, not ways of achieving equivalence.

Risk Strategy Description
Risk Taking Accepting potential negative consequences
Risk Avoidance Completely avoiding risky elements
Risk Transfer Shifting responsibility for risk
Risk Mitigation Accepting one risk while attempting to protect against consequences by incurring second risk, without removing initial risk

Example of Risk Mitigation

Journalist-translators omit difficult words used in quotes or complicated phrasing to avoid/mitigate risk of making translation difficult to understand.

Pym & Matsushita (2018:15): Use of strategies like mitigation "should challenge the huge binarisms [foreignizing/domesticating] that would classify whole translations as being of one kind or the other"

Specific Elements of News: Headlines

Why Headlines?

Substantial changes in translation often make comparison impossible. Many scholars face absence of traceable source text. Headlines and quotations are easy to detect and permit comparison.

Functions of Headlines

  1. Capture readers' attention
  2. Provide succinct view of content
  3. Serve to identify the article

Although headlines can function independently, they are semantically linked to information and context. Translation of headline/lead influenced by rest of article.

Case Study: El País Controversy (2019)

Bolsonaro at Davos

Spanish edition: "Bolsonaro anima a los ejecutivos de Davos a invertir en el nuevo Brasil" (Bolsonaro urges Davos executives to invest in new Brazil)

Brazilian edition: "O breve discurso de Bolsonaro decepciona em Davos" (Bolsonaro's brief speech disappoints in Davos)

Reason: Editors changed headline based on last-minute additions about negative reactions. New angle justified different headline.

Result: Controversy - readers accused newspaper of manipulation.

Research Findings

Sidiropoulou (1995): "Headlines are only rarely transferred intact into target language"

Petronienė & Žvirblytė (2012): "Absolute equivalence is quite hard to be achieved"

Kaniklidou & House (2013): Headlines are "product of re-capturing, reframing and re-narrating"

Zhang (2013): Headlines "heavily mediated and recontextualised" - transeditors put own knowledge/values into texts

Creating New Headlines vs Translation

Every language has own conventions for headlining. Target audiences accustomed to reading headlines following textual conventions in their media context. This leads to preference for substituting original headline with new one instead of translating it.

Evidence: Sidiropoulou (1995), Samaniego (2003), Hernández Guerrero (2004), Bani (2013)

Ideological Component

Scholar Finding
Kontos & Sidiropoulou (2012) Ideological assumptions influence how headlines are translated to communicate different messages
Ali (2010) Lexical selection, style, rhetoric in headlines is deliberate - portal for hidden opinions/ideologies
Đorđević (2020) Serbian outlets adapt news to confirm political affiliation to government for survival
Valdeón (2007b, 2008) BBC/CNN/Reuters: Ideological implications through omissions, additions, permutations
Guangjun & Huanyao (2015) Chinese translations: Priority on producing translations suitable to censors' ideology

Specific Elements of News: Quotations

Quotes in News

Quotes are statements (spoken/written) by those featured in news stories. Attributing statements to sources is a resource to increase truthfulness and credibility.

Types: Direct quotes (exact words) and Indirect quotes (paraphrase)

Schäffner (2008): Quotes in other languages presented as if pronounced/written in target language, but must be translated.

Blackledge (2005): Recontextualization involves "filtering of meaning potentials" - reflected in omissions, additions, reformulations.

News Agencies and Quotes

Bielsa & Bassnett (2009)

Agencies place fundamental importance on direct sources. Journalists instructed to quote sources whenever possible rather than paraphrasing, using idiomatic translation (domesticating strategies).

Davier (2012)

Journalists display flexible translation concept, EXCEPT in two cases where more literal method applied:

  1. Signed dispatches
  2. Quotes

Reason: Fear of mistranslation. When literal translation not possible/awkward, journalists either:

  • Omit the quote, OR
  • Include using paraphrase

Result: Translated dispatches likely contain more indirect discourse than source texts.

Scammell (2018) - Reuters

Most common: Domestication

Reuters Handbook: "Translate quotes idiomatically rather than with pedantic literalness. However, give literal translation if statement is tendentious and likely to be subject of close analysis"

Proposal: Update handbook guidance; defend ethical potential of foreignized approach for intercultural communication and accuracy.

Newspapers and Quotes

Chen (2009) - Ideological Manipulation

New York Times & Washington Post → Chinese (Taiwan)

Finding: Each paper used selection, addition, deletion of quotations to reflect dissimilar ideological perspectives.

"Quotation is typically conceived of as 'impartial' reproduction... However, quotation is usually adopted to implicitly convey certain preferred interpretations... The final version may have undergone reinterpretations by series of people. Its original communicative intention and purpose are no longer kept intact."

Matsushita (2014) - Risk Management

Obama speeches → Five Japanese newspapers

Most frequent strategy: OMISSION (ahead of addition, substitution, paraphrasing, reorganization, explicitation)

Conclusion: Omission common strategy when managing translator's risks.

Progression Analysis

Perrin & Ehrensberger-Dow (2018)

Multi-method approach: ethnographic observation, interviews, computer recordings, retrospective protocols

Study: Swiss television (Tele Züri) - quotes in unfamiliar languages

Strategies Observed:

  1. Altering quotes for dramaturgical objectives
  2. Solving problems by consulting non-linguist colleagues
  3. Translating only key sentences to allow audience to hear original
  4. Purposeful omission of utterances not understood (choosing English instead of Arab quotes)

Finding: Journalists "decide which voices are to be heard in their important function as gatekeepers."

Translingual Quoting (Haapenen & Perrin 2019)

Process of news-writing where original discourse is translated during quoting.

Two types: (1) Translating ready-made quotes, (2) Translating interview utterances

Digital Age: Social Media Content

Hernández Guerrero (2020) - Tweets as Quotes

Study: "Universo Trump" blog (El País Spanish digital newspaper)

Strategies when using English tweets as quotes:

  1. Paraphrase (preferred)
  2. Literal translation
  3. No translation

New practice: Screenshots of original tweets make translation more obvious and visible in information transmission.

Challenges and Future Directions

1. Methodological Challenges

Davier, Schäffner & van Doorslaer (2018)

Limitation: Product-oriented approaches insufficient for deep understanding.

Proposed Solution: TRIANGULATION

  • Textual analysis + Participant-centered methods
  • Semi-structured interviews
  • Non-participant observation
  • Collection of internal documents

Hypotheses from textual analysis can only be confirmed/amended through first-hand fieldwork information.

2. Digital Age Transformations

Journalism immersed in transformative process due to communication technologies:

New forms: Citizen journalism, blogs, webs, social networks, multimedia platforms

Media convergence: Newspapers+web, web+radio, web+radio/television

Result: Changing content production methods, transforming news production process and journalist practices

Caimotto (2019) - Challenges for Researchers

Difficulty investigating news translation when:

  • Target texts updated repeatedly
  • Source text fragmented into different genres: oral, written, subtitles, voice-over, live posts, tweets

3. Emerging Trends

Davier (2019) - Le Droit (Canada)

Finding: Translation avoided in audiovisual production (video clips). French voices systematically preferred over English.

Prediction: "May reinforce trend to exclude sources in second language"

Hernández Guerrero & Díaz López (2020) - RT Multimedia

RT uses television + multilingual platform (6 languages)

Content: Infographics, hyperlinks, audiovisual material, social media = multimedia stories

Translation Process Simplified:

  • New headline created
  • Opening paragraph (lead) rewritten
  • Other elements: Omitted OR substituted for digital content in target language

Strategy of substitution optimizes resources and speeds production (fundamental in digital platforms).

Future Research

Studies point to possible decrease in use of translation, but too early to confirm. More research needed on:

  • New journalistic practices
  • Influence of media convergence on news translation strategies

Summary and Conclusion

Key Takeaways

1. Nature of News Translation: Involves transformation and manipulation to create new text suited to publication needs and target readers.

2. Macro-level Domestication: Institutions and individuals use domesticating strategies to produce easily readable information.

3. Four Core Microstrategies:

  • Omission
  • Addition
  • Substitution
  • Reorganization

4. Impact: Research reveals impact on:

  • Journalistic construction of reality
  • Public opinion formation

5. Major Challenges:

  • Methodological: Need triangulation with participant-centered methods
  • Digital age: Changes in production affecting translation processes

6. Future Directions:

  • Research on new journalistic practices
  • Influence of media convergence
  • Possible decrease in translation use (needs confirmation)
