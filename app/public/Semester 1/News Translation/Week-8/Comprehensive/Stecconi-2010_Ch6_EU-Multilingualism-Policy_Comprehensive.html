Author: Ubaldo Stecconi

Introduction: Historical and Institutional Context

Origins of European Integration (1957)

Political and ideological movement born from ravages of two world wars. Six European countries met in Rome to sign treaties establishing:

  • European Economic Community (EEC)
  • European Atomic Energy Community (Euratom)

Original Goal (Robert Schuman, 1950): Secure lasting peace by pooling industries and technologies essential for warfare (coal and steel).

Revolutionary Organizing Principle

Countries would give up some sovereign powers and place them under independent authority (first: European Coal and Steel Community, 1951).

Why revolutionary? Historically, European countries managed relations through bilateral agreements or multilateral summits (Congress of Vienna 1814), but NEVER willingly ceded sovereign powers for common good.

Principle of Equality

Core Innovation

Congress of Vienna system (1814-1914) was structurally flawed: deals inevitably biased toward larger, more powerful countries at expense of minor powers.

EU Innovation: Based on principle of equality. Within treaty limits, ALL countries that joined EU enjoy:

  • Same rights
  • Same duties
  • Fair representation in common institutions

Result: Creation of independent bodies with mandate to defend/promote common good opened "a clearing in international law, which had been a Hobbesian jungle until then."

Language in Original Treaties

Important Note: Original treaties did NOT include any provision related to language among articles devoted to Community tasks. Tasks focused on:

  • Common economic goals (internal market, growth, jobs)
  • Social and environmental protection
  • Social cohesion and solidarity
  • Equality between men and women

Legal Basis of EU Language Policy

Article 21 (TEC)

Citizen Communication Rights

"Every citizen of the Union may write to any of [its] institutions or bodies [...] in one of the languages mentioned in Article 314 and have an answer in the same language."

Significance: EU institutions are multilingual bodies. To author's knowledge, NO other international/supranational organization is mandated by foundational law to interact with constituents in languages they understand.

Closer to: Arrangements in multilingual countries (Belgium, Canada, South Africa).

Principle: Law cannot give preferential treatment to any language lest discrimination be legalized.

Note: Use of verb "write" presupposes official exchanges between institutions and natural/legal persons. Language regarded as operational and administrative issue.

Article 314 (TEC)

Equally Authentic Languages

"This Treaty, drawn up in a single original in the Dutch, French, German, and Italian languages, all four texts being equally authentic, shall be deposited [...]"

Original: Four languages (1957)

Current: Amended several times, now includes 23 languages

"Equally authentic": Intriguing phrase reaffirming principle of equality - different language versions are equally authentic.

Article 290 (TEC)

Decision-Making on Languages

"The rules governing the languages of the institutions of the Community shall [...] be determined by the Council, acting unanimously."

Crucial implications:

  • Languages absent from original policies of Community
  • ANY decision regarding languages taken by Council (representatives of member state governments)
  • Each government has VETO POWER

Council Regulation No. 1 of 1958

First Regulation Adopted

Although languages regarded as mere administrative challenge, solving it was precondition for newly established institutions to work at all.

Article 1: "The official languages and the working languages of the institutions of the Community shall be Dutch, French, German and Italian."

Article 3: "Documents which an institution of the Community sends to a Member State or to a person subject to the jurisdiction of a Member State shall be drafted in the language of such State."

Confirms: Official languages of European institutions coincide with official languages of member countries.

Official and Working Languages

Distinction and Challenge

Regulation uses both terms - "official and working languages" - but makes NO distinction between them (van Els 2005; Ammon 2006).

When 4 languages: Distinction not crucial

Today (23 languages): Potentially major difficulty - unrealistic to expect such linguistic prowess of EU officials

Practical Arrangements

Internal Communication Reality

When EU staff talk/write to each other, they normally use:

  • French or English
  • German a distant third

Result: Passive bilingualism not uncommon - people comprehend both French and English perfectly but prefer one for writing/speaking.

Situation: EU institutions home to people from 27 nationalities using mainly TWO languages to communicate, and for large majority NEITHER is mother tongue.

EU Institutional Culture

Koskinen (2000, 2004)

Claims that EU institutions form a culture of their own. Author's observations confirm this insight.

Key Questions:

  • What is position of EU institutional culture vis-à-vis cultures of its constituencies?
  • Is it a melting pot? An interculture (Pym 2002)? An ivory tower detached from real world?
  • What about citizens' participation and democratic legitimacy?

Stakes: Interface between EU institutional culture and cultures across continent potentially affects democratic legitimacy.

More Recent Developments

Treaty of Maastricht (1992, entered force 1993)

Article 149 (ex Article 126)

"Community action shall be aimed at: developing the European dimension in education, particularly through the teaching and dissemination of the languages of the Member States."

Significance: Short point, hardly basis for comprehensive policy, language only mentioned via education. HOWEVER, languages ceased to be entirely administrative issue.

Constitutional Treaty (2004) - Rejected but Influential

Article I-10: Citizenship Right

"(d) the right to petition the European Parliament, to apply to the European Ombudsman, and to address the Institutions and advisory bodies of the Union in any of the Constitution's languages and to obtain a reply in the same language."

Evolution: Using one's language with EU institutions stated as citizenship right.

Article II-101: Right to Good Administration

"4. Every citizen may write to the Institutions of the Union in one of the languages of the Constitution and must have an answer in the same language."

Preserves: Traditional view - language rights part of citizen's right to good administration.

Source: EU charter of fundamental rights of 2000 (mentioned language as grounds for prohibited discrimination).

The "Equally Authentic" Paradox

Author's Theoretical Analysis

Most important theoretical feature: Phrase "equally authentic"

Two interpretations:

  1. Focus on "equally": No matter which language text drafted in, that language has NO higher status than languages into which it was translated
  2. Focus on "authenticity": Although all versions but one must be translations, they are NOT to be regarded as such

Likely Reason for Interpretation #2: Prevent EU's primary legal text being weakened by translation

Potential Problems without "authentic" clause:

  • Translated article could be challenged in court for not "saying the same" thing as original
  • Easy to make claim, devilishly difficult to settle
  • Ruthless lawyers could freeze trials about to lose
  • Could make European law difficult to implement
  • Nationalist politicians might object to theirs being language INTO which legislation translated (given lower social/cultural status of translations across Europe)

Implicit View: Translation as corrupting operation producing second-rate texts - not made explicit but merely implied in primary legislation

Power of Implicit Communication: When you use inferential processes to extract implicit views from text, becomes more difficult to formulate critical response - inference located in your mind, not in text before eyes - somehow already belongs to you.

Community vs Inter-Governmental Methods

Current State

Treaties in force and Reform Treaty under ratification leave area of languages to Member States:

  • Little room for administrative decisions at community level
  • Even less room for policy-making

Note: NOT to imply Commission/Parliament should take over. Many excellent reasons why national authorities should keep tight grip on languages.

However: Legislation and policies should keep pace with changes in body politic and social.

Three Major Changes in European Linguistic Landscape

1. Migration

Europe has become sought-after destination for migrants from poorer regions. Together with their ability/willingness to work (propping up economy threatened by ageing population), they add customs and languages to cultural and human diversity of Europe.

2. Erasmus Generations and Internal Mobility

Never been so easy to move to another EU country for study/work. Progressive dismantling of internal barriers. Increased mobility may not enrich diversity but will mix existing palette to create new colors.

3. Devolution to Regions

Growing demand in certain countries for devolution of central powers to regions. Linked to success of EU as institutional model - created alternative to monopoly position of "the nation [as] an imagined political community" (Anderson 1991:5).

Result: European citizens of tomorrow likely to feel multiple, equally strong allegiances toward own regions, countries, and united Europe. Regional languages may acquire higher status.

Inter-Governmental Example: Co-Official Languages Debate (2004)

Spain vs France

According to Spanish press (ABC, La Razón 18 May 2004), during final conference approving Constitution:

Spain requested: Recognition of "co-official languages" in constitutional treaty

  • Consistent with trends granting regions autonomy, including use of regional languages (Catalan, Galician, Basque)

France opposed: Consistent with traditional French-only policy (e.g., "loi Toubon" 1994 making French compulsory in all advertising)

Resulting Article IV-448(2)

"This Treaty may also be translated into any other languages as determined by Member States among those which, in accordance with their constitutional order, enjoy official status in all or part of their territory [...]"

Effect: Would have allowed Madrid to have European Constitution exist in languages other than Castilian.

Declaration appended: Expressed lofty ideals about respecting "Union's rich cultural and linguistic diversity"

Curiosity: Only FIVE occurrences of term "translation" and cognates in famously long Constitution.

A Policy Document on Multilingualism (2005)

Three Events Marking Ascent of Multilingualism Policy

  1. 2004: Inclusion of multilingualism in portfolio of Commissioner Figel' (alongside education, culture, youth, sport)
  2. November 2005: Publication of communication "A New Framework Strategy for Multilingualism"
  3. 1 January 2007: Language policies became entire/sole responsibility of newly appointed Romanian Commissioner Leonard Orban

Communication: "A New Framework Strategy for Multilingualism" (2005)

First Comprehensive Policy Document

Followed 2003 communication "Promoting Language Learning and Linguistic Diversity: An Action Plan 2004-2006"

Terminological Issues

Defined "multilingualism" THREE ways:

  1. "A person's ability to use several languages"
  2. "The co-existence of different language communities in one geographical area"
  3. "The new field of Commission policy that promotes a climate conducive to full expression of all languages, in which teaching and learning of variety of languages can flourish"

Problem: Overstretched term. Would have been preferable to use distinct terms to keep referents apart.

Policy Aims

  • Promoting language learning and linguistic diversity
  • Promoting the multilingual economy
  • Making EU legislation available in official languages of Union

Practical Upshots

  • Advisory panel: Independent experts to analyze progress, suggest new initiatives
  • Ministerial conference: EU countries share experiences, plan future work
  • Future development: Commission would use input from expert group and national authorities to develop approach

Characteristics: Openness, involvement, careful policy design (contrast with 2004 negotiations behind closed doors)

Content Beyond Administrative Challenge

Clearly tried to go beyond traditional view of multilingualism as administrative challenge.

Recognition: Language domain much broader; multilingualism crucial for long-term success of European integration.

Traditional positions maintained:

  • Focus on language learning
  • "English is not enough" orientation (learning foreign language should not necessarily mean learning English alone)

New element: Focus on economic impact of languages

  • Part 1: Wealth directly generated by language/mediation skills (e.g., localization industry)
  • Part 2: What public authorities can/should do to assist continental economy becoming increasingly multilingual

Remarks: Three Important Aspects

1. Language Services of EU Institutions

Most Developed Part of Multilingualism

Ability to ensure communication in all official languages is:

  • Prerequisite for proper functioning of institutions
  • Essential for democratic legitimacy of Union

Citizens and institutions must understand legal documents and relevant information in their language.

Which Institutions Have Language Services?

  • Council
  • Parliament
  • Court of Justice
  • Economic and Social Committee
  • Committee of the Regions
  • Court of Auditors
  • European Commission
  • European Investment Bank
  • European Bank for Reconstruction and Development
  • European Central Bank

Translation Centre (Luxembourg): Takes care of remaining bodies and agencies

Directorate General for Interpretation: Covers interpretation on inter-institutional basis (except Parliament and Court of Justice which have own services)

DGT - Directorate General for Translation (Commission)

By far the largest service

February 2007 statistics:

  • 2,300 staff total
  • 1,700 full-time translators
  • Rest: support staff

2006 output: Over 1.5 million pages (about 20% translated by freelancers)

2. English Only? Language Learning Policy

Commission Recommendation

Goal: Each European citizen should speak TWO foreign languages apart from mother tongue

"Mother tongue plus two"

Why Not Just One Language?

Answer from opening chapter: EU respects, fosters, and is based on DIVERSITY

Comparison to US:

USA EU
English-only tradition Multilingual diversity
Melting pot - people give up languages to integrate Welding - pieces welded together, each clearly recognizable as distinct

Language Barriers vs Diversity

Praise of diversity should NOT prevent Europeans from having some languages in common.

Language barriers hinder:

  • Free circulation of capital, people, goods, services
  • Political, social, cultural integration

Solution: Co-ordinated effort to teach other European languages since earliest years of schooling

Challenge: Determining WHICH language(s) should be taught

Current situation: No co-ordinated explicit orientation for whole EU. Whatever de facto policies exist = sum of member state policies.

English Dominance Debate

Two Extreme Views (Both Wrong)

  1. Pro: English as natural lingua franca of Europe and world
  2. Con: English as demonized symbol of negative impact of globalization

Eurobarometer 2006 Data

Finding: English most popular foreign language spoken across EU (38% of non-English-speaking Europeans claim they can hold conversation)

However: NOT a behemoth as sometimes portrayed

  • Many Europeans (especially in smaller countries) familiar with languages of larger neighbors
  • German popular in: Slovak Republic, Czech Republic, Hungary, Slovenia
  • Italian popular in: Malta, Croatia
  • Russian popular in: Former Baltic Republics, Bulgaria

Interpretation: "Mother tongue plus two" corresponds to many-centered Europe where significant number already learn English PLUS neighbor's language

Author's Caveats

  1. Read data carefully: No such thing as enough information on language behaviour/perceptions for policy makers
  2. Design carefully: Common language policy should be accurately designed around existing trends and as unobtrusive as possible
  3. Avoid top-down: Attempts by public authorities to promote top-down language policies associated with totalitarian/undemocratic regimes (Franco's Spain, Mussolini's Italy)
  4. Worst examples today: Assimilation policies discouraging minority languages in Afghanistan, Burma, Indonesia, Iraq (none paragons of democracy)
  5. Languages = identity: Languages intertwined with affirmation/identification of personal and community identities. People would resist abrupt policies or reject as undue intrusions into most private thoughts/feelings
  6. Main point: Go along with observed language behaviour; involve as many people as possible in all stages using transparent/accessible platforms

3. Promoting Linguistic Diversity

Evolution of Concept

Traditional focus: Large non-national and co-official regional languages (e.g., Welsh, Catalan)

Increasingly means: Dealing with weaker/smaller aboriginal language minorities AND languages of migrants

Complexity of Diversity

Eurobarometer data insights: In several countries, many respondents indicated national language as NOT their mother tongue

Examples:

  • Spain: 1 in 10 indicated Castilian as main foreign language (probably native speakers of regional languages)
  • Germany: Similar proportion recorded (probably immigrants born abroad)
  • Turkey: 7% indicated Turkish as foreign language (belonged to one of 34 language communities listed by SIL International)

Scale of Linguistic Diversity

Aboriginal languages in EU: About 90 (Gordon 2005)

Migrant languages: DWARF aboriginal languages in number

Example - British National Centre for Languages Survey (2004-05):

As many as 273 languages spoken by school children in England, Wales and Scotland

Policy Imperatives

ANY language policy should protect and help develop ALL languages spoken within EU borders irrespective of being:

  • National
  • Regional/minority
  • Migrant languages

Priority: If EU to keep mission to "respect its rich cultural and linguistic diversity" (TEC I-3(3)), should give priority to language communities that MOST need tangible and symbolic support

High Stakes

Much at stake:

  • Migrant families: Language and culture tensions common, especially as young generations born/raised in Europe grow up
  • Indigenous communities: Similar tensions affect communities, embitter inter-cultural relations
  • Major challenge: Poised to become major stumbling blocks for successful integration of newly arrived Europeans and for balanced/peaceful development of societies
