Subtitling Practices in Islamic Satellite Television

Author: Yasmin Moll

Source: Bielsa (2022), Chapter 29

Type: Comprehensive Study Guide - Ethnographic Research

Introduction: Iqraa Channel Context

About Iqraa

  • Founded: 1998 by Saudi media mogul
  • Type: Transnational Islamic satellite channel
  • Content: Arabic language preaching programmes
  • Translation: Sometimes subtitled into English by dedicated team in Cairo, Egypt

Translators' Self-Perception (Dual Role)

  1. 'Preachers by proxy': Transmitting correct religious knowledge
  2. 'Cultural mediators': For radically different subtitle-reading audience from Arabic original

Goal: Subtitle audience as edified and entertained as Arabic-language audience presumed to be

Central Research Focus

Translation as key site for enacting religious reformist ethic attuned to:

  • Compelling this-worldly representation of Islam
  • Individual otherworldly consequences of translating incorrect interpretations of divine revelation

Key finding: Translators argued from within Islamic frame but did NOT espouse single 'Islamic' translational ideology - rather multiplicity of ethico-religious idioms, all claiming Islamic mantle

Methodological Note

This chapter presents ethnographic material - NOT what translators (who hold advanced degrees in linguistics/translation studies) would write about Arabic-to-English audiovisual translation.

Interest: Explicating subtitling as animated by specific, at times conflicting translational ideologies - scrutinizing translators' practices of critique for pragmatic assumptions about what communication is and how it works

Question: What were both social and individual consequences of using words in translation in particular ways?

Approach: Looking at subtitles not on screen, but off

Religious Responsibility and the Stakes of Translation

The "Muslim and Proud" Crisis (2011)

Crisis Background

Workflow at Iqraa translation centre:

  • Egyptian translators: Create English subtitles of original Arabic programmes
  • Foreign editors: Ensure translations sound 'native' in English

The Incident

Programme: "Muslim wa Aftakhir" (Muslim and Proud)

  • Shot in Nepal
  • Featured observational footage of Hindu devotional rituals
  • On-location commentary by young Saudi preacher
  • Slotted for Iqraa International (targeting Anglophone viewers)

Adam's Critique (Muslim-American editor)

  • Instead of editing, sent scathing indictment
  • Suggested name: "Muslim wa Ahtakir" (Muslim and Contemptuous)
  • Felt preacher made Hindu worship sound 'silly' and 'meaningless'
  • Implicit message: all Hindus destined for hell
  • "Even if one believes that all religions besides Islam are damned, is it advisable to announce this on international television?"

Randa's Response (Translation Manager)

Position: Moral imperative for Islamic outreach (da'wa) channel to extol virtues of Islam

Intended audience:

  • Muslims born in West (might be ashamed due to vilification in popular culture)
  • Non-Muslims interested in learning about Islam

"This isn't a travel show and we are not doing interfaith dialogue. We are calling people to Islam."

Frustration: Arabic-language polemics might jar Western sensibilities, BUT Adam's refusal to 'manipulate' source text to render it more culturally resonant

Translators' Two-Fold Task

1. Cultural Mediators

  • Responsible for countering perceived Western stereotypes about Muslims
  • Through subtitles
  • Making strange familiar, difference intelligible

2. Preachers by Proxy

  • Transmitting correct and relevant religious knowledge to viewers
  • Exercising moral thoughtfulness aimed at producing true religious knowledge
  • Thus Godly persons
  • At times necessitated departing from content of programmes they subtitled

Resources Drawn Upon

To accomplish both aims, translators drew in complex and sophisticated ways on:

  • Biblical translation strategies
  • Postcolonial theory
  • Professional translation manuals
  • Euro-American cultural norms
  • Qur'anic moral imperatives
  • Their own life experiences

Self-authorization: Saw themselves as authorized and qualified to appraise, debate, modify - in other words, critique - the ideas, textual evidence, rhetorical devices utilized by Iqraa preachers

Manipulation vs Lying

Translators' View: Manipulation

For Iqraa translators, 'manipulation' of source text was:

  • Skilled and sensitive enactment of cross-cultural competence
  • Necessary for successful accomplishment of their tasks
  • Not translating language (lugha) but culture (thaqafa)

Required ability: Traverse and momentarily adopt multiplicity of cultural viewpoints - including another culture's view of one's own culture

Adam's View: Lying

To editor Adam, manipulation sounded like euphemism for lying

Differing stances index:

  • Distinctive epistemologies of authorship and meaning
  • Different approaches to critique

The Geopolitics of Translation

Power-Laden Global Mediated Regimes

Context: Islamophobic Representational Circuits

Iqraa translation strategies shaped within power-laden global mediated regimes claiming to represent Islam through 'uncovering' or 'unveiling' its 'real' meanings:

  • Hollywood films (Shaheen 2008)
  • Western news coverage (Said 1981)
  • Memoirs by (ex)-Muslim women (Abu-Lughod 2013)

These constituted imaginative horizons of both translator and (un)intended viewers

MEMRI Threat

MEMRI: Zionist organization

  • Sends subtitled clips from Middle Eastern TV channels to American news outlets and US Congress free of charge
  • Translation scholar Mona Baker (2010: 120): MEMRI specializes "in circulating translations of carefully selected Arabic source texts to elaborate a narrative of Arab societies as extremist, anti-Semitic and a threat to Western democracies"

Result: Iqraa translators felt they had to be very careful with subtitles compared to colleagues in non-Islamic channels

Stakes of Translation

For Iqraa translators, subtitles were about:

  • Reclaiming representational power within context of political, economic, military inequalities between Muslim countries and West
  • Showing how one could be both Muslim and proud
  • Contesting prevailing Western discourses on Islam

Representational Anxiety

Sometimes used terms 'trans-creation' or 'adaptation' instead of manipulation because:

  • Anxious that negative connotations of 'manipulation' in English could lead to strategies being seen as cynical attempt to mislead
  • When Randa invited to European conference on manipulation in translation, worried presentation could be used to 'tarnish' further image of Muslims

Translation Centre Evolution

Organizational Structure

Parent company: Arab Radio and Television (ART) network

Original setup: Subtitle into Arabic foreign (usually American) films acquired by ART, and into English Egyptian films

Mid-2000s shift: ART's English entertainment channels floundered → translation centre worked solely on subtitling Arabic-language programmes on Iqraa

Autonomy: Greater degree than other departments - recruited own staff in Egypt, controlled entirety of operations

Manager: Randa

  • Brought dedication, passion, attention to detail
  • Oversaw close-knit group of 4 full-time translators/editors
  • About half dozen freelancers (worked from home)
  • 8 other staff for cueing, researching religious references, coordinating production cycle

Translator Profiles

  • Middle-class urban backgrounds
  • Bachelor degrees from Egyptian public universities (linguistics or English departments)
  • All held advanced degrees in linguistics and translation studies
  • Used words like 'love' and 'passion' about their work
  • Most said working in 'Islamic translation' incidental to enjoying practice of subtitling
  • BUT: Dedication to Islam motivated them to excel professionally
  • Excellence of subtitles reflected on them as professionals AND on Islam itself
  • Saw themselves as 'ambassadors of Islam' to rest of world

Technical Workflow

Subtitling Production Cycle

  1. Cueing: Indicating in/out times for each subtitle
    • 'Golden rule': each subtitle synchronous with verbal utterance it translates
    • Divides transcribed audio into units to be subtitled (5-6 seconds each)
  2. Translation: Following spatial/temporal constraints
    • 13 characters per second
    • No more than 2 lines of text
    • Each line max 35 characters
    • Superimposed, centered, bottom third of screen
    • Necessitated paraphrasing and condensing (NOT word-for-word)
  3. Editing: Foreign editors ensure 'native' English sound
  4. Revision: Randa personally signed off on each episode

Standard: Randa boasted team worked within parameters on par with European Union ISO 9001 standards

9/11 and the Shift in Translation Strategy

Catalytic Event

9/11 attacks catalyzed major rethinking of translation strategies

Most translators understood US government declaration of 'war on terror' as 'war on Islam'

Felt plausibility among ordinary Americans hinged on representation of Islam by US media (cited Fox News as particularly egregious) as civilizational other

Began to see own existing translation strategies as contributing to this problematic othering

From Foreignization to Domestication

Before 9/11: Foreignization

  • Transliterated key Arabic terms (e.g., 'Allah' instead of 'God')
  • Asked non-Arabic speaking, potentially non-Muslim audience to decipher meaning behind Arabic words
  • Rather than representing words in terms making linguistic and cultural sense to them

After 9/11: Domestication (Randa spearheaded)

  • Transliterated Arabic words kept to minimum
  • Preference given to translating Arabic into English equivalents
  • Sought to reclaim intelligibility of Islam
  • Audience presumed to include well-meaning if misinformed viewers + intermittently hostile ones
  • Enabled counter-critiques of Western critiques of Islam
  • Itself critical rejoinder to othering upon which Western critiques rested

The Foreignization vs Domestication Debate

Lawrence Venuti's The Translator's Invisibility (1995)

Argument: Dominant translation norm of fluency/domestication is "an ethnocentric reduction of the foreign text to target-language cultural values" (Venuti 1995: 2)

Call for: Foreignizing translation registering socio-linguistic differences of source text from target culture, thereby challenging norms of latter

Postcolonial theorists: Foreignization = practice of resistance resistant to cultural imperialism

'Good' translation: Sounds/reads like translation - non-idiomatic, non-fluent, leaves un-translated key words/phrases

Iqraa Translators' Counter-Argument

Problem with foreignizing translations:

  • Made Arabic content seem incoherent, child-like, extra-terrestrial
  • In reception context greatly militating against sympathetic reading
  • Made possible perpetuation of source-culture misperceptions about target culture
  • Left in original terms that may have entered source language lexicon in highly selective ways

Example: jihad

  • Non-translation served NOT to subvert target cultural norms about what Muslims believe
  • But reinforced them
  • Glossed over other contextual meanings ('personal struggle' not just 'armed struggle')

Domestication of language = domestication of otherness

Aimed at challenging negative appraisals of Islam by non-Muslims

"I didn't want us to sound like aliens. I didn't want to reconfirm the stereotypes. I wanted to show that we are normal people, that we have much in common." - Randa

Appropriating Dominant Strategies

Iqraa translators appropriated textual strategies usually associated by theorists (and anthropologists) with ethnocentrism and domination to speak back to negative representations of source culture by target culture

Randa, contra Venuti: Adopted domestication strategy precisely as counter to representational othering that previous foreignization strategy had left unchallenged

'Idiomatic' Subtitles as Normative Standard

  • Anglophone audience ideally should read without considering they translate prior, non-English text
  • Foreignizing translations indexed amateurness, lack of source language mastery
  • Marked subtitles as awkward approximations of English rather than 'just' English

Theological Grounding of Domestication

Religious Justification

Representational burdens of translation intimately entwined to theological obligations

Core belief: Apprehending 'truth' of Islam predicated on making religion intelligible to those yet to embrace it

Qur'anic verse cited (14:4):

"And never have We sent forth any apostle otherwise than [with a message] in his own people's tongue, so that he might make [the truth] clear unto them"

Message of Islam could - indeed must - be conveyed within idioms of people it addressed to

Qur'anic verse on diversity (30:22):

"And among His wonders is the creation of the heavens and the earth, and the diversity of your tongues and colors..."

Human diversity, linguistic and otherwise, itself indicative of divinity

What mattered: Not particular form of language but what it communicated

Moral Thoughtfulness: Thinking Otherwise

To speak another language = understand another point of view, mode of life, see yourself as seen by others

This type of moral thoughtfulness or capacity to think otherwise crucial to becoming competent translator

Nawal's Insight

"In the beginning, you don't see the cultural other or the religious other, so you are speaking from your point of view alone. You are translating as if you are talking to yourself, to the people living here in Egypt with us."

Credited working with foreign editors: came saying didn't understand, so she'd rephrase, gained habit of asking "is this understandable? Will others get it?"

"Once I gained this awareness, I was willing to change and to think from others' point of view so that I can get my own message across better."

Assumption: Possibility of mutual comprehensibility enables criticism of miscomprehension or deliberate misrepresentation

Debating Translation Strategies: Internal Dissent

Postcolonial Critique from Within

Small group of translators at Iqraa did NOT always agree with centre's official preference for domestication

Reasons echoed postcolonial critiques: Domestication injurious to source language-culture

One translator's argument:

"I am not going to use words to make it easier for the target culture at the expense of the source culture. We are going to present Islam the way it is, and if the viewer wants to know more, he has to do research, we are not going to pamper or spoon-feed him."

Analogy: Like watching physics program without understanding physics - that's too bad. If simplifying comes at expense of source [culture], that's a problem

The 'Isa vs Jesus Debate

Dissenting translator's example of distorting dangers of domestication:

"For example, translating sayyidna (our master) 'Isa as Jesus or the Messiah - these two names have specific connotations contradicting the fundamental Islamic conception of this person, our master 'Isa. Using Jesus or the Messiah confirms in a way [Christians'] distorted versions of this person. 'Isa is not Jesus - there is a big difference between the two. For them Jesus is the son of God, and this is certainly not what 'Isa was."

Danger: NOT that Qur'an or Islam would continue to be unfamiliar, but that distinctive theological claims - and therefore divine truth - would be grossly misrepresented through being rendered familiar

Problem: Using 'Jesus' for Qur'anic 'Isa would elide ways latter (son of Mary, prophet of God) very different from former in Islamic creed

Moral responsibility: Impart through subtitles correct religious knowledge

Domestication Champions' Response

Those championing domestication equally cared about fidelity to Islamic theology and accurate conveyance to others

But reasoned: Should be accomplished using terms and names recognizable to non-Muslims even as they're given new definitions and content in use

In other words: 'Jesus' could come to pragmatically signify Qur'anic 'Isa

Online Debate

Dissenting Translator

"I am tired pampering our viewers and spoon-feeding them. We should now IMPOSE our dear Arabic culture and help it muscle its way through to our audiences."

Linking use of Arabic linguistic forms (in transliteration) to transmission of Arabic culture itself, in danger of being lost to viewers through translation tailored to their own socio-linguistic references

Randa's Response

Made clear metapragmatic norms underlining domesticating translations:

"For communication to be successful, one needs to make sure to deliver clear messages. If we are to propagate Islam, we need to try to speak in the tongue of those we are trying to present the message of Islam to. All prophets spoke the tongue of the peoples they preached to, which I believe is the right approach. Communication is not a fight, where one tries to impose on another, it's a genuine attempt to be understood."

Parallel: Task of translator similar to task of preachers they subtitled

  • Preacher translated Qur'anic stories into vernacular idiom (e.g., colloquial Egyptian)
  • Similarly, domestication aimed to create 'shared ground of experience' (Tarek Shamma 2009)

Language, correctly manipulated, creates world in common

Problem with foreignization: Rendered it mere medium for describing radically different world while ironically insisting on irreducible relationship between meaning and form

Ta'arruf: Mutual Knowing

Qur'anic Verse (49:13)

"[We] have made you into nations and tribes so that you might come to know one another..."

For domesticating translators:

  • Linguistic diversity compelled particular ethical orientation toward others
  • One of ta'arruf (mutual knowing)
  • Intercultural communication = practice valued by God
  • Up to humans to learn each other's languages to make it happen

NOT ecumenism:

  • Shared ground did NOT operate whereby all religious traditions 'equally true' or essentially same
  • Translators regarded historical religion of Islam as final revealed and most perfected
  • Took religious difference quite seriously
  • BUT: God does not will all humans be Muslim
  • Must find way to act in common despite not practicing/believing in common

Using religious language/imaginaries to meet ethical standards held in common despite religious difference

Second Part of Ta'arruf Verse

"...Verily, the noblest of you in the sight of God is the one of who is most deeply conscious of Him"

Significance for translators:

  • Comprehensibility and mutual knowing = divinely obligated goods in themselves
  • Also ends to establishing right conduct fostered by God-consciousness
  • Cultivation of such consciousness hinged on knowing 'true message' of Islam
  • Meant at times taking directly on others' - including Muslims' - distortions of it

Critical Translation: Translating as Critique

Creating 'Parallel Programs'

Original Arabic vs English-Subtitled

Subtitling practices created what translators described as 'parallel programs':

  1. Original Arabic-language programme
  2. English-subtitled one

Only possible - and morally responsible - because of:

  • What original text was (religious knowledge)
  • Who was doing translating (pious Muslims with religious knowledge)

Specific nature gave translators moral responsibility lacking if translating 'ordinary' (non-religious) content

Example: Marriage and Love

Nawal's Dilemma

Episode content: Preacher listing reasons to marry, noting love least important

Nawal's reaction: "Westerners are going to hear this talk and think that Muslims are mutakhlifin (backwards)"

Important context:

  • Nawal married husband of 35 years not because in love (although liked him)
  • Because he was first-cousin, kind, honest, would take good care of family
  • Quite happy with marriage, spoke highly of husband
  • Hesitancy NOT because own ideals/experiences contradicted preacher's proposal
  • But because contradicted norms of target culture
  • Or rather confirmed them regarding what Muslims like when it comes to spousal intimacy

Shifting Focus: Translator vs Content

Question: Is accommodation to Western cultural values only way to understand this textual intervention?

Alternative: Shift focus from inequality of languages/life-worlds to position of translator herself vis-à-vis specific content she's translating

Preacher's Elaboration

  • Man should be especially grief-stricken at wife's death
  • Because major source of his hasinat (divine recompense for good deeds) eliminated
  • Explanation: Women weak, need husbands to look after them, causing latter to accrue hasinat

Nawal's Response

  • Emphatically disagreed with characterization of women as always weak/dependent
  • Dismissed as Islamically incorrect
  • Also worried: dovetailed with Western stereotypes about Muslim women or what Muslim men thought of women

Final Subtitle

Used time-space of preacher's utterance about wifely death and hasinat to write:

Women enjoy a special status in Islam.
Those who support them receive God's mercy.

Analysis:

  • Nothing doctrinally incorrect
  • Women DO hold 'special status' (preacher's point)
  • However: while he provided particularities (women weaker, more dependent), English translation deliberately ambiguous about what makes women 'special'
  • All gender referents dropped (choice of 'those')

Self-Authorization as Proxy Preacher

Basis of Authority

Like most translators, Nawal's self-authorization as proxy preacher stemmed from:

  • Self-identification as pious Muslim
  • In no need for religious guidance offered by most Iqraa programmes/preachers
  • Held many preachers in low regard, citing:
    • Basic mistakes in Qur'anic recitation
    • Dubious commentaries on Qur'anic verses
    • Faulty citation of Prophetic sayings with weak chains of transmission
  • Told family members: always take anything heard from TV preachers with large grain of salt

Paradoxically: Content required great deliberation and care precisely because dubious while categorized as religious

Also: Intended audience (both Arabic original and English translation) imagined as religious neophytes in great need of basic Islamic knowledge

Religious vs Technical Texts

Key Distinction

Technical Text (e.g., computer/medical) Religious Text (preaching programmes)
Translate with total 'faithfulness' to source text Not 'literally' translate but attempt to understand meaning behind speaker's words
Without consideration/attention to accuracy of what actually said Rephrase in manner appropriate for target audience
Source text subject Meaning NOT fixed by intentions of speaker
- Rather by intentions of God and Prophet, whose words both preacher and translator interpreting
No moral responsibility Translator morally responsible before God

Speech Not Window onto Individual Subjectivity

In this specific context:

  • Speech NOT window onto internal, individual subjectivity
  • Original text NOT original Arabic broadcast
  • Rather 'Message of Islam' as interpreted by each translator
  • Consequently: Fidelity to God at times necessitated betrayal of preacher
  • Form: Textual interventions bypassing preacher's Arabic speech
  • Tacitly create new, more correctly Islamic, programme through English subtitles

Translation = not only interpretive but practice of critique

Dual Critique

External Critique

  • Aimed to challenge non-Muslims' criticisms of Islam
  • Show religion's true nature to imagined audience of religious neophytes

Internal Critique

  • Directed at other Muslims' (the preachers) interpretations of Islam

In both cases: Critique conducted from within normative theological terrain of Islamic tradition

Fine-Grained Distinctions

General Norm with Contextual Applications

Religious programmes different from technical ones because translators morally responsible for dissemination through subtitles in qualitatively different way - responsible before God

Translator as last person having altered words before scale of audience widened = person most responsible even as original continued to simultaneously exist as Arabic speech

Example Distinctions

Book on atheism by atheist:

  • Should be 'faithfully' translated
  • Muslim's intention in reading: find out how atheists think to more effectively debunk them

Islamic outreach channel:

  • Different translation strategy
  • Viewers tuned in to learn about Islam but didn't know much
  • Invested in authority accrued to Islamic preachers by being on TV
  • Translator responsible to ensure what promoted as 'Islam' on screen in fact Islamic
  • Only had this responsibility because possessed requisite religious knowledge to critically evaluate preacher
  • What preacher saying = itself interpretation of God's words revealed in Qur'an

Tafsir and Tarjama: Interpretation and Translation

Brett Wilson (2014): Historical Relationship

13th-century Arabic dictionary Lisan al-Arab:

  • Translator = mutarjim
  • Defined as 'interpreter of language', mufassir al-lisan (literally 'of tongues')

In Muslim contexts:

  • Practices of interpretation (tafsir) and translation (tarjama) defined one another
  • Often difficult to distinguish

Consequences for Qur'anic translations in modern period:

  • Islamic scholars grounded legal reasoning behind permissibility of Qur'anic translation in equivalence between tafsir and tarjama
  • Both carried similar risk of error
  • Both interpreter and translator must make discretionary choice about apt meaning
  • "Maybe the chosen meaning is what God intended and maybe it is not" (Wilson 2014: 211)

Within Islamic TV subtitling:

  • Dynamic unfolds beyond actual words of Qur'an to words ABOUT Qur'an
  • Relevant question: NOT degree of equivalent correspondence of translation to original text (preaching programme)
  • More its conformity to God's will as interpreted by each translator through mediation of Qur'anic revelation

This theory made practice a space of argument and debate:

  • To use Talal Asad's (1995) terms: not only ABOUT text but WITH text

Other-Worldly Consequences

Yara's Parsing of Translatable vs Untranslatable

Translation choices had personal other-worldly consequences, at times determinative of what was/wasn't translatable

Religiously Incorrect as Opinion

"Let's say a preacher says that the hijab (head-scarf) is just a cultural habit and is not a religious obligation. That is of course false. But I will translate what he said and make sure to put some linguistic markers around the statement [for example prefacing the statement with 'I believe'] so that the viewers understand the statement as one person's opinion."

Religiously Incorrect as Ruling

"But let's say the preacher talks about an issue as if it is a religious ruling - for example, he says a fiancé has the right to see his betrothed's hair. I can't translate that at all."

Why difference?

  • Like hijab statement, contradicted consensus among Islamic scholars
  • UNLIKE first statement, this NOT just (albeit falsely) descriptive
  • Also potentially prescriptive
  • Yara couldn't translate because doing so would make her morally liable for sin incurred by viewer who then demands to see fiancée's hair

Other Translation Purposes

Beyond Subverting Preachers

Translators NOT continually attempting to subvert Islamic preachers' intended messages

Various purposes:

  • 'Technical' manipulation (time-space constraints, formal linguistic properties of Arabic)
  • Keeping viewers' attention for entire duration

Dealing with 'Fickle' Audiences

Assumed viewers would quickly switch channel if found programme boring

Key job: Produce stimulating copy, could mean departing from what speaker actually saying

Repetition Problem

  • Older preachers reiterated key points/details multiple times (common rhetorical technique in religious lesson - dars din)
  • Translators described as 'tedious' for non-Arabs
  • On 3rd/4th repetition: ignored what preacher saying, introduced new material
    • Vivid detail to Qur'anic story they knew independently
    • Summary of moral point (qima) of story preacher may never have mentioned

Example: Mosque Urination Incident

Episode: Saudi preacher discussing incident during Prophet's lifetime where man urinated inside mosque

Preacher described: In minute detail steps Prophet took to purify mosque

Yara's subtitle instead:

This incident shows us that
We need to have patience with those who are
not yet aware of the proper comportment in a mosque.

Yara's explanation:

  • Preacher correctly assuming Muslim audience in Arab countries would know story + ethical virtue (patience) it exemplifies
  • Western audience would have no idea
  • Might conclude story actually just about how to properly clean mosque urinated in
  • Since subtitling for audience with very little contextual knowledge, expressed frustration by 'laziness' of TV preachers
  • Especially Saudi preachers who sometimes don't finish sentence assuming viewers could fill in blanks correctly

Other Interventions

Types of Corrections

  • Correcting in subtitles Prophetic saying or Qur'anic verse preacher mangled
  • Correcting dates or place names
  • Attenuating empirically dubious assertions (e.g., way global financial markets work)

Example: Western Savings Law

Preacher's claim: Law in Western country stipulating state has right to seize citizen's savings after exceeding certain amount

  • Muslim living in Western country told preacher government takes bank savings after threshold
  • That's why Westerners go on five-star luxury vacations - want to spend surplus before government seizes it

Randa's reaction: "Laughable, embarrassing! What am I going to do? I told them [Saudi management] we shouldn't subtitle this preacher's programmes, he is idiotic, but no one listens."

Solution: Changed 'Western countries' to 'some non-Muslim countries', hoping viewers think preacher referring to defunct communist regimes

Editors vs Translators: Different Epistemologies

Editors' Stance

  • Argued passionately ABOUT programmes (right/wrong, boring/compelling)
  • Did NOT argue WITH them (responding to content with new content for same screen)
  • Preaching programmes = original source text to be faithfully publicly conveyed
  • Even if privately lampooned
  • Operating through epistemology: translations as replacements of original
  • Presumption: stability of meaning across movement from one linguistic code to another
  • Anxiety about manipulating subtitles: English translation stood on own precisely through standing in for original Arabic
  • Both had to mean same thing
  • Understanding of individual authorship and autonomy

Egyptian Translators' Stance

  • Subtitles had authorial autonomy
  • Limited NOT by autonomy of preacher
  • But by autonomous authority of divine revelation
  • Original Arabic programme merely interpretation of real original source texts
  • Real original: Qur'an and its embodiment in Prophetic example

Conclusion

Summary of Findings

Subtitling practices at Iqraa worked through complex and perceptive appraisal by Egyptian translators of:

  • Representational burdens engendered by global circulation of subtitles
  • Theological burdens engendered by global circulation of subtitles

Appraisal informed by:

  • Perceived needs of audience overwhelmingly imagined as Western (even if Muslim)
  • General principle to transmit only correct religious knowledge

Fidelity to God could and did demand betrayal of preacher being subtitled

Original Arabic programme in end, like its subtitles, merely interpretation of ultimate source-text of divine revelation

Translation as Dual Critique

Within this setting, translation was:

  1. Enabling of critiques of external representations of Islam
  2. Itself a form of internal critique directed at other Muslims

Stakes of translation:

  • NOT just getting it right linguistically or pragmatically
  • Getting it right by God
  • For which translators as individuals would be held accountable in Hereafter

But: What God wants and how to best convey this to others deeply contested:

  • From within Islamic tradition as lived/understood by Egyptian participants in country's Islamic revival
  • From without by foreigners they interacted with

Struggle of Epistemologies

Assumptions enabling these translation strategies as moral practice NOT shared by most foreign editors who:

  • Also adopted evaluative stance toward preaching programmes
  • Hewed to different notion of authorial intention
  • Associated different translation ethic

Subtitling in Islamic television thus became site of struggle between:

  • Competing epistemologies of translation and mediation
  • Competing epistemologies of critique

Future Research Directions

  • Ethnographic fieldwork on reception of subtitled programmes by viewers
  • Comparative research on subtitling practices across religious and secular channels in Arab world
  • Theorizations of work and effects of translation beyond impasses of:
    • Commensurability and incommensurability
    • Domestication and foreignization
