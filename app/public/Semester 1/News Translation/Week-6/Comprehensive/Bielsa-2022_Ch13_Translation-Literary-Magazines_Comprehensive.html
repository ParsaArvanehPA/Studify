Translation in Literary Magazines

Authors: Diana Roig-Sanz, Laura Fólica, and Ventsislav Ikoff

Source: Bielsa, Esperança (Ed.). (2022). The Routledge Handbook of Translation and Globalization, Chapter 13

Topic: Periodical Publications and Big Translation History

Study Focus: Translation studies, periodical publications, digital humanities, transnational networks

Introduction: The Importance of Periodical Publications in Translation Studies

This chapter explores the critical role of periodical publications—magazines, journals, and newspapers—in understanding translation practices and cultural exchange. While translation studies has historically focused on book-length works, periodicals represent a vital yet understudied domain where translation operated as a fundamental mediating force in cultural globalization.

Central Argument

Periodical publications functioned as privileged sites for translation, serving as laboratories for literary experimentation, vehicles for cultural transfer, and platforms for building transnational intellectual networks. Understanding translation in periodicals requires methodological innovation and digital approaches.

The chapter draws on the MapModern project (Mapping Hispanic/Lusophone Modernity, 1898-1959) as a case study to demonstrate how digital humanities methods can illuminate the role of translation in periodical publications and contribute to what the authors call "big translation history" (BTH).

Theoretical Framework: Translation Studies and Periodical Publications

The Periodical Turn in Translation Studies

Recent decades have witnessed a "periodical turn" in translation studies, recognizing that magazines and journals were not mere repositories of translated texts but active agents in shaping literary and cultural modernity. This shift requires scholars to move beyond traditional bibliographic approaches.

Bielsa & Bassnett (2009) emphasized that translation in news and media contexts operates differently from literary book translation, requiring attention to institutional contexts, temporal constraints, and collective production processes.

Schäffner (2012) highlighted the need to analyze translation within broader communicative events and institutional frameworks, particularly in media and journalistic contexts.

Big Translation History (BTH)

Big Translation History (BTH)

BTH represents a methodological approach that combines large-scale data analysis with qualitative interpretation. It seeks to identify patterns, networks, and trends across extensive corpora of translated texts while remaining attentive to specific historical and cultural contexts. BTH is enabled by digital humanities tools and databases.

The BTH approach challenges the traditional focus on individual translators, canonical texts, or isolated translation events. Instead, it emphasizes:

  • Systematic analysis of large corpora of translations
  • Network analysis to map relationships between actors, institutions, and texts
  • Quantitative patterns combined with qualitative interpretation
  • Transnational and transcultural perspectives
  • Digital tools for data visualization and analysis

World Literature and Translation

Casanova (1999, 2002) conceptualized "world literary space" as an unequal field structured by power relations between dominant and dominated languages/literatures. Translation serves as a mechanism of consecration, through which peripheral literatures gain recognition in central literary markets.

Venuti (1995, 2008) introduced the concepts of domestication and foreignization as translation strategies. Domestication assimilates foreign texts to target culture norms, while foreignization preserves source culture markers, potentially disrupting target culture assumptions.

Translation Strategy Characteristics Cultural Function
Domestication Adapts foreign text to target culture norms, values, and linguistic conventions Facilitates readability; may erase cultural difference
Foreignization Preserves source culture markers, linguistic peculiarities, and cultural references Challenges target culture; promotes cultural diversity

Periodicals as Translation Sites

Distinctive Features of Periodical Translation

Translation in periodicals differs significantly from book translation in several respects:

Temporal Dynamics: Periodicals operate on cyclical publication schedules (weekly, monthly, quarterly), creating different temporal pressures and opportunities for translators compared to book publishing.

Collective Production: Periodical translation often involves multiple actors—editors, translators, publishers, contributors—working collaboratively within institutional frameworks.

Paratextual Framing: Periodicals provide rich paratextual materials (editorials, notes, illustrations, advertisements) that frame translated texts and shape their reception.

Hybridity: Periodicals mix genres, languages, and cultural materials, creating hybrid textual spaces where translation operates alongside original production.

Functions of Translation in Periodicals

  1. Cultural Mediation: Periodicals introduced foreign literatures, ideas, and aesthetic movements to local audiences, functioning as cultural mediators.
  2. Literary Experimentation: Magazines served as laboratories for testing new literary forms, styles, and techniques through translation.
  3. Network Building: Translation in periodicals created transnational networks of writers, intellectuals, and cultural producers.
  4. Canon Formation: Editorial selection of texts for translation influenced emerging literary canons and hierarchies.
  5. Political Intervention: Periodical translation could serve political, ideological, or activist purposes, positioning publications within broader cultural debates.

Ehrlicher (2020) argued that periodicals were crucial platforms for "intercultural transfer," enabling circulation of ideas and aesthetic innovations across linguistic and national boundaries.

Methodological Challenges in Studying Periodical Translation

The Problem of Scale and Accessibility

Studying translation in periodicals presents significant methodological challenges:

Archival Dispersal: Periodicals are often scattered across multiple archives, libraries, and collections, making comprehensive study difficult.

Digitization Gaps: Many periodicals remain undigitized or poorly indexed, limiting researchers' access to primary sources.

Attribution Problems: Translations in periodicals often lack clear attribution, making it difficult to identify translators and trace translation networks.

Fragmentary Evidence: Individual issues may be missing; complete runs of periodicals are rare, creating gaps in the historical record.

Digital Humanities Solutions

Digital humanities methods offer potential solutions to these challenges:

  • Database Creation: Structured databases enable systematic cataloging of periodical contents, including translated texts.
  • Network Visualization: Digital tools can map relationships between translators, authors, publications, and cultural institutions.
  • Text Analysis: Computational methods can identify patterns in translation practices across large corpora.
  • Geo-Spatial Mapping: Geographic information systems (GIS) can visualize circulation routes and cultural geography of periodicals.
  • Metadata Standards: Developing metadata standards facilitates interoperability between different periodical databases.

Case Study: The MapModern Project

MapModern: Mapping Hispanic/Lusophone Modernity (1898-1959)

The MapModern project represents a collaborative digital humanities initiative designed to map the role of translation in Hispanic and Lusophone periodical cultures during the period 1898-1959. The project builds a comprehensive database of periodical publications, translated texts, cultural agents, and transnational networks.

Project Objectives

MapModern pursues several interconnected objectives:

  1. Comprehensive Cataloging: Creating a database of Hispanic and Lusophone periodicals that published translated literature.
  2. Translation Mapping: Identifying and cataloging translated texts, including source languages, authors, translators, and publication contexts.
  3. Network Analysis: Mapping relationships between cultural agents (translators, editors, writers) and institutions (publishing houses, cultural organizations).
  4. Circulation Studies: Tracing how translated texts circulated across different periodicals, regions, and language zones.
  5. Comparative Analysis: Enabling comparison of translation practices across different periodicals, periods, and cultural contexts.

Methodological Approach

Mixed Methods: Combining Distant and Close Reading

MapModern employs a mixed-methods approach that combines "distant reading" (large-scale quantitative analysis) with "close reading" (detailed qualitative interpretation of specific texts and contexts). This combination allows researchers to identify broad patterns while remaining attentive to particular historical and cultural circumstances.

Database Structure

The MapModern database is structured around several key entities:

Entity Type Description Key Attributes
Periodicals Magazines, journals, newspapers Title, publication dates, location, language, editorial line
Translated Texts Individual translations published in periodicals Source author, translator, source language, genre, publication date
Agents Translators, editors, writers Name, nationality, biographical data, roles, affiliations
Institutions Publishing houses, cultural organizations Name, location, period of activity, political/cultural orientation

Data Collection and Curation

Data collection involves several stages:

  • Archival Research: Systematic examination of periodical archives and collections.
  • Bibliographic Review: Consultation of existing bibliographies and catalogs.
  • Digital Scanning: Digitization of periodical issues when possible.
  • Metadata Creation: Structured description of periodicals and translated texts using standardized metadata schemas.
  • Data Validation: Cross-checking and verification of data through multiple sources.

Key Findings from MapModern

Translation as Network Building

Network analysis reveals that translation in periodicals was not a random or isolated phenomenon but operated through structured networks of cultural agents and institutions. Certain translators functioned as "cultural mediators," bridging different linguistic and cultural zones.

"The mapping of translation networks demonstrates that cultural exchange was mediated by specific individuals who occupied strategic positions within transnational intellectual communities. These mediators were often multilingual, mobile, and connected to multiple periodical projects simultaneously."

Geographic Patterns of Translation Flow

Geo-spatial analysis reveals asymmetrical patterns of translation flow:

Center-Periphery Dynamics: Translation flows were structured by power relations between cultural centers (Paris, Madrid, Buenos Aires) and peripheral regions, confirming Casanova's world literary space model.

South-South Connections: However, the data also reveals significant "South-South" translation connections that bypassed European centers, suggesting more complex circulation patterns than simple center-periphery models.

Temporal Patterns: Periodization of Translation Activity

Quantitative analysis identifies distinct periods of translation activity, often corresponding to broader political, cultural, or technological changes:

  • 1898-1918: Modernist experimentation; high volume of French-to-Spanish translation.
  • 1918-1939: Interwar avant-gardes; increased translation from multiple European languages; rise of political/ideological translation.
  • 1939-1959: Post-war period; shifts in translation patterns due to political censorship and exile communities.

Genre and Translation

Analysis of translated genres reveals that periodicals favored certain forms:

Genre Frequency Interpretation
Poetry High Shorter forms suited to periodical format; symbolist and modernist poetry particularly prevalent
Short Fiction High Practicality of length; experimental short fiction as laboratory for literary innovation
Essays/Criticism Medium Translation of critical texts to introduce foreign aesthetic theories and intellectual trends
Serialized Novels Lower Commitment required for serialization; some periodicals specialized in this form

Transnational Networks and Cultural Transfer

The Concept of Transnational Cultural Networks

Transnational Networks

Transnational networks refer to structured relationships among cultural agents (writers, translators, editors, publishers) that transcend national boundaries. These networks facilitated circulation of texts, ideas, and aesthetic practices across linguistic and cultural zones. Periodicals functioned as material infrastructures supporting these networks.

Types of Network Actors

MapModern research identifies several types of actors within transnational networks:

Cultural Mediators: Multilingual individuals who translated, edited, and facilitated connections between different linguistic communities. Examples include translators who worked across multiple periodicals and language zones.

Institutional Brokers: Publishers, cultural organizations, and editorial collectives that provided infrastructure for translation and cultural exchange.

Exile Communities: Political exiles who established periodicals in host countries, often engaging in translation as a means of cultural preservation and political resistance.

Network Analysis Techniques

Digital network analysis enables visualization and analysis of relationships:

  • Node-and-Edge Graphs: Represent actors as nodes and relationships (co-publication, translation) as edges.
  • Centrality Measures: Identify key actors based on number of connections, bridging positions, or influence.
  • Community Detection: Algorithmic identification of clusters or sub-networks within larger networks.
  • Temporal Network Analysis: Tracking how networks evolve over time.

Challenges and Future Directions

Ongoing Methodological Challenges

Data Incompleteness: Even comprehensive projects like MapModern face limitations due to incomplete archival records, missing issues, and undigitized materials.

Attribution and Anonymity: Many translations in periodicals were unsigned or pseudonymous, making it difficult to establish authorship and trace translator networks.

Multilingual Challenges: Working across multiple languages (Spanish, Portuguese, Catalan, Galician) requires multilingual research teams and raises questions about comparison and standardization.

Balancing Scale and Specificity: Big data approaches risk losing sight of specific historical contexts, translation strategies, and individual agency.

Future Research Directions

The chapter concludes by identifying several promising directions for future research:

  1. Interoperability: Developing standards to enable different periodical databases to communicate and share data.
  2. Comparative Studies: Extending the approach to other linguistic zones and historical periods to enable comparative analysis.
  3. Computational Text Analysis: Applying natural language processing and machine learning to analyze translation strategies at scale.
  4. Reception Studies: Moving beyond production to study how translated texts in periodicals were received by readers.
  5. Material Culture: Attending to material dimensions of periodicals—design, illustration, typography—and their relationship to translation.

Summary and Conclusion

This chapter makes a compelling case for the importance of studying translation in periodical publications and demonstrates the value of digital humanities methodologies for translation history. Several key arguments emerge:

Central Conclusions

  1. Periodicals as Translation Sites: Magazines and journals were not merely vehicles for translated texts but active agents in shaping cultural modernity, functioning as laboratories for experimentation and platforms for transnational exchange.
  2. Need for Methodological Innovation: Traditional bibliographic and textual methods are insufficient for studying translation in periodicals at scale. Digital humanities tools enable researchers to identify patterns, map networks, and analyze large corpora systematically.
  3. Big Translation History: Combining quantitative analysis ("distant reading") with qualitative interpretation ("close reading") offers a productive approach to translation history, revealing both broad patterns and specific contexts.
  4. Transnational Networks: Translation in periodicals operated through structured transnational networks of cultural mediators and institutions, challenging nation-centered literary histories.
  5. Complexity of Cultural Exchange: MapModern findings reveal that cultural exchange was more complex than simple center-periphery models suggest, with multiple circulation routes and South-South connections.

The chapter's contribution extends beyond Hispanic and Lusophone contexts, offering a model for studying translation in periodicals across different linguistic zones and historical periods. The methodological innovations demonstrated by the MapModern project—database construction, network analysis, geo-spatial mapping—are transferable to other research contexts.

However, the authors acknowledge significant challenges: data incompleteness, attribution problems, the difficulty of balancing scale with specificity, and the need for multilingual expertise. Future research must address these challenges while extending the approach to new contexts and questions.

Ultimately, the chapter demonstrates that periodicals deserve central attention in translation studies. By combining archival research with digital methods, scholars can illuminate how translation operated as a fundamental force in cultural globalization, shaping literary modernity and building transnational intellectual communities.

"Understanding translation in periodicals requires us to think beyond individual texts and translators, examining instead the complex networks, institutions, and circulation patterns that made cultural exchange possible. Digital humanities tools enable this shift in scale and perspective, opening new possibilities for translation history."
