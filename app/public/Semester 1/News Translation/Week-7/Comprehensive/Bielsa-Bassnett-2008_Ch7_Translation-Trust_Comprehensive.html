Translation and Trust

Authors: Esperanza Bielsa & Susan Bassnett
Source: Translation in Global News (2008), Chapter 7
Focus: Veracity, Authenticity, and Manipulation in News Translation

Central Paradox

We cling to the importance of truthfulness in news reporting, yet news production processes are inherently multiple, complex, and manipulative. When news is transposed interlingually, the question becomes: to what extent can any translation be said to be a truthful, accurate rendering?

1. Truthfulness and News Reporting

The Fundamental Premise

News transmission rests on assumption that it should be truthful. Readers/viewers expect honest relay of information. When proven false, public outrage ensues.

Example: British Newspaper Editor Resignation (2004)

Editor forced to resign after authorizing publication of photographs purporting to show British soldiers abusing prisoners in Iraq - turned out to be fakes.

Reality of News Production

The end product that is read or seen has been:

  • Shaped for receivers
  • Mediated through several sets of filters:
    • Time/space constraints
    • Editorial policy
    • Cultural acceptability
    • Market positioning
  • Manipulated - multiplicity accentuated when news transposed interlingually

2. Skopos Theory and News Translation

Why Skopos Theory Works for News

Skopos theory (Vermeer) is one of the most useful approaches for news translation because it is premised on notion of equivalent effect, NOT on any notion of exact textual transference.

Limitations

Has limitations when comparing how same translated news story is presented in different newspapers. Divergence of presentational aspects takes us beyond interlingual into other textual manipulation in target culture.

Alberto Orengo (2005)

Contribution: Theory of 'localization' for examining how news items are shaped for what he calls 'sub-locales' in Italian newspapers

Key insight: Ideological constraints come to fore; different conventions operate not only across countries but within each country (huge divergencies driven by market and politics)

Difficulty in Analyzing News Translation

  • Different conventions in different countries
  • Huge divergencies within each country (market and politically driven)
  • Yet: common threads discernible in story presentation
  • Commonality driven by accessibility to shared VISUAL IMAGES, rather than common verbal elements

3. Case Study: Saddam Hussein - Capture and Trial

3.1 The Capture (December 2003)

Visual Narrative: From Powerful Dictator to Abject Prisoner

Context

Saddam Hussein captured after Allied troops defeated his forces. War degenerated into occupation with spiraling violence.

The Defining Image: Dental Examination

Particularly striking photograph showed Saddam undergoing intrusive dental examination immediately after arrest.

Multiple Connotations of This Degrading Image:

  1. Emphasized fall from high position to present powerlessness
  2. Suggested reduction to level little more than animal
  3. Conversely: implied captors were humane people concerned about his welfare

Effect: Stressed abjectness of Saddam Hussein + implicit suggestion of being well-cared for by captors. Image became narrative in itself, setting tone for what followed.

3.2 First Court Appearance (July 2004)

The Clothing Theme: Cross-Cultural Thread

Daily Telegraph (2 July 2004)

Half-page photograph of Saddam "slimmer and healthier," wearing "grey pinstriped jacket, brown trousers and shiny black shoes"

Corriere della Sera (19 October 2005 - trial start)

Headline: "Vestito scuro e il Corano tra le mani" (Dark suit and Koran in his hand)

Dark suit denotes elegance/sobriety; Koran presented as prop to reinforce image of refinement/sincerity

Le Figaro (Same Date)

"Vétu d'un costume sombre, le col de sa chemise blanche ouvert, Saddam Hussein, les cheveux mi-longs mais soigneusement teints, la barbe poivre et sel et les traits tirés, fait ses 68 ans."

'Soigneusement teints' (expertly dyed) implies vanity

The Times (30 November)

Story about Saddam's tailor, Recep Cesur, a Kurd from Istanbul who "has become the style guru to Middle East," supplying 50 new suits every second month

Analysis of Clothing Theme

  • Cannot be traced to single source - cannot be said to have been directly translated
  • Surfaced repeatedly as trope
  • Common means of signaling gap between appearances and reality
  • Used as instrument of ridicule
  • Shows first stage in theatricalization of sensational trial

3.3 The Sun's Theatrical Presentation

Front page prefigured theatricalization in layout:

  • Two-thirds of page: photograph of unkempt, staring-eyed newly captured dictator
  • Block capitals: "SADDAM CAPTURED"
  • Main headline (whole left side): "WE GOT HIM"
  • Above: familiar public address phrase "Ladies and gentlemen..."

Effect: "WE GOT HIM" became "Ladies and gentlemen, we got him" - rhetorical device creating slightly comic tone. The Sun effectively presenting Saddam to public like old music-hall presenters introducing next act.

4. Determining Sincerity: Theoretical Framework

Martin Montgomery's Work on Princess Diana's Death (1997)

Case Study: Three High-Profile BBC Broadcasts

  1. Tony Blair (6 hours after news):
    • Television interview, open air, no notes, direct to camera
    • Voice trembling and hesitant with emotion
    • Invented term "the people's princess"
  2. The Queen (5 September):
    • Broadcast from Buckingham Palace using teleprompter
    • Composed, speaking clearly and fluently
    • Speaking as "your Queen and as a grandmother"
  3. Earl Spencer (Diana's brother at funeral):
    • Pledged "blood family" would raise her sons as she wished
    • Appeared on verge of breaking down in tears

Montgomery's Analysis

Examined rhetorical devices: hyperbole, repetition patterns, pairings ("your Queen and a grandmother"), but above all patterns of intonation and body language

Findings on Perceived Sincerity

  • Most sincere: Earl Spencer (marked signs of personal emotion)
  • Least sincere: Queen (emotional understatement, formal presentation)
  • Despite Queen's language containing "I say from my heart" and "I admired and respected her"

Habermas' Validity Claims

Three Criteria for Valid Speech

Montgomery's theoretical starting point: Habermas' discussion of validity claims

Criterion How Validated Characteristics
Truth Through negotiation in discourse Propositional accuracy
Appropriateness Through negotiation in discourse Contextual suitability
Sincerity Taken on trust; validated by speaker's behavior Requires faith in speaker

Montgomery's argument: Speech in public sphere projects itself as valid by being perceived as true, sincere, or appropriate (or one or other of these three).

Speech Acts and Sincerity

Montgomery (1999: 21) on Different Speech Acts:

"Speech acts such as complimenting, apologizing and promising may be seen as routinely implicating sincerity. But speech acts such as commanding, requesting, passing sentence and excommunicating do not. A judge, for instance, may direct a trial jury to acquit but has to pass sentence on a defendant if a guilty verdict is reached: the sentence will be passed effectively, irrespective of the judge's emotional commitment to it. Promising, on the other hand, includes sincerity as a condition of its satisfactory performance."

Authentic Talk in the Public Sphere

Montgomery (2001: 404) - Later Essay

Focus: "Authentic talk" in public sphere

Note: Broadcast talk normally staged for performance in some way

Conclusion: It is neither authority of speaker nor any sense of source being "authorized" that determines whether broadcast will be perceived as truthful, but rather "the nature and the manner of the talk itself that makes for compelling testimony"

Implication

Validity of speech act assessed according to:

  • Diverse sign systems
  • Contexts in which it is made and received

Key Insight: Framing and Context Matter

"The human significance of speech resides not just in the way it means something propositionally in the here and now, but also in the ways it can be deemed valid (or invalid) in the here and now" (Montgomery, 1999: 12)

Example: In Diana tributes, speeches made immediately after death (Blair) or at funeral (Spencer) conveyed greater sense of validation than Queen's more formal broadcast after several days of media speculation about Buckingham Palace silence.

Conclusion: Assessments of authenticity are both subjective and conditioned by context as well as body language, intonation, and vocabulary choice.

5. Theatricalizing the News

Performance vs Broadcast News

Actors' Manipulation of Responses

Actors understand and manipulate responses accordingly

Key Difference:

Broadcast News Interview Piece of Theatre
Deemed more authentic because more spontaneous Conveys sense of authenticity in situation everyone knows is contrived

Despite difference: Tools for analyzing broadcast news (Montgomery) and those for analyzing performance are very similar - both are complex semiotic systems that audiences must decode.

Transcription and Loss of Dimension

When Exchanges Are Transcribed and Published

Issues of veracity and authenticity become more fuzzy because physical dimension is removed:

What We Lose:

  • Body language
  • Changes of tone of voice
  • Pace of conversation
  • Original context

Nevertheless: Habermas' tripartite distinction (truth, appropriateness, sincerity) can still serve when examining such texts, particularly with substantial use of direct quotes or dramatic text layout.

Dramatic Text Format in News

Process of Theatricalization

When interviews/debates/courtroom scenes laid out as though they were dramatic work, with names of speakers before each utterance:

What happens: Real-life exchange written up as though it were piece of theatre

Paradox:

Theatre perceived as intrinsically less authentic than news reporting, yet newspapers choose to set down events in dramatic text format

Why it deserves critical attention: Strategy utilized for particular end

Usage in Saddam trial: Dramatic text layout used frequently in reporting of trial

6. Veracity and Domestication: Comparing Transcripts

6.1 The Problem of Exact Translation

Impossibility of Exact Translation

No translation can ever reproduce in identical format something written in another language - no two languages have identical semantic and syntactic patterns.

Example from Le Figaro:

"Saddam Hussein … fait ses 68 ans"

Idiomatic expression translating as "looked all of his 68 years"

Has equivalent effect (Vermeer's skopos model) but by no means exact word-for-word translation

Central Question for News Translation

Variation is inevitable aspect of translation, but question always present: Is there a limit to extent of possible variation?

If variation is extreme that there is no trace of source text, can this still be said to be translation?

At heart of any discussion of news translation: Often only traceable source is an event, not any single identifiable textual account of that event. Source material is synthesized even as it is transferred across languages, then adjusted stylistically according to target culture norms.

6.2 July 2004 Court Transcripts: Independent vs Daily Telegraph

Publication of Transcripts

British papers carried transcripts of relatively brief appearance before judge - curiously theatrical gesture

Intention:

  • Convey heightened sense of veracity
  • Invite readers to believe they're reading straightforward, unadulterated account

Paradox:

Given theatricalization from moment of first images, publication of transcripts also served to enhance soap opera effect

Daily Telegraph: "Saddam was not going to leave the stage without saying his piece" - theatrical dimension highlighted

The Independent Version

Editorial Note (Independent, 2 July 2004):

"The following is an edited transcript of the translators' words as Saddam Hussein answered questions from judge Ra'id Juhi. Some parts of the conversation were not included in the original transcript at all."

Implication: During hearing, journalists producing their own versions; when over, texts amalgamated with official transcript which didn't contain all exchanges recorded collectively

Online version note: Microphone did not pick up everything

Sample Exchange from Independent:

SADDAM: ... Hussein Majid, the president of the Republic of Iraq.
JUDGE: Profession? Former president of the Republic of Iraq?
SADDAM: No, present. Current. It's the will of the people.
JUDGE: The head of the Baath Party that is dissolved, defunct. Former commander and chief of the army. Residence is Iraq. Your mother's name?
SADDAM: Sobha. You also have to introduce yourself to me.
JUDGE: Mr Saddam, I am the investigative judge of the central court of Iraq.
SADDAM: So that I have to know, you are an investigative judge of the central court of Iraq? What resolution, what law formed this court?
The judge's response could not be heard.

The Daily Telegraph Version

Editorial Note (Daily Telegraph):

"Saddam's courtroom exchanges with the judge yesterday included" - signaling pared-down edited version

Sample Exchange from Telegraph:

JUDGE: Are you the former president of Iraq?
SADDAM: I am Saddam Hussein, president of Iraq.
JUDGE (to court clerk): Put down 'former' in brackets.
SADDAM: I am the president of the republic so you should not strip me of my title to put me on trial.
JUDGE: You are the ex-leader of Iraq and the ex-leader of the dissolved armed forces. Were you the leader of the Ba'ath party and head of the armed forces?
SADDAM: Yes. I've introduced myself to you but you haven't introduced yourself to me. So who are you?
JUDGE: I am a judge of the criminal courts of Iraq.

Comparison and Analysis

Significant Differences Between Versions

Information Supplied:

  • Independent includes mother's name question (not in Telegraph)
  • Telegraph has judge instructing court clerk (not in Independent)
  • Different amounts of detail throughout

Structure of Questioning:

  • Judge in Independent states facts (more authoritative)
  • Judge in Telegraph asks questions (less authoritative)

Judge's Self-Identification:

  • Independent: "investigative judge of the central court of Iraq"
  • Telegraph: "a judge of the criminal courts of Iraq"

Choreography:

  • Independent: "The judge's response could not be heard"
  • Telegraph: Judge gives instruction to court clerk

Tone Differences:

  • Independent judge: more authoritative
  • Telegraph judge: more questioning

Concluding Exchanges: Even More Different

Independent Version (Longer)

Saddam refuses to sign without lawyers, robust exchange:

  • Saddam: questions judge about rushing decisions
  • Extended back-and-forth about signing documents
  • Judge: "Then you can leave"
  • More dialogue before final dismissal

Telegraph Version (Shorter)

No altercation, Saddam says very little before dismissal:

  • Brief exchange
  • Final comment to guards: "Take it easy, I'm an old man"
  • Rendered into colloquial British English - jars with language used hitherto

Translation Strategies: Foreignization vs Domestication

Feature Independent (Foreignizing) Daily Telegraph (Domesticating)
Language style Some awkward turns of phrase suggesting translationese; sounds slightly more foreign Traces of foreignness played down; colloquial British English
Example sentence "I'm not holding fast to my position, but to respect the will of the people that decided to choose Saddam Hussein as leader of the revolution" (not standard English construction) "Take it easy, I'm an old man" (colloquial British)
Effect Retention of foreignness: reminder proceedings not in English; hint of Saddam's personal speaking style; reinforces authenticity Brings text wholly into target system; traces of foreignness minimized; local color inserted
Saddam's character More serious, articulate figure arguing strong case in own defense Blustering, rather coarse man; somewhat ridiculous figure
Newspaper reputation Strong reporting tradition on Middle Eastern affairs; editorial position opposed to Iraq war Conservative paper; supportive of British government's alliance with US

Key Distinction (Reminder)

  • Domestication: Brings text wholly into target system
  • Retention of foreign elements: Reminds readers what they encounter derives from somewhere else

7. Variations of Veracity: October 2005 Trial Transcripts

7.1 Evolution of Reporting Strategy

Device of printing court transcripts diminished as trial progressed - doubtless because of extent of material, requiring more summary and comment.

Purpose served: Reproduction of transcripts served purpose in preliminary hearing and start of trial, establishing what was to unfold in theatricalized terms

Transition: Supposedly verbatim exchanges gave way to articles written by journalists present in court

7.2 Daily Telegraph Version (20 October 2005)

Short Exchange Reported as "Argument":

JUDGE: Mr Saddam we ask you to write down your identity, your name, occupation and address and then we will allow you to talk. Now it is time to write down your identity.
SADDAM: First of all, who are you and what are you?
JUDGE: The Iraqi criminal court.
SADDAM: All of you are judges?
JUDGE: We don't have time to get into details. You can write down what you like.
SADDAM: I have been here in this military building since 2.30, and then from nine I have been wearing this suit. They have asked me to take it off and then put it on again many times.
SADDAM: [Continues with refusal to recognize court's authority]

Characteristics:

  • Judge asks Saddam to "write down" identity
  • Saddam complains about dressing/undressing
  • References to "aggression" being "illegitimate"

7.3 La Repubblica (Italian) Version - Same Date

Preface:

Three hours of 'duello' (duelling) in Baghdad court

Key Differences:

  • Judge (named as Judge Amin) asks Saddam to "comunicare" (communicate/state) - far more general than English "write down"
  • Includes stage direction: "(Saddam sorride)" - (Saddam smiles)
  • Waiting complaint in "best suit" (not undressing complaint)
  • References to "falsità" (falsehood), not "aggression"
  • Reference to occupation included

Use of Tu vs Lei:

  • Saddam: Uses familiar "tu" form - suggests disrespect for judge
  • Judge: Stays with formal "lei"
  • Judge courteous throughout: "per cortesia" (please)

7.4 Le Figaro (French) Version - Same Date

Headline:

"Saddam Hussein défie ses juges" (Saddam Hussein defies his judges)

Introductory Info:

Judge is Rizgar Mohammed Amin, of Kurdish origin

Key Differences:

  • First speech: "Qui êtes-vous? Que me veut ce tribunal?" - uses formal "vous" throughout (contrast with Italian "tu")
  • Judge refers to Saddam as "Monsieur Saddam" - very courteous
  • "Avez-vous déjà jugee auparavant?" (Have you ever judged before?) - asks about judge's experience
  • Verb choice: "choisir" (chose me) - implies more than election, suggests "chosen one"

Comparison of All Three Versions (20 October 2005)

Feature English (Telegraph) Italian (Repubblica) French (Figaro)
Order of exchanges Standard sequence Different order; complaints in reverse Different order again
Waiting complaint Undressing many times Waiting in "best suit" Not prominently featured
Judge's tone Abrupt to point of rudeness Courteous ("per cortesia") Courteous ("Monsieur Saddam")
Formality level N/A (English lacks tu/vous) Saddam uses "tu" (disrespectful); Judge uses "lei" Saddam uses "vous" (respectful) throughout
Legitimacy language "Aggression is illegitimate" "Falsità" (falsehood); occupation referenced Court "n'est pas légale" (not legal)
Saddam's characterization Blustering, somewhat ridiculous More serious, insistent on rights Commanding figure with moral dignity; "chosen one"
Newspaper position on Iraq War UK government allied with US Coverage varies Opposed to war; termed it "American occupation"

Critical Questions Raised

Do these details matter when both versions effectively give gist?

Answer: YES, they matter greatly

Reasons:

  1. For readers: Once we see differences between what purport to be accurate, truthful versions of same text, veracity of reporting of whole proceedings is called into question
  2. For translation analysts: Two texts create very different impression of event. If dramatized, actors would play very different roles depending on which script given
  3. Character representation varies dramatically:
    • Telegraph: Blustering, rather coarse man
    • Independent: More serious, articulate figure arguing strong case
    • Figaro: Commanding figure with moral dignity

8. Translation Processes in News

Multiple Translation Processes

Whole series of different translation processes have taken place:

  1. Spoken → Written
  2. Arabic → English
  3. Full length → Abbreviated
  4. Initial copy → In-house style tailored for particular readership
  5. In one case: Domestication strategy (less evident in other version)

At every stage: Manipulations have taken place

Yet: We are still invited to accept final product as true and accurate version for English readers

The Unanswerable Question of Veracity

The Problem

Both transcripts are edited. We're told parts of conversation in longer version were not included in original court transcript, though no indication where additions came from. Additions nevertheless included as translations of verbatim material recorded "somewhere by someone."

We are left with sense of uneasiness:

  • If we have no Arabic, cannot check accuracy against published transcript in source language
  • All we have: two English translations of composite transcript of court hearing in Arabic
  • Both edited in different ways conforming to:
    • Editorial policy of different newspapers
    • Expectations of readers

9. Truth, Appropriateness, and Sincerity as Culturally Determined

Cultural Variability of Ethical Criteria

Truth, appropriateness, and sincerity are culturally determined, therefore variable.

Examples of Cultural Disparities:

  • What passes as appropriate in one context may be deemed utterly inappropriate in another
  • British use of irony and understatement would be highly inappropriate where hyperbole and assertiveness are norms
  • Only through translation do such disparities emerge

The Problem for Journalists

Part of problem faced by journalists writing up Saddam Hussein trial events derived from this disparity:

Some of his speeches in court:

  • Acceptable in his own context
  • Would have been read in very different light had they remained unedited

Therefore, Editing Processes Were Multi-Layered:

  1. Recognizing differences in cultural expectations
  2. Accommodating house style
  3. Accommodating ideological position of newspaper

At same time: Presentation of trial as global theatre determined what was offered to readers in different countries

10. Conclusion: Translation and the News

What Is "Translation" in News Context?

Although we may use word 'translation' when referring to news translation, what happens during process of transfer is not translation as generally understood.

Why Journalists Object to Being Called Translators:

Prefer to refer to anyone who works across languages as "international journalist"

The Process Explained:

  • Bringing information out of one cultural context into another may involve element of interlingual transfer
  • BUT: Focus NOT on linguistic transferral
  • Focus IS on: Transposition of information in format aimed at meeting demands of target readership
  • All kinds of additional textual processes beyond interlingual are involved
  • Regardless of whether there is existing original text or journalist compiling report from variety of sources

The Absence of a Source Text

Challenging the Definition of Translation

Fact: There may not even be an original calls very idea of translation into question

Traditional definition: Translating involves transfer of text produced in one language into another

News reporting reality: Frequently what happens in news reporting cannot be defined as translation in this way

Yet: Arguably there IS translation dimension when story that begins in one context is told, then retold for other readers in another language somewhere else

Even With Source Text, Picture Remains Clouded

As we can see from examples of supposedly accurate translations from an original:

Variations are so great (result of various textual manipulations) that we have only loose grasp of what that original might have been

What Actually Happens in News Translation

The Real Process

Networks of foreign correspondents, working within or linked to news agencies, write and shape stories for designated audiences.

Result: Mainly creation of completely new texts, which resist definition of translation

Power Inequalities

Domination of certain languages globally means power inequalities existing in wider socio-political arena manifest themselves in textual strategies employed by international journalists

What Study of Global News Translation Does

Critical Awareness

Study of global news translation makes us all more aware of:

  1. Manipulative processes that underlie what we read
  2. Serious questions about extent to which we can ever know what was or was not said in another cultural context
