CHAPTER 9: TYPES OF SENTENCES
Quick Exam Summary

1. CLAUSES: THE BUILDING BLOCKS

CLAUSE: A group of words that contains a SUBJECT and a VERB.

Two Types of Clauses:

Independent Clause Dependent Clause
Definition: Complete thought, can stand alone as a sentence Definition: Incomplete thought, CANNOT stand alone
Example: "The sun rose." Example: "When the sun rose"
✓ Can be a sentence by itself ✗ SENTENCE FRAGMENT if alone
Has: Subject + Verb (+ complement) Has: Subordinator + Subject + Verb

Common Subordinators:

Time: when, while, after, before, until, since
Reason: because, since
Condition: if, unless
Contrast: although, though, even though, whereas
Result: so that
Others: that, who, which, where

2. FOUR SENTENCE TYPES

SENTENCE TYPE = TYPE OF CLAUSES USED
Type Formula Example
1. SIMPLE 1 independent clause Water boils at 100°C.
2. COMPOUND 2+ independent clauses Water boils at 100°C, and it freezes at 0°C.
3. COMPLEX 1 independent + 1+ dependent When water boils, it turns to steam.
4. COMPOUND-COMPLEX 2+ independent + 1+ dependent When water boils, it turns to steam, and it evaporates.

3. SIMPLE SENTENCES

SIMPLE SENTENCE: ONE independent clause (but can have compound subjects/verbs)

Variations:

Type Example
One subject, one verb Water boils.
One subject, two verbs (compound verb) Water boils and freezes.
Two subjects, one verb (compound subject) Water and oil boil.
Two subjects, two verbs Water and oil boil and freeze.
✓ All examples above = SIMPLE sentences (only 1 clause each)

4. COMPOUND SENTENCES

COMPOUND SENTENCE: Two or more independent clauses joined together

Three Ways to Join Independent Clauses:

Method 1: COORDINATORS (FAN BOYS)

Independent clause, coordinator independent clause.
Coordinator Meaning Example
For Reason People live longer, for they eat healthy diets.
And Addition (similar idea) They eat fish, and they eat vegetables.
Nor Negative addition They don't eat meat, nor do they eat dairy.
But Contrast (direct opposite) Diet is important, but it's not the only factor.
Or Alternative Limit animal fat, or risk heart disease.
Yet Unexpected continuation Smoking is harmful, yet some smokers live long.
So Result Stress is harmful, so avoid it.

Method 2: CONJUNCTIVE ADVERBS

Independent clause; conjunctive adverb, independent clause.
Purpose Conjunctive Adverbs
Addition also, besides, furthermore, in addition, moreover
Contrast however, nevertheless, nonetheless, still, on the other hand, in contrast
Alternative otherwise
Result accordingly, as a result, consequently, hence, therefore, thus
Example for example, for instance
Example: Tuition is low; however, many students need aid.

Method 3: SEMICOLON ALONE

Independent clause; independent clause.
Example: Poland turned away from communism; others followed.
(Only use when clauses are closely related)

5. COMPLEX SENTENCES

COMPLEX SENTENCE: ONE independent clause + ONE (or more) dependent clause(s)

Three Types of Dependent Clauses:

1. ADVERB CLAUSES (Tell when, where, why, how)

Pattern 1: Dependent clause, independent clause.

Pattern 2: Independent clause dependent clause. (no comma)

✓ Although women could own property, they couldn't vote until 1920.
✓ You can vote when you are 18 years old.

2. ADJECTIVE CLAUSES (Describe nouns)

Begin with: who, whom, which, whose, that, where, when

✓ Men who are not married are called bachelors.
✓ We vacationed in Cozumel, which features scuba diving.

3. NOUN CLAUSES (Act as subject or object)

Begin with: wh- words, that, whether, if

✓ That there is a hole in the ozone layer is well known. (subject)
✓ Scientists know what caused it. (object)

6. COMPOUND-COMPLEX SENTENCES

COMPOUND-COMPLEX: At least TWO independent clauses + at least ONE dependent clause (minimum 3 clauses total)
Examples:

1. I wanted to travel after I graduated; however, I had to work.
   (Ind. clause + dep. clause); (conj. adverb, ind. clause)

2. After I graduated, I wanted to travel, but I had to work.
   (Dep. clause), (ind. clause), (coordinator ind. clause)

3. I wanted to travel after I graduated, but I had to work because I had to support my family.
   (Ind. + dep.), (coordinator ind. + dep.)

Punctuation Rules:

  • Compound part: Punctuate like compound sentence (semicolon or comma + coordinator)
  • Complex part: Punctuate like complex sentence (comma after adverb clause at beginning)

7. SENTENCE VARIETY = GOOD WRITING STYLE

✗ POOR STYLE: Using only one sentence type
• Too many simple sentences = choppy, immature
• Too many compound sentences = boring, repetitive
• Need variety for effective writing!
✓ GOOD STYLE: Mix all four sentence types
• Simple sentences for emphasis
• Compound sentences for equal ideas
• Complex sentences for showing relationships
• Compound-complex for sophisticated ideas

8. QUICK EXAM TIPS

Punctuation Quick Reference

Pattern Punctuation
Independent, coordinator independent. Comma BEFORE coordinator
Independent; conjunctive adverb, independent. Semicolon BEFORE; comma AFTER
Independent; independent. Semicolon only (closely related)
Dependent, independent. Comma when dep. clause first
Independent dependent. No comma when dep. clause second

Common Errors

✗ ERROR #1: Comma Splice
Wrong: "I studied hard, however, I failed."
✓ Fix: "I studied hard; however, I failed."
✗ ERROR #2: Run-On Sentence
Wrong: "I studied hard I passed."
✓ Fix: "I studied hard, and I passed." OR "I studied hard; I passed."
✗ ERROR #3: Sentence Fragment
Wrong: "Because I studied hard."
✓ Fix: "Because I studied hard, I passed." (add independent clause)
✗ ERROR #4: Missing Comma
Wrong: "Although I studied I failed."
✓ Fix: "Although I studied, I failed."

Memory Aid: FAN BOYS

For = reason
And = addition
Nor = negative (question word order!)
But = direct contrast
Or = alternative
Yet = unexpected (surprising!)
So = result

To Identify... Count...

Compound-Complex 2+ independent, 1+ dependent
