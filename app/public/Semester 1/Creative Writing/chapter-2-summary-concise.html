CHAPTER 2: UNITY AND COHERENCE
Quick Exam Summary

1. UNITY

UNITY: All sentences in a paragraph must relate to ONE main idea (the topic sentence). A paragraph has unity when ALL sentences support the single main idea.

Key Rule

‚úó Don't introduce irrelevant information or new ideas that don't support your topic sentence.
‚úì Do ensure every sentence clearly connects to your controlling idea.

2. COHERENCE

COHERENCE: Ideas in a paragraph flow smoothly and logically from one sentence to the next. Readers can easily follow your thoughts.

Four Ways to Achieve Coherence:

Technique Description Example
1. Repeat Key Nouns Repeat important nouns rather than always using pronouns "Gold is valuable. Gold is used in jewelry. This precious metal..."
2. Use Consistent Pronouns Use the same pronoun throughout when referring to the same noun "Students must... They should... They need..." (not switching to "you" or "one")
3. Use Transition Signals Words/phrases that connect ideas and show relationships however, therefore, for example, in addition
4. Arrange in Logical Order Organize ideas in a pattern readers can follow Chronological, comparison/contrast, logical division

3. TRANSITION SIGNALS

Four Types of Transition Signals:

Type Examples Punctuation
1. Sentence Connectors however, therefore, furthermore, moreover, nevertheless ; however, OR . However,
2. Coordinators and, but, or, so, yet, for, nor (FANBOYS) , and
3. Subordinators although, because, while, since, if, when Although ..., OR ...because ...
4. Others the first reason, another example, the final point Varies

Common Transition Signals by Purpose:

Purpose Signals
Add Information in addition, furthermore, moreover, also, and, another
Show Contrast however, but, on the other hand, in contrast, although, yet
Show Cause/Effect therefore, consequently, as a result, because, since, so
Give Examples for example, for instance, such as
Show Time Order first, then, next, finally, after, before, when
Conclude in conclusion, to sum up, in brief, therefore

Punctuation Rules:

Sentence Connectors:

Sentence 1; however, sentence 2.

Sentence 1. However, sentence 2.

Coordinators:

Independent clause, and independent clause.

Subordinators:

Although dependent clause, independent clause.

Independent clause because dependent clause.

4. LOGICAL ORDER PATTERNS

Three Main Patterns:

1. CHRONOLOGICAL ORDER (Time Sequence)

  • Use for: Processes, narratives, historical events
  • Signals: first, then, next, after that, finally, before, when
  • Example: "First, gather ingredients. Then, mix them. Finally, bake for 30 minutes."

2. LOGICAL DIVISION OF IDEAS

  • Use for: Dividing topic into categories, reasons, types, or kinds
  • Signals: first, second, another, in addition, also
  • Example: "There are three types of learners: visual, auditory, and kinesthetic."

3. COMPARISON/CONTRAST

  • Use for: Showing similarities and/or differences
  • Signals:
Comparison (Similarities) Contrast (Differences)
similarly, likewise, also, both, too however, in contrast, whereas, while, but, unlike

Common Errors to Avoid

‚úó ERROR #1: Inconsistent Pronouns

Wrong: "Students should study hard. You need to manage your time."
(Started with "students" then switched to "you")

Right: "Students should study hard. They need to manage their time."

‚úó ERROR #2: Missing Transition Signals

Choppy: "Exercise is important. It improves health. It reduces stress."

Better: "Exercise is important. First, it improves health. In addition, it reduces stress."

‚úó ERROR #3: Wrong Punctuation with Transitions

Wrong: "I studied hard, however, I failed."
(Comma splice - cannot join two sentences with comma + however)

Right: "I studied hard; however, I failed." OR
"I studied hard. However, I failed."
