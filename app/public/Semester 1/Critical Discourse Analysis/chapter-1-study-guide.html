Session 1: Background & Theory of CDA

Critical Discourse Analysis | Chapter 1 - Introduction to CDA
Foundations, Methods, and Key Concepts

1. What is Critical Discourse Analysis?

Core Definition

Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) is the systematic study of how language is used to persuade, manipulate, and control individuals and social groups. It focuses on language as a controlling force in society and its role in maintaining or changing social structures.

Alternative Names: CDA is also referred to as Critical Linguistics or Critical Discourse Studies (CDS). These terms are often used interchangeably in academic literature.
"Social action and discourse are inextricably linked." — Scollon (2001)

1.1 Key Goals of CDA

The Four Pillars of CDA

  • Develop awareness of the relationship between language and ideology
  • Understand how discourse controls and shapes society
  • Question traditional roles in conventional discourses
  • Work towards social justice and positive change
Critical Note: The word "critical" in CDA does NOT mean negative evaluation. It means critique — which can be positive (e.g., analyzing successful resistance movements) or negative. Critical analysis examines the underlying structures, assumptions, and power relations in any discourse.

2. The Multidisciplinary Nature of CDA

Understanding Discourse

Discourse encompasses all forms of symbolic human interaction, including:

  • Language (spoken and written)
  • Gesture and body language
  • Pictures and images
  • Films and videos
  • Music and sound
Field Contribution to CDA
Linguistics Provides tools for analyzing language structures and patterns
Sociology Offers frameworks for understanding social structures and power
Psychology Helps understand cognitive processes in discourse comprehension
Anthropology Provides cultural context and ethnographic methods
Literary Theory Contributes textual analysis techniques
Sociolinguistics Examines language variation in social contexts

Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL)

CDA heavily draws from Systemic Functional Linguistics developed by M.A.K. Halliday. SFL is particularly valuable because it explicitly considers social context in language analysis.

Key insight: Language choices are not random but are systematically related to social functions and contexts.

Deeper Understanding

Why does this distinction matter for your research?

Think of it this way: Traditional linguistics is like studying how a car engine works. CDA is like studying where the car is going, who is driving, and why they chose that route.

Practical Example:

  • Traditional analysis: "The sentence 'Protesters clashed with police' uses active voice with the subject 'protesters'."
  • CDA analysis: "Why is 'protesters' placed as the agent? This makes them appear responsible for the violence. Compare: 'Police clashed with protesters' - same event, different framing, different responsibility assignment."

Key Takeaway: CDA asks not just "How does this language work?" but "What work is this language doing in society?"

3. CDA Methods

Four Key Research Methods

  1. Context Analysis: Examine the social setting in which discourse occurs
  2. Observational Techniques: Recording and transcribing natural language in use
  3. Participant Observation: Researcher takes an active role in the target community
  4. Use of Informants: Experts explain discourse practices to researchers

3.1 Practical Applications of CDA

Area Focus Impact
Advertising Persuasive language, truth distortion, manipulation Empowers consumers to resist marketing pressure
Forensic Linguistics Legal discourse, confessions, cross-examination Establishes justice, creates fairer legal practices

Case Study: Advertising Analysis

Context: A skincare advertisement claims: "9 out of 10 dermatologists recommend our product."

CDA Questions:

  • How were these dermatologists selected? (sampling bias)
  • What question were they actually asked?
  • Who funded the study?
  • What cultural assumptions does this exploit? (authority, expertise)

Result: CDA reveals how statistical language creates false authority and exploits trust in expertise.

4. Principles of CDA

Core Principles

  • Self-Critical: Analysts acknowledge their own position and biases (no claim to complete objectivity)
  • Ethical Practice: Proper permission, privacy protection, and responsible reporting
  • Tolerance: Accept varied approaches from multiple theoretical sources
  • International: Welcomes contributions from many cultures and countries
  • Power-Focused: Central understanding that discourse = power & control
The Self-Reflexive Principle: Unlike traditional linguistics, CDA practitioners openly acknowledge that complete objectivity is impossible. Every analyst brings their own worldview, experiences, and values to their analysis. This honesty is seen as a strength, not a weakness.

5. Key Terms and Definitions

5.1 The Six Meanings of DISCOURSE

# Meaning Example
1 Broad: All symbolic interaction (spoken, written, visual) Including films, music, gestures
2 Spoken: Simply spoken interaction (vs. written) Conversations, speeches, dialogues
3 vs. Text: Whole act of communication (production + comprehension) The complete communicative event
4 Institutional: Communication in specific contexts Legal discourse, medical discourse, scientific discourse
5 A discourse: A particular long text (NOT commonly used in CDA) A lecture, a sermon
6 Multi-modal: Uses more than one mode Words + pictures + music together

5.2 Text vs. Discourse

Text: The product of discourse. It is the linguistic record of a communicative event (electronic recording or written document). A text must meet the 7 Standards of Textuality to be considered a proper text.

Seven Standards of Textuality (Beaugrande & Dressler)

  1. Cohesion: How words and sentences connect in sequence (grammatical links)
  2. Coherence: Cognitive relations, logical connections, mutual knowledge
  3. Acceptability: Appropriateness to cultural setting and audience expectations
  4. Intentionality: The producer's purpose and goals
  5. Informativity: Suitable degree of new vs. given information
  6. Situationality: Relevance to the specific situation
  7. Intertextuality: Reliance on and reference to previous texts
Example: Cohesion vs. Coherence

Cohesive but not coherent: "The cat sat on the mat. Mats are made of fiber. Fiber is important for digestion. Digestion occurs in the stomach." (Grammatically connected, but no logical unity)

Coherent but low cohesion: "A: What time is it? B: The bus just left." (No grammatical link, but perfectly coherent in context)

5.3 Domain, Social Practice, and Genre

Domain: A socially recognized context containing specific social practices and genres. Examples: science, law, education, BBC, university.
Social Practice: Human behaviors following social conventions with some degree of freedom. A single instance is called a social event or speech event. Examples: business meetings, religious services, classroom teaching.
Genre: A specific product of social practice. A culturally recognized discourse form with agreed structures. Examples: novel, poem, lecture, letter, business report, news article.

5.4 Speech Community vs. Discourse Community

Speech Community Discourse Community
Share language features May never meet face-to-face
Frequently interact in person Conscious, chosen participation
Usually accident of birth/location Deliberate membership
Example: Geordie dialect speakers Example: Medical researchers, online communities

5.5 Other Essential Terms

Speech Acts: Utterances that perform actions simply by being spoken. Theory developed by J.L. Austin in the 1950s. Examples: promise, instruction, bet, pronouncement of marriage. Note: Speech acts may be interpreted differently than intended.
Ideology: A set of beliefs and attitudes shared by a social group. Often unconscious and taken for granted. Can be hidden in single words (e.g., "democracy," "freedom," "peacekeeping," "collateral damage").
Frames: Mental structures that shape how we see the world (concept from George Lakoff). Frames are triggered by words and are culture-specific cognitive models. Example: The word "cricket" triggers a frame including: pitch, ball, bat, umpire, wicket, innings.
Archive: The set of frames handed down in a culture, shared by group members. It facilitates in-group communication but can hinder cross-group communication.
Connecting the Concepts

How do these terms work together?

Imagine you're analyzing a news report about immigration:

  • Domain: News media / journalism
  • Genre: News report
  • Discourse Community: Journalists, editors, news consumers
  • Ideology: May contain hidden assumptions about national identity, economy, security
  • Frames: "Flood of immigrants" (triggers water/disaster frame), "Immigration crisis" (triggers emergency frame)

CDA would ask:

  1. What frames are being activated? (disaster, invasion, burden)
  2. Whose ideology is being expressed? (government, opposition, media owners)
  3. What speech acts are performed? (warning, criticism, fear-mongering)
  4. What is NOT said? (immigrant contributions, historical context)

6. Language and Institutions

The Concept of "Making Strange"

Making Strange is the practice of observing familiar customs as an outsider would. This is an essential skill for critical analysis. It involves stepping back from normalized practices to see them with fresh eyes.

Case Study: Marriage Ceremony Language

Traditional vow: The bride was required to promise to "obey and serve" — a requirement not placed on the groom.

CDA analysis: This linguistic asymmetry reflected and reinforced gender inequality. The practice was so normalized that it seemed "natural."

Change: Through "making strange" and critical awareness, this practice became denormalized, leading to more equal modern vows.

Normalization: When customs, practices, or language use become so familiar they seem unremarkable or "natural." CDA's job is to challenge normalized practices and expose the power relations they encode.
Example: Generic Pronouns

Old rule: "He embraces female" — using masculine pronouns as generic (e.g., "Each student should bring his book")

Change: Through critical awareness, this was denormalized. Now singular "they" is widely accepted ("Each student should bring their book")

Lesson: Language that seems "natural" often encodes ideology. Change is possible through awareness.

7. What Makes CDA "Critical"?

7.1 Non-Critical vs. Critical Discourse Analysis

Non-Critical DA Critical DA
Identify how people use language View discourse as product AND producer of society
Develop analysis methods Question texts AND the processes that create them
Build communication theories Commit to social concerns and change
Describe what IS Ask why things are this way and how they could be different

7.2 Objectives of CDA

Practical Objectives

  • Analyze discourse practices that reflect or construct social problems
  • Investigate how ideologies become "frozen" in language
  • Increase awareness to address injustice, prejudice, and power misuse

Theoretical Objectives

  • Demonstrate language's significance in power relations
  • Investigate how meaning is created in context
  • Examine the role of speaker/writer purpose and authorial stance

8. Key Researchers in CDA

Teun van Dijk

Focus: Racism and prejudice in discourse

Key contributions: Analysis of how racist ideologies are reproduced through everyday discourse, news media, and political speech.

Norman Fairclough

Focus: Global capitalism and "new capitalism"

Key contributions: Three-dimensional framework for CDA (text, discourse practice, social practice). Concept of synthetic personalization in marketing.

Robert de Beaugrande

Focus: Ecological issues and discourse

Key contributions: Seven standards of textuality. Application of CDA to environmental discourse.

Summary: The CDA Mindset

What does it mean to "think like a CDA analyst"?

After studying this chapter, you should develop a new way of seeing language:

1. Nothing is neutral

  • Every word choice carries meaning beyond dictionary definition
  • Even "objective" texts contain hidden positions
  • Silence (what's NOT said) is as important as what IS said

2. Language creates reality

  • How we name things shapes how we think about them
  • Discourse doesn't just reflect society — it shapes society
  • Change the discourse, change the world

3. Power is everywhere

  • Who gets to speak? Who is silenced?
  • Whose frames become "common sense"?
  • What ideologies are normalized as "natural"?

4. Analysis is action

  • CDA is not just academic — it's a tool for social change
  • By exposing hidden power, we enable resistance
  • Awareness is the first step to transformation

Remember: You are now equipped to look beneath the surface of any text and ask: "Whose interests does this serve? What power relations does this maintain? What alternatives are silenced?"

9. Summary: Key Points to Remember

Essential Takeaways from Chapter 1:
  • CDA studies language as a tool of power and social control
  • It is multidisciplinary, drawing from linguistics, sociology, psychology, and more
  • CDA analysts acknowledge their own biases (self-reflexivity)
  • Key terms: discourse (6 meanings), text, genre, domain, ideology, frames
  • Seven standards of textuality: cohesion, coherence, acceptability, intentionality, informativity, situationality, intertextuality
  • "Critical" means critique, not negative — CDA works for social justice
  • "Making strange" helps denormalize problematic practices
  • Main theorists: van Dijk (racism), Fairclough (capitalism), de Beaugrande (ecology)
