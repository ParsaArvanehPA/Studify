üåê 1. News Agencies in the 21st Century

Definition & Function:
  • Offer crucial services of news gathering and news alerts to media outlets
  • Subscribers share costs of gathering/distribution but depend on agencies for international (sometimes domestic) news
  • Mostly B2B (business-to-business) organizations providing news to media outlets, commercial clients, government agencies
  • Growing trend: distributing news to private customers (B2C)
  • Non-media clients = great source of income and financial stability
Historical significance: News agencies were the FIRST multinational corporations. Agence Havas (precursor of AFP) initially specialized in translation of foreign press.

Role in the Mediascape - Agenda-Setting Power

Agenda-Setting Role (Undisputed Among Scholars):
  • Newswires determine which topics become news
  • "Their news selection decisions are often followed by other journalists" (Lorenz 2017: 949)
  • Issues ignored by wholesalers seldom considered by other media
  • Even stronger for online media
Studies on Actual Influence:
  • Paterson (2005): Iran coverage - 43-60% of "original" content actually originated from AP and Reuters newsrooms
  • Lewis, Williams & Franklin (2008): Nearly half of news in 5 prestigious London newspapers came wholly/mainly from agency services
  • Welbers et al. (2018): Dutch media show increased reliance on agency copy (1996-2013)

Churnalism - Critical Issue

Churnalism (Davies 2009): Uncritical use of public relations material (press releases)

Lorenz (2017) findings - Brussels correspondents:
  • International news agency correspondents LESS likely to incorporate PR material than independent correspondents
  • PR from European institutions churned more frequently than lobbyists/NGOs documents

Forde & Johnston (2013): Australian Associated Press can become "de facto distributor of public relations material"

üíª 2. News Agencies in the Internet Era

Blurring of Retail/Wholesale Distinction

Changes:
  • Some agencies' websites make content directly available to end users (Reuters 2015)
  • Threatens B2B model BUT "many agencies persist primarily as news wholesalers" (Boyd-Barrett 2016: 164)
  • Wholesale/retail distinction no longer absolute

Why Agencies Still Thrive Despite Media Crisis

Strategy Details
Diversification Financial news, media monitoring, video, press release dissemination, weather forecasting, databases, IT solutions
State Support Some benefit from government financial support (direct/indirect) - e.g., AFP, Xinhua
Expanded Client Base "Considerably augmented their client and user bases" - newspapers/broadcasters have fewer local bureaus/correspondents
Multimedia Services Broadband connections improved - more multimedia output. Convergent newsrooms (Reuters, dpa, AAP)

Social Media Integration

Few studies, but results converge:
  • AP (Boyd-Barrett 2010, Griessner 2012): Social networks (Facebook, Twitter, Yahoo, etc.) used "first and foremost as tool for newsgathering"
  • AFP (Nicey & Palmer 2012): Similar - use social networks to "cover hot-spots from which agencies were absent"
  • Distribution: AP available on social media for credibility/brand presence but doesn't interact much with users
  • AFP strategy: Management encourages journalists to use social networks to interact with public

Risks of Convergence

If same journalists produce text, pictures AND videos = risks for delivery of accurate information (S√†nchez Mar√≠n et al. 2017)

Boyd-Barrett's Future Predictions

  • Intensification of multilingual and multimodal services
  • Further convergence "towards standard Anglo-American classic model of journalism"
  • Development of "point-of-view journalistic narratives"
  • Continued threats to financial sustainability

Research Scattered & Isolated:
  • Few studies into global agencies (Reuters, AFP)
  • Several publications about national agencies
  • Diverse scholarly perspectives: product-, process-, reception-oriented approaches

Three Approaches:
  • Product-oriented: Look at (multimedia) texts
  • Process-oriented: How translations produced, by whom, in what contexts

"Agency dispatches are destined for other journalists rather than the general public: to radio, television and newspaper editors who might decide to pick up some information and transmit it to their audience after having adapted and modified it to suit their particular needs and style." (Bielsa & Bassnett 2009: 88)

Journalists Instead of Translators - The Rule

General Observation: News agencies do NOT hire translators as a rule (Bielsa & Bassnett 2009: 57; Davier 2017: 146)

Exceptions:
  • Alternative agencies (e.g., IPS) - in-house translators choose texts, have more time
  • Efe international Arabic service - editors check/edit translator output
Journalists function as "natural" or "non-professional translators" - any bilingual individual presumed able to translate without formal training (Antonini 2011)
Key Characteristics:
  • Not trained in translation
  • No specific guidelines on how to translate
  • "Learning by doing" - don't think about practice much
  • Example quote from Keystone-ATS senior editor: "we really learn on the job, it's 'learning by doing', and actually we don't think about our practice a lot"

Why No Translators? Lay Understanding of Translation

Problem: Journalists have lay understanding of translation as LEXICAL TRANSFER (Bielsa & Bassnett 2009: 1; ComƒÉneci 2012; Davier 2014)

Consequences:
  • Translation conceived as literal = simple task requiring no training
  • Journalists don't acknowledge difficulties or look for help
  • Poses risks for accuracy
  • Translation confined to communications of lesser importance (soft news, non-urgent stories, smaller audiences)

Contradictory Practices: Major Rewritings AND Lexical Transfer

PARADOX: Translation both invisible AND involves major interventions!
Phenomenon Description
1. Textual Invisibility In Davier's corpus of 344,850 words: equivalents of "translate" occurred only 4 times, never referring to translations in dispatches
2. Avoidance Journalists avoid literal translation (don't enjoy it):
"Well, it's a job no one of us really likes doing" (Keystone-ATS journalist)
Make up excuses: "there's nothing new" or "it's badly written"
3. "Deselection" (Davier 2017: 290) Avoid quotations needing translation, substitute with/"reselect" quotes in language of writing.
Argue: faster and more tailored to audience needs
4. Major Textual Interventions Additions, deletions, changes in paragraph order, changes of title and lead (first paragraph)
5. Word-for-Word Quotations Quotations tend to be translated literally (Bielsa & Bassnett 2009; Davier 2017; Scammell 2018)

Example: Verbal Patchwork (Davier 2017: 208)

News item = verbal patchwork consisting of:
  • Quotations (original language)
  • Translated quotations
  • Culture-specific concepts
  • Text copied from press release (translated or not, in agency or beforehand)
  • Translated explanations
  • Text written from scratch
  • Background information copied from archive
Senior AFP journalist explains the paradox (Davier 2017: 144): "this means that one has to be extremely faithful with translation when one deals with quotations, uh... and not confined to this precision when one... one explains"
If journalists conceive translation as lexical transfer, they avoid it in favor of rewriting when they think they have freedom, but fall back on unidiomatic transfer when compelled by original's form (quotes).

üìê 5. Consequences for Translation Studies

Problems with Fundamental Terms

Translation, Source, Target, Text - ALL Questioned!

Translation in agencies "resists the definition of translation" (Bielsa & Bassnett 2009: 132)
Concept Problem in News Agencies Proposed Solution
Source Text Can be SEVERAL source texts. Uncertainty surrounding which dispatch was model, whether elements integrated from several sources, or if relay used Refer to segments as "first" and "second" or segment in language A and B
(Davier 2017: 208)
Target Text Can be SEVERAL target texts. Whether parts used by other media and translated into another language Question usefulness of these categories
Author Collective authorship in newswires Revisit individual concept of author inherited from literary field
Text Unit of translation rarely complete dispatch. Translational elements appear more in quotations & culture-specific concepts Compare TEXT SEGMENTS, not full texts
Study translations as texts on own right WITHOUT comparing to original

Problems with Domestication

Three Perspectives on Domestication-Foreignization:

1. Bielsa & Bassnett (2009): "Dominant strategy is absolute domestication, as material is shaped in order to be consumed by target audience"
  • Applies at level of TEXTS or whole production

2. Scammell (2018): Criticizes status quo which "obscures journalist's intervention and distances reader from foreign source"
  • Proposes foreignization at level of PHRASES/SENTENCES - introducing foreign words
  • Reader-response experiment: foreignization doesn't compromise clear reporting, no negative feedback (except foreign words in headlines)
  • More restrictive definition than Venuti's original

3. Venuti (1995) original definition:
  • Resistant "choice of foreign texts"
  • "Foregrounding linguistic and cultural differences of source-language text"
  • "Transforming hierarchy of cultural values in target language"

Problems with Equivalence

Why Equivalence Fails in News Agencies:

1. Linguistic Equivalence:
  • Major textual interventions + textual invisibility of translation
  • "No identified source text" in many cases

2. Equivalent Effect:
  • Already challenged in TS: understanding is individual, difficult to evaluate, "reading is not merely passive reception"
  • News dispatches adapted to audiences that diverge dramatically (e.g., AFP English in Asia/Middle East vs French in France)
  • Must respond to different needs of different readers
  • Equivalence/equivalent effect meaningless in journalistic translations

New Theoretical Proposals

INDIRECT TRANSLATION: As bridge concept between different branches of TS, may open new conceptual doors and overcome field fragmentation (Assis Rosa et al. 2017: 113)

Broadly understood as: translation of a translation

üéì Conclusion & Future Research

Significance:
  • News agencies = first multinational corporations
  • Agence Havas (AFP precursor) initially specialized in translation of foreign press
  • Agenda-setting power widely acknowledged
  • Criticized for churnalism practices
  • Still thriving despite media crisis

Future Research Needed

  • Unanswered questions: Will more agencies open content to end users? Will convergent newsrooms become the rule? Will social media play greater role?
  • Understanding journalists' view of translation: If mainstream journalism equates translation with lexical transfer, likely to keep translation invisible and avoid it ‚Üí risk replicating linguistic/cultural proximity
  • Convergence effects: Existing studies examine media before convergent forms or use pre-convergence frameworks. Need research on:
    • Visual output (photographs, infographics, video clips)
    • News gathering/dissemination on social networks
    • Dialogue with audiovisual translation and social media translation
  • Theoretical studies: Exploring new concepts (e.g., indirect translation) or importing from other disciplines
