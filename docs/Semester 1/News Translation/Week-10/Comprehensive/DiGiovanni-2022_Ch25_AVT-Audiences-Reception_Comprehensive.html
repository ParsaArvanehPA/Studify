Introduction: The Complexity of Media Audiences

Factors Feeding Audience Complexity

  • Ever-changing nature of media (production and distribution)
  • Shifting composition of audience(s)
  • Evolving ways media texts are consumed
  • Makes empirical research on reception a renewed challenge

Reception Studies in AVT: Recent Developments

Reception of translated audiovisual texts has only recently become object of systematic investigation, driven by:

  • Technologies: Eye tracking, electroencephalography (EEG), galvanic skin response
  • Sophisticated traditional tools: Questionnaires (administered in various ways)
  • Media accessibility studies expansion: Focus on needs of deaf/hard of hearing, blind/partially sighted audiences

Audiovisual Media, Translations and Audiences

Defining 'Audience': A Complex Task

As many scholars in media studies state, defining 'audience' is complex and occasionally daunting. Further specification is needed for the concept to get its substance.

Ian Christie's Classifications (2012)

Types of Audiences

Classification Type Description
Contemporary vs Aggregated Aggregated = cumulative viewers over years/decades (diachronic perspective)
Imagined vs Economic/Statistical Imagined = sociological approach; Economic = measuring economic impact from marketing perspective
Individual vs Mass Focus on individual spectator needs/features OR audience as whole in terms of mass behavior
"We" vs "They" Researcher position: external observer OR part of the audience (participatory approaches)

Note: All classifications presuppose interdisciplinarity, highlighting cross-disciplinary nature of audience concept.

Abercrombie & Longhurst (1998): IS vs DOES

Preliminary Distinction

What an audience IS vs what it DOES

Applied to three types present in contemporary society:

1. Simple Audience

  • Most traditional, perhaps least contemporary type
  • Clear distinction between producers and consumers of performance
  • Performance occurs in public space
  • Participate in events with substantial ceremonial/sacred quality
  • Social distance between agents involved
  • Involvement: High

2. Mass Audience

  • Ushered in by advent of mass communication and technology
  • Moves experience from local to global sphere
  • From public to private setting (more powerful and widespread)
  • Tend to devote less attention to performance
  • Involvement: Medium
  • No spatial localization
  • Communication not so direct
  • More everyday experience, not invested with ceremony

3. Diffused Audience

  • Results from recent social and cultural changes
  • Main feature: Coming together of consumers and producers
  • Part of performance and influences it in participatory ways
  • Shifts focus from passive observation to active participation
  • Expands notion of performance

"In contemporary society, everyone becomes an audience all the time. Being a member of an audience is no longer an exceptional event, nor even an everyday event. Rather, it is constitutive of everyday life." (1998: 61)

Performance and Audience

"Critical to what it means to be a member of an audience is the idea of performance. Audiences are groups of people before whom a performance of one kind or another takes place. Performance, in turn, is a kind of activity in which the person performing accentuates his or her behaviour under the scrutiny of others... It is 'behaviour heightened, if ever so slightly, and publicly displayed; twice-behaved behaviour' (Schechner, 1993, p. 1)."

Adrian Athique (2016): Transnational Audiences

Anthropological Perspective

Athique reflects on audiences in relation to:

  • Media globalization
  • Transnational flows
  • Mediascapes

Globalization revolves around three forms of movement: Money, People, Media Content

Diasporic Audiences

  • Connected to minority cultural and linguistic groups
  • Related to dominating role of mass broadcasters
  • International media industries since early days of international cinema

Networked Audiences

  • Evoke notions of virtual, non-synchronous media consumption
  • Recall Abercrombie & Longhurst's diffused audiences
  • Shift from passive to active role
  • Shape creation and distribution of media content

"Proceeding from the formal qualities of digital technologies, we naturally perceive the audience as being comprised of various device operators who are simultaneously interacting with malleable content and with each other. In this context, we can no longer assume the physical co-presence of audience members, nor their simultaneous activity in a common moment of time... the networked audience is inherently an 'active' proposition." (2016: 61)

Particularly meaningful: Today's consumption of old and new media content in translation. Though connected virtually, their strength is tangible and shapes media services worldwide.

AVT and Audience-Centred Research

Yves Gambier's Three Rs (2018)

Overarching concepts in reception studies:

  1. Response: Investigated through psychological methodologies (common in experimental AVT studies)
  2. Reaction: Concerned with shared knowledge by partners in communication process and inferential processing
  3. Repercussion: Effects of reception process on both receiver(s) and sociocultural context they're part of/aspire to

Variables to consider:

  • Sociological nature
  • Technical nature (typical AVT constraints)

Features include: Textual and paratextual parameters

Kruger & Doherty (2018b)

Highlight importance of questionnaires and interviews in film and AVT studies, but encourage systematic inception of psychometric methods and tools:

"The most obvious measure in determining audience reception of AVT products is post-hoc attitudinal questionnaires and interviews. This measure is a mainstay of film studies and will always remain an important way to obtain qualitative information. We will here focus on a different strand in reception research that is more focused on using psychometric instruments to determine the impact of AVT products on audience immersion, enjoyment and cognitive load."

Types of Measures

  • Subjective: Self-rating by respondents (e.g., perceived cognitive effort)
  • Objective: Comprehension, recall, learning (though distinction is not clear-cut)

Essential: High number of participants to even out results and identify regularities

Historical Approaches to Reception Studies

Two Major Research Avenues

  1. Evolution of studies: Observe development in multi/interdisciplinary terms - analyze strategies and techniques over time (metadiscourse on media reception research)
  2. Historical reception of texts: Explore reception over years/decades (aggregated audience) OR focus on given time, text, audience of the past

First avenue: More theoretical, doesn't necessarily focus on audience as 'people'

Second avenue: Firmly based on mapping people's reactions to audiovisual texts over time or in past

Evolution of Reception Studies (Biltereyst & Meers)

Historical Timeline

Period Developments
Late 19th century Early endeavors from first years of cinema - focused on "psychological and societal dangers of films and cinemas"
Pre/Post WWI Social scientists and psychologists examined comprehension and influence. E.g., Emilie Altenloth surveyed filmgoers in Mannheim (groundbreaking focus on gender, female filmgoers)
Post WWII Studies flourished from various disciplinary perspectives
1960s Film studies institutionalized as scholarly field; systematic streams of audience research began. Drew from aesthetic and literary theories
1970s Psychoanalytical approaches ignited film theory. Audience's gaze and identification studied
1980s Influenced by feminist theory, sociology, anthropology, pragmatics
Today Landscape so varied and rich - hardly possible to single out most relevant strands. Multiple approaches needed for diffused, networked audiences

Note on Censorship

Controversies around harmful motion pictures and dark viewing spaces resulted in censorship and control, which triggered intensive research on film audiences and their reactions.

Historical Reception in AVT Studies

Carol O'Sullivan & François Cornu

Pioneers in historical AVT research - reconstructing cinema history through translation lens

  • Pre-sound era: "Films were silent, but not speechless" (title cards, commentators)
  • Talkies: "Ushered multilingualism into cinema on a worldwide scale"

Individual Contributions

  • Cornu (2014): Explored historical evolution of dubbing and subtitling in France and elsewhere
  • O'Sullivan (2018): Essay on film paratext - helps reflect on role of translation in film appreciation/success

O'Sullivan on Paratext

Drawing on Genette's theory:

  • Peritext: Elements internal to main text (e.g., subtitles burned onto film reel)
  • Epitext: Elements external to main text (e.g., today's multi-faceted subtitle options for viewers)

Impact on reception: Interesting to investigate how this affects appreciation

Retranslation

Central to Diachronic Reception Studies

"Retranslation is an intrinsic feature of AVT. This has not, to date, been much investigated in AVT studies, which have tended to think in terms of the dubbed and the subtitled version of a film. However, it is common for the same film to be subtitled more than once into a single language for theatrical exhibition, for DVD release, for television, on reissues of restored films). It is also not uncommon for films to be dubbed more than once into a single language." (O'Sullivan 2018: 270)

Current research: On the increase (Zanotti 2011, 2015; Di Giovanni 2016; Mereu Keating 2016)

Contributes to: Exploration of aggregated audiences (all people who viewed/appreciated AV texts in translation over time)

Common denominator: Archival research (film archives, libraries, private documents, journals, publishing houses)

Reception Studies in AVT: The Tools

Current trend: "Much of the recent research on AVT has focused on the examination of cognitive processing of audiovisual texts" (Kruger & Doherty 2018b: 91)

Timeline: Phenomenon increasingly recorded over slightly less than ten years, especially since media accessibility became well-established

Cognitive Approaches: Historical Development

  • Pre-Translation Studies: Linguistic studies showed interest in cognitive processes (e.g., Levý 1967)
  • 1980s: Specific publications began appearing, "mostly without relying on input from cognitive psychology"
  • 1990s: Surge in empirical research on non-literary translation → more careful eye on psychological theories/methodologies
  • Turn of century: Cognitive approaches in full bloom (PACTE research group 2003)

Offline vs Online Measures (Kruger & Doherty)

Offline Measures

  • Rely mainly on questionnaires and interviews
  • Aim to gauge: comprehension, recall, learning, cognitive load, immersion

Online Measures

  • Related essentially to eye tracking and electroencephalography
  • Defined as "typically more objective and physiological"
  • Obtained through tools respondents cannot control

Functional Distinction (Di Giovanni 2018b)

  • Perception: Essentially gauged by online measures
  • Reception: Requires both types of measures for complete evaluation

Both types converge onto overall evaluation of reception

Key Research Examples

Perego, Del Missier & Bottiroli (2015)

Unusual approach: Evaluated reception of subtitles against dubbing for same film and language pair

Multi-section Questionnaire Combined:

  • Cognitive measures: General comprehension, dialogue recognition, face-name association, visual scene recognition
  • Evaluative measures: Film appreciation, self-reported effort, judgments of memory

Points to: Great variability of psychological (cognitive) measures in classification and application

Szarkowska & Gerber Morón (2018)

Focus: Reading speed for subtitles and reception

Motivation: Constant increase in reading speeds globally (encouraged by Netflix)

Participants: 70 individuals

Languages: English, Polish, Spanish

Tools: Questionnaire (offline) + eye tracker (online)

Parameters Evaluated:

  • Comprehension
  • Scene and subtitle recognition
  • Cognitive load
  • Preferences and enjoyment

Results:

  • Participants had no difficulties processing fast subtitles
  • No increase in cognitive load or significant loss of visual intake
  • Unexpected: Cognitive load generally higher for English-speaking participants regardless of subtitle speed (possibly due to limited exposure to subtitling)

Louise Fryer: Audio Description Psychology-Based Strand

Since early 2010s: Promoted studies on psychological impact of AD on end users (visually impaired and not)

Background: Experimental psychologist, presenter, and audio describer

Key contribution: Inception of parameters such as immersion and presence in AVT (media accessibility) research

Works: 2012, 2013, 2014, 2017 (alone and with other scholars)

Triangulation of Methods

Kruger & Doherty (2018b) provide detailed discussion of triangulation of:

  • Physiological measures
  • Psychometric measures
  • Performance measures

To evaluate cognitive processing of subtitling - solid procedural and analytical recommendations

What the Future Holds

Current Challenges

  • Constant increase in extemporaneous, individual, private consumption
  • Exponential growth of video-on-demand and streaming platforms
  • Accentuated by pandemic lockdown worldwide
  • Mapping reception becoming increasingly challenging

Paradox: Audiences More Proactive (Di Giovanni 2019)

Today's media audiences:

  • Influence production and worldwide distribution
  • Criticize untimely distribution (and translation) choices with great resonance through blogs/social media
  • Increasingly direct content producers

Result: Great dynamism in consumption modes and shifting audience roles → need to reconsider observation tools and strategies

Innovative Trends in Audience-Centred AVT Research

1. Accessible Filmmaking (Pablo Romero Fresco 2019)

Definition: 'Accessibility' = all that serves purpose of allowing any person to enjoy a film (from translation to audio description)

Key Principles:

  • Films designed as accessible from very first conceptual steps
  • Catering for interlingual translations, captions, AD as part of production stage (NOT post-production)
  • Redesigns space for translators - highlighting relevance as part of film production
  • Supports production of films for all (accessible for people with host of different abilities)

Application: Can be applied to series, theatre, live shows - all media texts

Audience role: Tantamount - diverse needs at core of perspective

2. From Accessibility to Inclusion

Bottom-up approach: Questioning conventional notion of accessibility

Key Shift:

  • 'Disability' → 'Alternative abilities'
  • All abilities essential to design and equal consumption
  • Different abilities = essence of true inclusion
  • Leave behind idea of dis-abilities needing access to something un-accessible

Origins in AVT Practice:

  • Connected to fansubbing and fandubbing
  • Empowerment of consumers turned produsers

New stance: Embracing notion of diffused audiences and diluted notion of performance pervading our lives

3. Inclusive Design and Design-for-All

Core principle: People (not services or texts) at core of creative efforts

Goal: "Provide all people with dignity, comfort and convenience" (Nussbaumer 2012: 33)

Allows: People to be independent and participate equally

Like accessible filmmaking: Focuses on diverse audience needs as foundation

Conclusion

Key Takeaways

  • Audience definition: Tantamount to setting up any reception study
  • Chameleonic nature: Audiences change over time, across space, based on scholar's viewpoint and research aims
  • Once stance taken: Decisions needed on type of analysis, methodology, tools, expected results

Media Accessibility Contributions

  • Unquestionable merit of boosting reception studies
  • Brought users of dedicated services into spotlight (captions, AD, audio subtitling)
  • Promoted and enhanced practice of accessibility
  • Key achievement: Turning accessibility into inclusion
  • Gives scholars and practitioners more positive, constructive stance
  • Hopefully a new starting point

Central Message

Understanding audiences - the people behind the texts translated and consumed every day - requires:

  • Recognizing complex development of audience notion
  • Acknowledging quintessentially chameleonic nature
  • Defining audiences before beginning research
  • Choosing appropriate methodologies and tools
  • Embracing interdisciplinary approaches
  • Moving toward inclusive paradigms
