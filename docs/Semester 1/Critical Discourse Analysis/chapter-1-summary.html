1. What is CDA?

Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA): Study of how language is used to persuade, manipulate, and control individuals and social groups. Focuses on language as a controlling force in society and its role in maintaining or changing social structures.
Key Goals of CDA:
  • Develop awareness of language and ideology
  • Understand how discourse controls society
  • Question roles in conventional discourses
  • Work towards social justice
Exam Tip
CDA is also called Critical Linguistics or Critical Discourse Studies (CDS). Know all three terms!

Linguistics Goals:

  • Study how language works
  • Interest in language for its own sake

CDA Goals:

  • How language achieves social goals
  • Role in social maintenance & change

3. CDA Methods

4 Key Methods:
  1. Context Analysis - examine social setting
  2. Observational Techniques - recording & transcribing natural language
  3. Participant Observation - researcher takes role in target community
  4. Use of Informants - experts explain discourse practices

4. Examples of CDA Practice

Area Focus Impact
Advertising Persuasive language, truth distortion Empowers individuals to resist marketing pressure
Forensic Linguistics Legal & police discourse, confessions, cross-examination Establishes justice, fairer practices
Doctor-Patient Medical communication patterns Improves healthcare practices
Family Discourse Aggressive speech styles, bullying Requires team research (psychologists, social workers)

5. Principles of CDA

Core Principles:
  • Self-Critical: Analysts acknowledge their own position and biases (no claim to complete objectivity)
  • Ethical Practice: Proper permission, privacy protection essential
  • Tolerance: Accept varied approaches from multiple sources
  • International: Contributions from many cultures and countries
  • Power-Focused: Central understanding that discourse = power & control

6. Key Terms & Definitions

DISCOURSE (6 meanings - know them all!)

# Meaning
1 Broad: All symbolic interaction (spoken, written, visual)
2 Spoken: Simply spoken interaction (vs. written)
3 vs. Text: Whole act of communication (production + comprehension)
4 Institutional: Communication in specific contexts (legal discourse, science discourse)
5 A discourse: Particular long text (lecture, sermon) - NOT used in this book
6 Multi-modal: Uses more than one mode (words + pictures + music)
Text: Product of discourse. Linguistic record of a communicative event (electronic recording or written). Must meet 7 standards of textuality.
7 Standards of Textuality (Beaugrande & Dressler):
  1. Cohesion - how words connect in sequence
  2. Coherence - cognitive relations, mutual knowledge
  3. Acceptability - appropriateness to cultural setting
  4. Intentionality - producer's purpose/goals
  5. Informativity - suitable degree of information
  6. Situationality - relevance to situation
  7. Intertextuality - reliance on previous texts

Other Essential Terms:

Domain: Socially recognized context (e.g., science, BBC, university). Contains social practices and genres.
Social Practice: Human behaviors following social conventions with some freedom (e.g., business meetings, religious services). Single instance = social event or speech event.
Genre: Specific product of social practice. Culturally recognized discourse form with agreed structures (e.g., novel, poem, lecture, letter, business report).
Speech Community:
  • Share language features
  • Frequently interact
  • Usually accident of birth/location
  • Example: Geordie dialect speakers
Discourse Community:
  • May meet rarely or not at all
  • Conscious participation
  • Chosen membership
  • Example: medical researchers, online chat groups
Speech Acts: Utterances that perform actions in context (promise, instruction, bet, marry). Theory by Austin (1950s). May be interpreted differently than intended.
Participants & Social Roles: People engaged in discourse (speakers, listeners, readers, writers). Each plays multiple roles (e.g., Ahmed = husband + father + architect + umpire).
Ideology: Set of beliefs/attitudes shared by social group. Often unconscious and taken for granted. Can be hidden in single words (e.g., "democracy", "peacekeeping").
Frames: Mental structures that shape how we see the world (Lakoff). Triggered by words. Culture-specific cognitive models (e.g., cricket frame: pitch, ball, bat, umpire, wicket).
Archive: Set of frames handed down in a culture, shared by group members. Facilitates in-group communication but hinders cross-group communication.

7. Language & Institutions

Key Concept: "Making Strange"
Observing familiar customs as an outsider would. Essential skill for critical analysis. Example: Marriage ceremony inequality ("obey and serve" requirement for women only) became denormalized through awareness.
Normalization: When customs become so familiar they seem unremarkable. CDA challenges normalized practices.

Example: Gender-free pronoun "they" (singular) vs. old rule "he embraces female" - changed as social practices changed.

8. What Makes CDA "Critical"?

Non-Critical DA:

  1. Identify how people use language
  2. Develop analysis methods
  3. Build communication theories

Critical DA:

  • Discourse as product of society
  • Dynamic force influencing society
  • Questions texts & processes
  • Commitment to social concerns

Main Objectives of CDA:

Practical Objectives:
  • Analyze discourse practices reflecting/constructing social problems
  • Investigate how ideologies become frozen in language
  • Increase awareness to address injustice, prejudice, power misuse
Theoretical Objectives:
  • Demonstrate language's significance in power relations
  • Investigate how meaning is created in context
  • Investigate role of speaker/writer purpose and authorial stance
Key Researchers & Focus Areas
  • Teun van Dijk: Racism research
  • Norman Fairclough: Global capitalism ("new capitalism")
  • Robert de Beaugrande: Ecological issues
