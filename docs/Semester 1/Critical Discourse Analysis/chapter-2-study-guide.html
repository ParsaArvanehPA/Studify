Session 2: Discourse & Social Context

Critical Discourse Analysis | Chapter 2 - Context and Meaning
Signs, Context, Pragmatics, and Institutional Discourse
"The force and cogency of most language behaviour derives from the firm grip it has on the ever-recurrent typical situations in the life of social groups." — J.R. Firth (1937)

1. Sign, Context, and Meaning

The Fundamental Principle

Meaning is created when a sign occurs in a specific context. The same sign can have completely different meanings depending on the context in which it appears.

Examples of Context-Dependent Meaning:
  • Red flag on a beach: Danger/no swimming
  • Red flag at a demonstration: Political symbol (socialism/communism)
  • Cross (X) on student work: Error/incorrect answer
  • Cross (X) on a ballot: Selection/vote
  • "I love you" from mother: Parental affection
  • "I love you" from romantic partner: Romantic love
Semiosis: The relationship of sign to meaning — the process by which something comes to stand for something else.
Semiotics: The study of signs and sign systems — how meaning is made and understood through signs.

1.1 Performatives

What are Performatives?

A performative is an utterance that IS an act of doing something. The words themselves perform the action simply by being spoken in the appropriate context.

Examples of Performatives:
  • "I now pronounce you husband and wife" (performs the marriage)
  • "I promise to return your book" (performs the promise)
  • "I bet you five dollars" (performs the bet)
  • "I name this ship the Queen Mary" (performs the naming)
  • "I sentence you to five years" (performs the sentencing)
Critical Condition: Performatives ONLY work when the right conditions are met:
  • The speaker must have the authority to perform the act
  • The circumstances must be appropriate
  • The proper procedures must be followed

Example: "I now pronounce you husband and wife" only works if said by an authorized person in a real ceremony — NOT if said by an actor in a play or by someone without legal authority.

1.2 Meaning vs. Sense vs. Reference

Term Definition Example
Meaning Dictionary definition of words (inherent, context-free meaning) "Dog" = four-legged domesticated animal
Sense Full discoursal meaning in context (communicative function) "What a dog!" (insult about a person)
Reference Who or what is actually referred to (real-world entity) "The dog" = that specific animal over there
Meaning Potential All possible meanings available in a language system (SFL concept) All the ways "dog" can be used in English
Understanding the Distinction

Why does this matter for CDA?

Consider the phrase "freedom fighters":

  • Meaning: People who fight for freedom (dictionary definition)
  • Sense: Could be positive (heroes) or ironic (terrorists calling themselves this)
  • Reference: Which specific group is being described?

CDA is particularly interested in sense because the same words with the same meaning can carry very different ideological messages depending on context.

Consider: "Terrorist" vs. "Freedom fighter" vs. "Rebel" vs. "Insurgent" — these might refer to the SAME group, but the sense is completely different!

2. Socially Shared Knowledge (Mutual Knowledge)

What is Mutual Knowledge?

Socially Shared Knowledge (also called Mutual Knowledge) is the knowledge that participants use in any communicative act. Without shared knowledge, communication breaks down.

Seven Types of Mutual Knowledge

  1. Subject matter facts: Knowledge about the topic being discussed
  2. Cultural knowledge: Festivals, customs, legal system, historical events
  3. Social roles & hierarchies: How people are expected to behave in different roles
  4. Institutional practices: Workplace norms, school procedures, genre conventions
  5. Moral values: What the group considers right/wrong, appropriate/inappropriate
  6. Co-text & context: What has already been said in this specific event
  7. Individual participants: Knowledge about the specific people involved
CDA Critical Point: All seven categories incorporate issues of identity and power. The value systems of a social group constitute its ideology. When shared knowledge includes biased assumptions, those biases become "invisible" and seem like "common sense."

2.1 Cultural Assumptions

Case Study: Hidden Assumptions in Advertising

Common cultural assumptions exploited by advertisers:

  • "Looking young is desirable" → anti-aging products
  • "Big is good" → supersized products, growth economics
  • "Natural is better" → "organic" and "natural" labeling
  • "Experts know best" → "9 out of 10 dentists recommend..."
  • "New is improved" → constant product updates

CDA insight: These assumptions are often accepted without question because they're part of shared cultural knowledge. Advertisers and propagandists exploit these to manipulate.

3. Purposeful Communication (Pragmatics)

Pragmatics: The branch of linguistics studying aspects of language and communication that cannot be explained by linguistics alone. Includes: reference, communicative function, presupposition, and implicature.

3.1 Learning Discourse

How We Learn to Communicate

  • We learn to recognize common discourse events
  • We learn unspoken rules of conversation
  • We learn appropriate behavior (varies by social group)
  • We become familiar with genres (some through exposure, others through explicit teaching)
Genres: Text types socially constructed over time to fulfill social functions. They follow agreed social rules and customs learned through education or contact. Examples: academic essay, job application, wedding speech, tweet, news article.

4. Reference, Identity, and Role

Grammatical Reference

Reference is crucial for text cohesion. It allows pronouns to substitute for nouns (she, he, I, they) and pro-forms for other elements (here, there, that, then).

4.1 Self vs. Other

Identity Construction Through Language

  • Self: We identify with our own social groupings
  • Other: We place ourselves in opposition to other groups
  • We choose how closely to identify with different groups
  • We play multiple roles: profession, religion, nation, family, interests

4.2 Pronouns and Identity

Speaker Use of "We" Identity Constructed
E.M. Forster (anti-Nazi speech) "We" = British anti-Nazis ("mongrels") Creates in-group vs. Nazis
Countee Cullen (Black poet) "We" = Oppressed Black Americans Excludes non-Black Americans
James Baldwin "We" = All people (Black & White) resisting racism Broader identity transcending race
CDA Analytical Point: Always ask WHO is included/excluded by pronouns like "we," "us," "they," "them." This reveals identity positioning and power relationships. A politician's use of "we" may include or exclude the audience depending on context.
Practical Analysis Tip

When you encounter "we" in a text, ask:

  1. Inclusive "we": Does it include the reader/listener?
  2. Exclusive "we": Does it exclude the reader/listener?
  3. Royal "we": Does it really mean "I"?
  4. Generic "we": Does it mean "people in general"?

Example Analysis:

"We need to protect our borders from them."

  • "We" = citizens (included)
  • "Our" = national possession
  • "Them" = outsiders, immigrants (excluded, othered)

This simple sentence constructs an us/them divide — a key feature of exclusionary discourse.

5. Grice's Co-operative Principle and Maxims

H.P. Grice (1975)

British philosopher who proposed that conversation works because participants follow an unspoken Co-operative Principle.

The Co-operative Principle (CP)

"Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."

In simple terms: Say what is appropriate for the conversation at hand.

5.1 The Four Maxims

Maxim Simple Rule Detailed Requirements
QUANTITY Say enough, but not too much 1) Be as informative as required
2) Don't be more informative than required
QUALITY Tell the truth 1) Don't say what you believe to be false
2) Don't say what you lack evidence for
RELATION Be relevant Make contributions relevant to the current exchange
MANNER Be clear 1) Avoid obscurity
2) Avoid ambiguity
3) Be brief
4) Be orderly
Memory Aid: "Queen Makes Really Quiet" = Quality, Manner, Relation, Quantity

5.2 Implicature

Implicature: Meaning that is evident although not explicitly stated — "reading between the lines." Implicature relies on the Co-operative Principle to work.
Example of Implicature:

A: "Is John a good student?"

B: "He attends class regularly."

Implicature: B didn't say John is a good student. By only mentioning attendance, B implies that's all they can positively say — suggesting John is NOT particularly good academically.

5.3 Violating vs. Flouting Maxims

Violating a Maxim Flouting a Maxim
Deliberately breaking the maxim Blatantly and obviously failing to fulfill it
Intent: to deceive or mislead Intent: to trigger implicature
The hearer doesn't notice the breach The hearer notices and draws conclusions
Example: Lying Example: Irony, sarcasm, exaggeration

Case Study: Flouting for Irony

Context: A book review of a young footballer's autobiography

Reviewer's statement: "I would equate it with the first folio of Shakespeare."

Analysis: This flouts the Quality maxim — the reviewer cannot possibly believe this is true. Readers recognize this as blatant overstatement and understand it as sarcasm/mockery.

Clash of Maxims

Sometimes two maxims come into conflict, and we must choose which to follow.

Example:

A: "Where exactly is Tallinn?"

B: "In one of the Baltic States."

Analysis: B fails the Quantity maxim (not informative enough) in order to observe Quality maxim (doesn't claim to know exactly which Baltic state).

Maxims in CDA Analysis

Why are Grice's maxims important for CDA?

Politicians, advertisers, and propagandists regularly violate maxims while pretending to observe them:

Quality violations:

  • "The economy has never been stronger" (while data shows recession)
  • Statistics taken out of context

Quantity violations:

  • Giving too little information to hide problems
  • Overwhelming with irrelevant detail to obscure key facts

Relation violations:

  • Answering a different question than asked ("pivoting")
  • Introducing unrelated topics to distract

Manner violations:

  • Using jargon to confuse
  • Being deliberately vague

CDA Task: Identify when these violations occur and whose interests they serve.

6. Analysis of Context

6.1 Two Levels of Context

Context of Culture Context of Situation
Traditions of the community Setting (physical/social environment)
Institutions Identity of participants
Discourse communities Direct production of meanings
Historical context Specific instance of communication
Knowledge base of society Power relationships present
Under continuous long-term change Changes with each contribution
Critical Insight: Context is dynamic, not static. Language use is PART OF context (this is circular but unavoidable). Language and context are inextricably bound in meaning production — each shapes the other.

6.2 Practical Context Analysis Checklist

What the Analyst Should Identify:

  1. Setting: Place(s) where the event occurs
  2. Time: When the event occurs and its temporal aspects
  3. Mode & Medium: Face-to-face, TV, written, illustrated, digital, etc.
  4. Participants: Who is involved and what roles they play
  5. Topic/s: What is being discussed, distance from topics
  6. Purpose: Goals of the discourse event and participants
  7. Attitude: Participants' orientations toward subject and each other
  8. Dynamics: How events, participants, and topics change during discourse
  9. Genre: What type of text/discourse is this?

6.3 Levels of Delicacy

Example: Topic Specification

Topic can move from general to specific depending on analytical purpose:

Engineering → Electrical engineering → Electrical supply systems → Electrical circuits → Contact breakers → Danger factors in obsolete contact breakers

Which level is relevant depends on the analyst's purpose and the text being analyzed.

6.4 Phatic Communion

Phatic Communion: Greetings and expressions that establish contact or solidarity, rather than conveying propositional content about the world.
Examples of Phatic Expressions:
  • "Hello!" / "Hi there!"
  • "How are you?" (not expecting detailed answer)
  • "Nice weather, isn't it?"
  • "See you later!"
  • "Take care!"
  • "Cheers!"

Function: These maintain social bonds and "oil the wheels" of interaction rather than transmit information.

7. Institutional Constraints

Institutional Discourse

Institutions impose constraints on discourse practices. They create specific text types with stated purposes and pre-formatted structures.

Examples of Institutional Discourse Constraints

Institution Discourse Constraints
Hotels Training in specific "scripts," "house style" for guest interaction
Legal System Court procedures, contract formats, will templates, cross-examination rules
Business Meeting protocols, report formats, email conventions
Education Lecture formats, essay structures, grading language
Healthcare Consultation scripts, medical record formats, consent procedures
CDA Critical Point: Institutional discourses are crucial for CDA because the power structure of a nation or group is enshrined here. The context of discourse is not just reflected by but is CREATED BY discourse itself.

7.1 Core vs. Fringe Genres

Core Genres Fringe Genres
Central institutional genres Draw on and adapt central genres
Highly regulated, formal More flexible adaptations
Court procedures, legal documents Newspaper court reports, insurance contracts
Academic journal articles Popular science writing
Chapter Summary: From Context to Power

What have we learned in this chapter?

1. Meaning requires context

  • Signs don't mean anything in isolation
  • The same words mean different things in different contexts
  • Performatives only work with the right conditions

2. Shared knowledge enables communication

  • Seven types of mutual knowledge underpin understanding
  • Cultural assumptions can be exploited by propagandists
  • Ideology is embedded in what we take for granted

3. Pronouns construct identity

  • "We" and "they" create in-groups and out-groups
  • Identity positioning is a form of power
  • The same speaker can shift identity through pronouns

4. The Co-operative Principle reveals hidden meaning

  • We expect others to be informative, truthful, relevant, and clear
  • Violations deceive; flouting creates implicature
  • Politicians often violate maxims strategically

5. Institutions shape discourse

  • Institutional constraints regulate what can be said and how
  • Power structures are encoded in institutional genres
  • Context is not just setting — it's created by discourse itself

Key insight: Context is never innocent. Who controls the context controls the meaning. CDA examines how powerful groups shape contexts to their advantage.

8. Key Theorists and Contributions

J.R. Firth (1937)

Contribution: Context of situation — emphasized that meaning depends on setting, participants, and behavior in context.

H.P. Grice (1975)

Contribution: Co-operative Principle and four conversational maxims — explained how implicit meaning works.

J.L. Austin (1950s)

Contribution: Speech act theory and performatives — showed that saying can be doing.

M.A.K. Halliday (SFL)

Contribution: Meaning potential and systematic attention to social context in linguistic analysis.

9. Summary: Key Points to Remember

Essential Takeaways from Chapter 2:
  • Meaning is created by signs in context — same sign, different context = different meaning
  • Performatives are utterances that DO things (but need proper conditions)
  • Distinguish: Meaning (dictionary) vs. Sense (contextual) vs. Reference (real-world)
  • Seven types of mutual knowledge enable communication
  • Pronouns (we/they) construct identity and us/them divides
  • Grice's Co-operative Principle: Quantity, Quality, Relation, Manner
  • Violating maxims = deception; Flouting maxims = implicature (irony, sarcasm)
  • Context of Culture vs. Context of Situation
  • Context is dynamic — language shapes context as context shapes language
  • Institutional discourse encodes power structures
  • Phatic communion maintains social bonds without conveying information
