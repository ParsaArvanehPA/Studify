üìç Chapter 3: Positioning and Point of View

üéØ EXAM FOCUS: Understand how stance can be conscious (explicit/hidden) or unconscious. Know how to identify authorial positioning in different text types (reviews, promotional texts, racist/sexist discourse, history).

1. Position & Attitude (Stance)

Stance: The authorial position taken in text, reflects attitude toward subject matter and audience. Can be conscious or unconscious.
Type of Stance Description Text Examples
Conscious Stance (Explicit) Deliberate positioning, openly revealed to reader Film/book reviews, critical essays, opinion pieces
Conscious Stance (Hidden) Deliberate positioning, concealed from reader Promotional letters, advertisements, persuasive texts
Unconscious Stance Unaware positioning, reveals ideology/worldview Racist/sexist discourse, biased reporting, textbooks

2. Film and Book Reviews (Conscious - Explicit)

Key Feature: Reviewers openly express subjective opinions using evaluative language and first-person pronouns.

Linguistic Features of Reviews:

Feature Purpose Examples
First-person pronouns Show personal involvement "I found...", "We believe..."
Evaluative adjectives Express judgment Brilliant, tedious, compelling, disappointing
Modality Hedge or strengthen claims "Should see", "might enjoy", "must watch"
Direct address Engage reader "You will love...", "Don't miss..."
Presupposition Assume shared values "As we all know...", "Obviously..."
Example Analysis: "This film is undoubtedly one of the worst I've seen this year" ‚Üí First-person + intensifier + evaluative adjective = explicit negative stance

3. Promotional Letters (Conscious - Hidden)

Key Feature: Writers deliberately hide persuasive intent while positioning product/service favorably. They manufacture intimacy and trust.

Deceptive Strategies:

  • Simulated Friendship: Use of first names, informal tone
  • Synthetic Personalization: "Dear [Name]" though mass-produced
  • Pseudo-Intimacy: "We know you..." / "You deserve..."
  • False Scarcity: "Limited time offer" / "Only for you"
  • Authority Claims: "Experts recommend..." / "Studies show..."
  • Flattery: "As a valued customer..." / "Sophisticated buyers like you..."
Technique Linguistic Marker Hidden Purpose
Inclusive "we" "We're offering you..." Create false solidarity
Second-person focus "You'll love...", "Your needs..." Simulate personal attention
Positive presupposition "When you order..." (not "if") Assume compliance
Imperative softening "Why not try...", "Just send..." Disguise commands as suggestions
üéØ EXAM TIP: For promotional text analysis, identify how the writer creates false intimacy while concealing commercial intent.

4. Racist and Sexist Discourse (Unconscious)

Unconscious Stance: Writers unaware they're expressing discriminatory worldview. Reveals naturalized ideology through language choices.

Racist Discourse Markers:

Feature Function Example
Othering pronouns Create us/them divide "They are taking our jobs"
Nominalization Obscure agency "Immigration problem" (who says it's a problem?)
Generalizations Stereotype groups "Those people always..."
Metaphors Dehumanize "Flood of refugees", "swarm of migrants"
Presupposition Naturalize bias "Even the good ones..." (presumes most are bad)

Sexist Discourse Markers:

  • Generic Masculine: Using "he/him" as universal (excludes women)
  • Asymmetric Address: "Mr. Smith and Janet" (unequal naming)
  • Semantic Derogation: Negative connotations for female terms
  • Role Stereotyping: "Female doctor", "male nurse" (marked forms)
  • Trivialization: Diminutives (lady doctor, career girl)
  • Appearance Focus: Describing women's looks when irrelevant
Example: "The politician and his attractive wife arrived" ‚Üí Sexist: man defined by role, woman by appearance + possessive "his wife"

5. History Textbooks (Mixed Conscious/Unconscious)

Key Issue: Textbooks claim objectivity but contain subtle positioning through lexical choices, voice, and narrative structure.

How Stance Appears in History Texts:

Device Effect Example
Passive Voice Hide agency, obscure responsibility "Mistakes were made" (by whom?)
Nominalization Turn actions into things "The colonization of..." (obscures colonizers)
Lexical Choice Frame events ideologically "Settlers" vs. "invaders", "discovery" vs. "invasion"
Modality Present opinion as fact "It is clear that...", "Obviously..."
Omission Silence alternative perspectives Ignoring indigenous viewpoints
Compare these framings:
‚úì Neutral: "British forces arrived in Australia in 1788"
‚úó Biased: "Australia was discovered by Britain in 1788" (ignores indigenous presence, frames as empty land)

6. Analyzing Authorial Stance - Checklist

  • Pronoun Use: Who is "we"? Who is "they"? Inclusive or exclusive?
  • Modality: Certainty markers (must, will, should, might, could)
  • Evaluative Language: Adjectives, adverbs expressing judgment
  • Presuppositions: What is assumed as shared knowledge?
  • Voice: Active (shows agency) vs. Passive (hides agency)
  • Lexical Choices: Word selection reveals ideology (terrorist/freedom fighter)
  • Metaphors: Conceptual frameworks used (war, journey, disease)
  • What's Absent: Whose voices are silenced? What's not said?
  • Genre Expectations: How does text conform to/deviate from norms?

7. Key Theorists & Concepts

Theorist/Concept Key Idea
Van Dijk Racist discourse analysis; ideological square (positive self-presentation, negative other-presentation)
Fairclough Power relations in language; naturalization of ideology
Lakoff Conceptual metaphor theory; how metaphors reveal worldview
Synthetic Personalization Fairclough's term for mass communication disguised as personal
Ideological Square Van Dijk: Emphasize our good, emphasize their bad, mitigate our bad, mitigate their good

üìã Quick Reference Summary

Stance Types (Memorize This!):

  1. Conscious-Explicit: Reviews ‚Üí "I think this film is brilliant"
  2. Conscious-Hidden: Ads ‚Üí "You deserve this luxury" (hiding sales motive)
  3. Unconscious: Bias ‚Üí "They are flooding our country" (unaware racism)

Analysis Framework:

  1. Identify text type and expected stance
  2. Examine pronouns (who is included/excluded?)
  3. Find evaluative language (judgment words)
  4. Check voice (active reveals, passive conceals)
  5. Note lexical choices (loaded words)
  6. Identify presuppositions (what's assumed?)
  7. Consider omissions (what's silenced?)
üéØ COMMON EXAM QUESTIONS:
  • Analyze the authorial stance in [text extract]
  • How does the writer position the reader?
  • Identify examples of conscious/unconscious positioning
  • What ideological assumptions underlie this text?
  • Compare stance in two different text types
  • How is bias revealed through linguistic choices?

üéì Exam Strategy Tips

  • Always identify whether stance is conscious or unconscious first
  • Quote specific linguistic evidence (pronouns, modality, lexis)
  • Link language features to ideological effect
  • Consider who benefits from this positioning
  • Note the gap between claimed objectivity and actual bias
  • Reference theorists (Van Dijk, Fairclough) when relevant
