1. Sign, Context & Meaning

Meaning Creation: Meaning is created when a sign occurs in a specific context. Same sign = different meanings in different contexts.
Examples:
  • Red flag on beach (danger) vs. red flag at demonstration (political symbol)
  • Cross (X) on student work (error) vs. ballot (selection)
  • "I love you" from mother vs. from romantic partner
Semiosis: Relationship of sign to meaning
Semiotics: Study of signs

Performatives

Performative: Utterance that IS an act of doing something. Words perform the action simply by being spoken.
Exam Tip
Performatives ONLY work in the right context with right conditions met. Example: "I now pronounce you husband and wife" only works if said by authorized person in real ceremony, NOT in a play.

Meaning vs. Sense vs. Reference

Term Definition
Meaning Dictionary definition of words (inherent meaning)
Sense Full discoursal meaning in context (communicative function)
Reference Who/what is referred to (actual people, events, objects)
Meaning Potential Lexis + grammar + sound systems available to make meaning (SFL concept)
Key Point
Language can properly construct meaning ONLY when part of wider social event. Context is needed AND helps create context simultaneously.

2. Language in Use - Socially Shared Knowledge

Socially Shared Knowledge (Mutual Knowledge): Knowledge used by participants in communicative act. Essential for successful communication.
7 Types of Mutual Knowledge:
  1. Subject matter facts
  2. Cultural knowledge (festivals, customs, legal system)
  3. Social roles & hierarchies (how people behave in roles)
  4. Institutional practices (workplace, schools, genres)
  5. Moral values of groups
  6. Co-text & context of specific event
  7. Individual participants in immediate event
Critical Point
All 7 categories incorporate issues of identity and power. Value systems of social group = ideology.

Cultural Assumptions

Examples of Cultural Assumptions:
  • "Looking young is desirable" (used in advertising)
  • "Big is good" (growing economy, increasing markets)
  • Can be exploited by propagandists & advertisers
  • Often accepted without question at particular time/culture

3. Purposeful Communication (Pragmatics)

Pragmatics: Branch studying aspects of language/communication NOT explained by linguistics. Includes reference, communicative function, presupposition, implicature.

Purpose & Context

Learning Discourse:
  • We learn to recognize common discourse events
  • We learn unspoken rules of conversation
  • We learn appropriate behavior (varies by social group)
  • We become familiar with genres (some learned through exposure, others taught)
Genres: Text types socially constructed over time to fulfill social functions. Follow agreed social rules/customs learned through education or contact.

4. Reference, Identity & Role

Reference (grammatical): Important for text cohesion. Allows pronouns to substitute for nouns (she, he, I, they) and pro-forms for other parts (here, there, that).

Self vs. Other

Identity Construction:
  • Self: Identify with own social groupings
  • Other: Place ourselves in opposition to other groups
  • We choose how far to identify with groups
  • Multiple roles: profession, religion, nation, clubs, etc.

Analysis: Pronouns & Identity

Example "We" Referent Identity
E.M. Forster (anti-Nazi) British anti-Nazis ("mongrels") Includes self + listeners vs. Nazis
Contee Cullen (Black poet) Oppressed Black Americans Excludes non-Black Americans
James Baldwin All people (Black & White) resisting racism Broader identity beyond race
Exam Tip
Pay attention to WHO is included/excluded by pronouns "we," "us," "they," "them." This reveals identity positioning and power relationships.

5. Grice's Co-operative Principle & Maxims

Co-operative Principle (CP): "Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose of the talk exchange in which you are engaged."
Grice's 4 Maxims (Simplified):
Maxim Simple Version Detailed Version
Quantity Say as much as needed 1) Be as informative as required
2) Don't be more informative than required
Quality Tell the truth 1) Don't say what you believe false
2) Don't say what lacks evidence
Relation Be relevant Make contributions relevant to current exchange
Manner Be clear 1) Avoid obscurity
2) Avoid ambiguity
3) Be brief
4) Be orderly

Key Concepts

Implicature: Meaning evident although not explicitly stated ("reading between the lines"). Relies on CP to work.
Violating Maxim:
  • Deliberately breaking maxim
  • To deceive or mislead
  • Success depends on hearer assuming CP
Flouting Maxim:
  • Blatantly failing to fulfill it
  • To trigger implicature
  • Includes irony & sarcasm
Clash of Maxims: Two maxims in conflict. Example: "Where is Tallinn?" â†’ "In one of the Baltic States" (failing Quantity to observe Quality - doesn't know exactly).
Exam Example
Irony/Sarcasm (flouting Quality): Young footballer's autobiography â†’ "I would equate it with the first folio of Shakespeare." Readers know this CAN'T be true, so understand it as mockery.

6. Analysis of Context

Models of Context

Context of Culture

  • Traditions
  • Institutions
  • Discourse communities
  • Historical context
  • Knowledge base
  • Under continuous change

Context of Situation

  • Setting (social environment)
  • Identity of participants
  • Direct production of meanings
  • Specific instance
  • Power relationships
  • Changes with each contribution
Critical Point
Context is dynamic. Language use is PART OF context (circular but unavoidable). Language and context are inextricably bound in meaning production.

Practical Approach - Analyst Should Identify:

Context Analysis Checklist:
  • Setting (place/s) of event
  • Time & aspect of event
  • Mode & medium (face-to-face, TV, written, illustrated, etc.)
  • Participants & their roles
  • Topic/s, themes (distance from topics)
  • Purpose of discourse event & participants
  • Attitude of participants
  • Dynamics (how events, participants, topics change during discourse)
  • Genre (where applicable)

Levels of Delicacy

Which level is relevant depends on analyst's purpose.

Phatic Communion

Phatic Communion: Greetings/expressions that establish contact or solidarity, NOT propositions about world. Examples: Hello!, Cheers!, Love you, See you later, Ups a daisy!

7. Institutional Constraints

Institutional Discourse: Institutions impose constraints on practices. Create specific text types with stated purposes & pre-formatted structures.
Examples:
  • Hotels: Training in specific "scripts," "house style"
  • Legal: Court procedures, contracts, wills, cross-examination formats
  • Business: Reservation forms, check-in/out procedures, complaint handling
  • Government, Education, Police, Army: All have established discourse genres
Critical Point
Institutional discourses are important to CDA because power structure of nation/group is enshrined here. Context of discourse is CREATED BY discourse.

Core vs. Fringe Genres

Core Genres: Central institutional genres (court procedures, legal documents)
Fringe Genres: Draw on central genres (newspaper court reports, insurance contracts)
